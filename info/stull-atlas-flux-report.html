The Chemistry of Melting:[6pt] What Fluxes Do, How the Atlas Analyses Them, and What More It Should Do

Abstract

In ceramic glaze chemistry, fluxes are the oxides that break apart the glass network, lower the melting temperature, and control nearly every property a potter cares about: whether a glaze flows or sits, crazes or compresses, leaches or resists, shines or goes matte. Yet fluxes are overwhelmingly discussed as a single lump—“total flux”—when the identity, ratio, and diversity of individual flux species matter enormously. This report provides a deep examination of flux chemistry as it exists in the Stull Atlas today (7 dedicated Z-axes plus flux-dependent properties throughout the 133-axis system), then maps out what a full flux analysis toolkit should include: interactive ratio controls, substitution path modelling, flux fingerprinting, eutectic mapping, and flux-aware optimisation. The intended audience is the developer extending the atlas, and the technically minded potter or glaze technician seeking a quantitative understanding of how fluxes shape fired results.

What Is a Flux?

The Network Model of Glass

To understand fluxes, one must first understand the structure they destroy.

A silicate glass is, at the atomic scale, a three-dimensional network of corner-sharing SiO2 tetrahedra. Each silicon atom sits at the centre of four oxygen atoms arranged in a tetrahedron. Each oxygen is shared between two adjacent tetrahedra, forming a continuous, aperiodic framework—Zachariasen's “random network” (1932). This framework is enormously strong: pure silica glass (SiO2) melts above 1720 °C and would be useless for pottery.

Fluxes are the oxides that break this network. When a flux oxide like Na2O dissolves into the silica melt, each Na+ ion positions itself near a bridging oxygen (an oxygen shared between two tetrahedra) and severs the Si–O–Si bond, converting the bridging oxygen into a non-bridging oxygen (NBO) bonded to only one silicon. The result is a weaker, more compliant, lower-melting glass.

⚡ The Fundamental Reaction
The introduction of a flux oxide into the silica network can be written schematically:
\[\equiv\text{Si--O--Si}\equiv \;+\; \text{Na}_2\text{O} \;\longrightarrow\; 2 \times (\equiv\text{Si--O}^- \;\text{Na}^+)\]
Each mole of Na2O breaks one bridging oxygen and creates two non-bridging oxygens. The NBO/T ratio (non-bridging oxygens per tetrahedron) quantifies the cumulative damage to the network. A fully connected network has $\text{NBO/T} = 0$ (pure SiO2); a fully depolymerised melt has $\text{NBO/T} = 4$ (isolated SiO2 tetrahedra floating in a sea of modifier cations).

The UMF convention normalises total flux to 1.0 mole: $\sum \text{R}_2\text{O} + \sum \text{RO} = 1.0$. This means every UMF recipe encodes a flux distribution—the relative proportions of the 10 flux species—as a compositional vector that always sums to unity. This is the foundation of flux ratio analysis.

Why Flux Identity Matters

It is tempting to treat all fluxes as interchangeable “network breakers.” They are not. Each flux ion has a different:

  1. Size (ionic radius): Li+ = 0.76 Å, Na+ = 1.02 Å, K+ = 1.38 Å, Ba2+ = 1.35 Å. Larger ions disrupt the network differently and occupy different interstitial volumes.
  2. Charge density (field strength): Ca2+ ($F = 0.33$) exerts twice the electrostatic pull on oxygen as Na+ ($F = 0.19$), even though both create one NBO per cation equivalent. This affects viscosity, durability, and crystallisation.
  3. Thermal expansion contribution: Appen expansion factors span a 100× range: $\alpha_{\text{Na}_2\text{O}} = 39.5$, $\alpha_{\text{K}_2\text{O}} = 28.3$, $\alpha_{\text{CaO}} = 13.0$, $\alpha_{\text{MgO}} = 0.45$, $\alpha_{\text{Li}_2\text{O}} = 10.0$ (all $\times 10^{-7}\;/{}^\circ\text{C}$). A glaze with 0.5 moles Na2O will craze on stoneware; a glaze with 0.5 moles MgO will not.
  4. Melting power: Eppler & Eppler (2000) assigned fluxing effectiveness values normalised to Na2O = 1.0: Li2O $\approx 5.0$, PbO $\approx 2.0$, CaO $\approx 1.0$, MgO $\approx 0.4$, Al2O3 $\approx -1.0$ (anti-flux). A mole of lithia generates five times the melting power of a mole of soda.
  5. Surface effects: MgO promotes matte surfaces via microcrystallisation (diopside, forsterite). BaO produces classic satin textures. ZnO promotes willemite crystal growth. CaO at high levels drives wollastonite crystallisation. PbO produces the distinctive brilliance and depth of historical low-fire glazes.
  6. Toxicity and safety: PbO and BaO are toxic if leachable. Li2O is expensive and supply-constrained (lithium mining). These practical constraints shape which flux combinations are viable.

The Flux Oxides — Individual Profiles

Each flux oxide has a distinct personality. This section profiles them individually, covering their glass-science behaviour, practical effects, typical UMF ranges, and interactions with other oxides.

Na2O — Soda

ℹ️ Soda at a Glance
Cation: Na+Valence: $+1$
Ionic Radius: 1.02 ÅField Strength: 0.19
Appen COE Factor: 39.5 ($\times 10^{-7}\;/{}^\circ\text{C}$)Eppler Effectiveness: 1.0 (reference)
Common Sources: Soda feldspar (albite), nepheline syenite, soda ash, Gerstley borate, frits
Typical UMF Range: 0.05–0.40Danger Threshold: $\gt 0.35$ (craze risk)

Soda is the most common alkali flux in functional stoneware. It is cheap, abundant (every soda feldspar delivers it), and effective at lowering the melting point. However, it comes with the highest thermal expansion contribution of any common oxide, making it the number-one cause of glaze crazing.

Structural role: Na+ ions are moderately sized and settle into the interstices of the silica network, breaking bridging oxygens efficiently. Each mole of Na2O introduces 2 Na+ ions and converts one BO to two NBOs. The low field strength means Na+ bonds weakly to its surrounding oxygens, making the glass relatively fragile and ionic conduction easy (Na+ is the primary charge carrier in most silicate glasses).

Key interactions:

  • With K2O (Mixed Alkali Effect): Partially replacing soda with potash suppresses thermal expansion below the linear average and dramatically reduces ionic conductivity. The MAE ($= 4 x_{\text{Na}} x_{\text{K}}$) peaks at 50:50 ratio. This is one of the most powerful tools for managing crazing.
  • With CaO: Calcia provides network-tightening through Ca2+'s higher field strength, partially compensating for soda's network-loosening. The Na2O–CaO–SiO2 ternary is the basis of window glass (soda-lime glass), the most produced glass in human history.
  • With SiO2: More silica = lower expansion, but also higher viscosity. The classic craze fix is “add silica, reduce soda.”

Atlas properties most affected: COE (α), glaze stress (σ), VFT viscosity (lower $B$, lower viscosity), ionic conductivity (highest), mixed alkali effect, food-safety leach risk (alkali glasses leach more easily).

K2O — Potash

ℹ️ Potash at a Glance
Cation: K+Valence: $+1$
Ionic Radius: 1.38 ÅField Strength: 0.13
Appen COE Factor: 28.3Eppler Effectiveness: 0.9
Common Sources: Potash feldspar (orthoclase), Cornwall stone, wood ash
Typical UMF Range: 0.05–0.30

Potash behaves similarly to soda but with important differences. The K+ ion is 35% larger than Na+, occupying more interstitial volume and creating a slightly different local distortion of the network. This produces:

  • Lower expansion: $\alpha_{\text{K}_2\text{O}} = 28.3$ vs. 39.5 for soda. Substituting potash for soda directly reduces craze risk.
  • Higher viscosity: K+'s larger size impedes its mobility and increases the effective viscosity of the melt. Potash-heavy glazes are “stiffer” and less prone to running.
  • Warmer colour response: Potash feldspars tend to produce warmer, slightly more amber surfaces than soda feldspars with iron-bearing clays.
  • Slower melting: Slightly lower fluxing effectiveness means potash recipes need marginally higher temperatures or longer soaks.

Strategic use: Potash is the “safety soda”—wherever a recipe has too much Na2O (causing crazing or running), partial substitution with K2O is the first fix to try. The mixed alkali effect amplifies the benefit.

Li2O — Lithia

ℹ️ Lithia at a Glance
Cation: Li+Valence: $+1$
Ionic Radius: 0.76 ÅField Strength: 0.23
Appen COE Factor: 10.0Eppler Effectiveness: ∼5.0
Common Sources: Spodumene, petalite, lithium carbonate, lepidolite
Typical UMF Range: 0.00–0.15

Lithia is the outlier of the alkali family. Despite being monovalent like Na+ and K+, Li+ is tiny—smaller than Mg2+. This anomalous size produces remarkable properties:

  • Extreme fluxing power: Mole-for-mole, lithia is roughly 5× as effective as soda at lowering the melting temperature. This makes it invaluable for pushing cone temperatures down without adding excessive flux mass.
  • Low expansion: $\alpha_{\text{Li}_2\text{O}} = 10.0$—one-quarter of soda's contribution. Li+'s small radius means it fits tightly into the glass network interstices without large vibrational amplitude, so it does not expand the glass much on heating.
  • High field strength for an alkali: At 0.23, lithia approaches the field strength of Ca2+. This gives it some “earth-like” character—tightening the network more than other alkalis.
  • Cost and supply: Lithium minerals are expensive and increasingly demanded by the battery industry, making lithia an expensive flux relative to soda or potash.

The lithia trick: Replacing 0.1 moles of Na2O with 0.02 moles of Li2O provides equivalent fluxing power and drops COE by ∼3 units, often converting a crazing glaze into a compressive one. This substitution is one of the most powerful single moves in glaze development.

CaO — Calcia

ℹ️ Calcia at a Glance
Cation: Ca2+Valence: $+2$
Ionic Radius: 1.00 ÅField Strength: 0.33
Appen COE Factor: 13.0Eppler Effectiveness: 1.0
Common Sources: Whiting (CaCO3), wollastonite, dolomite, wood ash, bone ash
Typical UMF Range: 0.20–0.70

Calcia is the workhorse flux of stoneware ceramics. In functional glazes from cone 4 to cone 10, calcia typically accounts for 30–70% of total flux. Its popularity is earned through a combination of moderate expansion, strong fluxing, excellent durability, and low cost (whiting is essentially crushed limestone).

Structural role: Ca2+'s double charge and moderate radius give it the highest field strength of the common alkaline earths ($F = 0.33$). Unlike monovalent alkalis, each Ca2+ can coordinate with two NBOs, cross-linking separated chain segments and partially re-stiffening the network. This “tightening” effect is why calcia-rich glazes are more durable and less prone to leaching than alkali-rich glazes.

Crystallisation: Above ∼0.50 in UMF, calcia increasingly drives wollastonite crystallisation (CaSiO3) on cooling. This can be desirable (satin/matte surfaces from devitrification) or undesirable (cloudiness in intended-gloss glazes), depending on the potter's intent.

Phase diagram: Calcia is one vertex of the CaO–Al2O3–SiO2 ternary, the most important phase diagram in ceramics (Muan & Osborn, 1965). The atlas uses this diagram for liquidus field assignment and eutectic distance computation.

Key interactions:

  • With Na2O: The soda-lime system achieves deep eutectics, enabling low melting temperatures with good durability.
  • With MgO: Dolomite delivers both simultaneously. The Ca:Mg ratio controls the matte/gloss transition.
  • With ZnO: In moderate-Ca, moderate-Zn systems, willemite (Zn2SiO4) competes with wollastonite for crystal nucleation, producing complex mixed-crystal surfaces.

MgO — Magnesia

ℹ️ Magnesia at a Glance
Cation: Mg2+Valence: $+2$
Ionic Radius: 0.72 ÅField Strength: 0.45
Appen COE Factor: 0.45Eppler Effectiveness: 0.4
Common Sources: Talc, dolomite, magnesium carbonate
Typical UMF Range: 0.00–0.35

Magnesia is the anti-craze flux. With an Appen expansion factor of just 0.45—nearly zero—it can be added in substantial quantities without raising COE. This makes it the go-to flux for potters fighting crazing.

Anomalous behaviour: Mg2+'s field strength (0.45) is above Dietzel's modifier/intermediate boundary of 0.4. This means magnesia behaves partly as a network intermediate: some Mg2+ ions enter tetrahedral coordination at high temperatures, temporarily acting as network formers rather than modifiers. This dual personality explains both its low expansion (network-forming tendency reduces thermal vibration amplitude) and its matte-promoting effect (tetrahedral Mg creates compositional inhomogeneities that seed crystallisation on cooling).

The matte mechanism: At levels above ∼0.20 in UMF, magnesia promotes microcrystallisation of diopside (CaMgSi2O6), forsterite (Mg2SiO4), or enstatite (MgSiO3) on cooling, producing the characteristic buttery matte texture beloved of studio potters. This is not a surface phenomenon—it is bulk devitrification of the glaze into a glass-ceramic composite.

Downsides: Low fluxing effectiveness (0.4× soda) means high-MgO recipes need strong co-fluxes to melt. Excessive magnesia (>0.35) can produce pinholed, dry, unhealed surfaces.

ZnO — Zinc Oxide

ℹ️ Zinc Oxide at a Glance
Cation: Zn2+Valence: $+2$
Ionic Radius: 0.74 ÅField Strength: 0.43
Appen COE Factor: 7.0Eppler Effectiveness: 0.7
Common Sources: Zinc oxide (industrial grade)
Typical UMF Range: 0.00–0.25

Zinc is the crystal-glaze artist's best friend. At moderate levels (0.05–0.15) it acts as a useful flux with low expansion. At higher levels (>0.20), it promotes the spectacular growth of willemite crystals (Zn2SiO4)—the hexagonal prisms and needles that are the hallmark of crystal glazes.

Like magnesia, zinc sits at the modifier/intermediate boundary. Some Zn2+ enters tetrahedral coordination, contributing to network formation. This makes zinc a versatile “swing” flux: it can be a gentle network modifier in small amounts or a crystal nucleator in larger amounts.

Colour interactions: Zinc strongly affects chromium coloring. In zinc-free glazes, Cr2O3 produces green. In zinc-bearing glazes, zinc-chromium brown (ZnCr2O4 spinel) forms, producing brown instead of green. This interaction is notorious for causing unexpected colour shifts when zinc is added.

BaO — Baria

ℹ️ Baria at a Glance
Cation: Ba2+Valence: $+2$
Ionic Radius: 1.35 ÅField Strength: 0.24
Appen COE Factor: 17.0Eppler Effectiveness: 0.6
Common Sources: Barium carbonate
Typical UMF Range: 0.00–0.25Safety: Toxic if raw or leachable

Barium is the satin flux. It produces the distinctive cool, smooth, semi-matte surfaces that are impossible to achieve with other flux combinations. The mechanism is not fully understood but is believed to involve Ba2+'s exceptionally large ionic radius creating local structural heterogeneity that scatters light without forming discrete crystals.

Safety: Soluble barium compounds are acutely toxic. In properly fired glazes, barium is locked into the glass matrix and does not leach. However, under-fired barium glazes or those with poor durability may release barium under acidic conditions. Barium glazes should always be tested for leach resistance before use on food-contact surfaces. The atlas flags barium content as a safety concern via the food-safety and leach-risk axes.

SrO — Strontia

ℹ️ Strontia at a Glance
Cation: Sr2+Valence: $+2$
Ionic Radius: 1.18 ÅField Strength: 0.28
Appen COE Factor: 11.0Eppler Effectiveness: 0.7
Common Sources: Strontium carbonate
Typical UMF Range: 0.00–0.25

Strontia is the “safe baria”—it produces similar satin surfaces to BaO without the toxicity concerns. Strontium compounds are non-toxic and strontium glazes do not pose leach-safety issues. Its ionic radius (1.18 Å) is intermediate between calcium (1.00 Å) and barium (1.35 Å), and its glass-forming behaviour similarly sits between the two.

Practical use: Strontia is increasingly popular as a 1:1 molar replacement for baria in educational and food-safe studio settings. The substitution is not exact (expansion and surface texture differ slightly), but it captures most of the aesthetic character without safety risk.

PbO — Lead Oxide

⚠️ Lead Oxide — Toxic
Lead oxide is the historical low-fire flux that dominated ceramic technology for millennia. It produces unmatched brilliance, depth, and color quality at low temperatures (cone 06 to cone 02). However, lead is acutely toxic and chronically poisonous. Lead glazes on food-contact surfaces are banned or heavily regulated in most countries. Lead-free alternatives (frits, boron, lithia) have replaced lead in virtually all modern functional pottery.

The Stull Atlas includes lead in its oxide system for historical completeness and for analysis of antique ceramics, but its use in new functional ware is strongly discouraged.

Cation: Pb2+Valence: $+2$
Ionic Radius: 1.19 ÅField Strength: 0.27
Appen COE Factor: 13.0Eppler Effectiveness: 2.0
Common Sources: Red lead, litharge, lead bisilicate frit
Typical UMF Range: 0.00–0.60 (historical)

Lead's exceptional fluxing power ($2.0 \times$ soda) and its unique optical properties (high refractive index, low dispersion) made it the dominant flux for fine earthenware, majolica, and decorative ceramics from ancient Rome through the 18th century. Lead glazes produced the brilliant colors of Italian Renaissance majolica, Chinese Tang dynasty sancai ware, and English slipware.

Why lead works so well: Pb2+ has a “lone pair” of electrons ($6s^2$) that creates an asymmetric coordination environment, producing a very fluid, very optically dense glass with unusually wide melting ranges. No other flux replicates this combination of properties.

Historical & Regional Flux Traditions

Every ceramic culture in history solved the same fundamental problem—“how do I melt silica at a temperature my kiln can reach?”—and each arrived at a different flux solution shaped by local geology, available fuels, kiln technology, and aesthetic preference. The result is a global map of flux traditions that doubles as a tour of the flux simplex.

The Ancient World: Lead & Alkali

Mesopotamia & Egypt (3000–500 BCE): The earliest glazes were alkaline—plant ash (high Na2O, K2O, CaO) applied to quartz bodies and fired in wood or dung kilns. Egyptian faience used a self-glazing technique where soluble soda migrated to the surface during drying and formed a thin alkali glaze on firing. These glazes were high-expansion, chemically fragile, and limited to turquoise (Cu2+ in soda flux) and blue (cobalt). Flux fingerprint: $\text{Na}_2\text{O} \approx 0.5–0.7$, $\text{CaO} \approx 0.2–0.3$, little else.

Roman Empire (200 BCE–500 CE): Roman potters adopted PbO as their primary flux, producing the brilliant honey-amber and green lead-glazed earthenware that spread across the Mediterranean. Lead's low melting point (usable below $1000\;\text{°C}$) and forgiving firing range made it ideal for the relatively primitive updraft kilns of the period. Flux fingerprint: $\text{PbO} \approx 0.5–0.7$, minor alkalis from clay impurities.

East Asia: The Calcium-Feldspar Revolution

Chinese stoneware & porcelain (1000 BCE–present): China's access to high-quality kaolin and high-temperature wood-fired climbing kilns (reaching cone 9–12) enabled a fundamentally different flux strategy: calcium-dominant, high-fire. Classic Longquan celadon relies on a simple flux system—calcia from limestone plus small amounts of potash from feldspar—fired hot enough for calcia's full fluxing power to engage. The result is the jade-like depth, iron-green colour, and extraordinary durability that has been admired for a millennium.

Flux fingerprint (Longquan celadon): $\text{CaO} \approx 0.55–0.65$, $\text{K}_2\text{O} \approx 0.15–0.25$, $\text{MgO} \approx 0.05–0.10$, $\text{Na}_2\text{O} \approx 0.05$.

ℹ️ Jun (Chün) Blue — Phase Separation in Action
The celebrated Jun blue glaze of the Song dynasty (∼1100 CE) is not coloured by a pigment—it is an optical effect caused by liquid-liquid phase separation (see §sec:phase_sep). The high-lime, low-alumina, phosphorus-bearing composition undergoes spinodal decomposition on cooling, creating nanoscale droplets that scatter short wavelengths (Rayleigh scattering). The flux system is critical: high CaO ($\sim 0.55$) provides the thermodynamic driving force for immiscibility; trace P2O5 from bone ash seeds the phase boundary. No other flux combination produces this effect.

Japanese traditions:

  • Shino: The most alkali-heavy of all classic stoneware glazes. $\text{Na}_2\text{O} + \text{K}_2\text{O} \gt 0.60$, $\text{CaO} \lt 0.15$. Feldspar-heavy, silica-poor, producing the thick, soft, orange-peel texture and warm carbon-trapping characteristic. The extreme alkali content makes shino one of the least durable stoneware glazes—not food-safe by modern standards without modification.
  • Oribe: Copper green over feldspar-based glaze. Moderate alkali, moderate calcia, with the alkali content tuned to produce the particular green that copper gives in a sodium-calcium borosilicate matrix.
  • Wood ash glazes (Iga, Shigaraki, Bizen): Accidental glazing from wood ash falling on ware during multi-day firings. Flux composition is whatever the wood delivered: $\text{CaO} \approx 0.40–0.60$, $\text{K}_2\text{O} \approx 0.10–0.20$, $\text{MgO} \approx 0.05–0.15$, $\text{P}_2\text{O}_5 \approx 0.03–0.10$, plus $\text{Na}_2\text{O}$ and micronutrients.

Korean celadon (Goryeo dynasty, 918–1392): Korean celadons share the Chinese calcium-feldspar approach but with higher iron content and a subtly different Ca:K ratio, producing the distinctive “kingfisher blue-green” praised in Chinese sources as surpassing their own celadons.

The Islamic World: Extreme Alkalis

Persian & Central Asian alkaline glazes (800–1500 CE): Islamic potters developed the most alkali-extreme glazes in history. Using plant ash (from desert halophytes rich in sodium) and natron deposits, they achieved $\text{Na}_2\text{O} \gt 0.6$ in UMF—far higher than any other tradition. These soda-rich glazes produced the iconic turquoise (Cu2+), cobalt blue, and lustre-ware that defined Islamic ceramic art.

The extreme alkali content created challenges: high expansion (addressed by applying thick glazes on porous bodies that absorbed the stress), poor durability (many surviving examples show surface deterioration), and limited colour palette (high-alkali melts dissolve many pigments). But the extraordinary clarity, depth, and brilliance of soda-rich glass—combined with the low firing temperatures achievable—outweighed these drawbacks for decorative ware.

Europe: Lead, Tin, and the March to Industry

Mediterranean tin-opacified ware (Majolica/Faience): Tin ($\text{SnO}_2$) opacified lead glazes created the white surface that made painted decoration possible. The flux system is PbO-dominant ($0.4–0.6$) with moderate alkalis, and $\text{SnO}_2$ at 5–10% acts as a suspended opacifier (not a flux). This is a lead-era technology with direct lineage from Roman lead glazes.

Salt glazing (Germany, 1300–present): Not a flux applied to the ware but a flux delivered as vapour. Sodium chloride thrown into the kiln at peak temperature decomposes: $2\text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \to \text{Na}_2\text{O} + 2\text{HCl}$. The $\text{Na}_2\text{O}$ vapour reacts with the alumino-silicate clay surface to form a thin soda-alumina-silica glaze in situ. This is the purest possible “soda flux” application: a single-flux glaze formed at the atomic scale.

Industrial revolution (1750–present): Josiah Wedgwood and successors replaced intuitive flux selection with systematic chemistry. The development of synthetic frits (pre-melted glass powders with controlled flux compositions), boron chemistry (borax, Gerstley borate, colemanite), and eventually lead-free formulations drove flux technology toward the multi-component, entropy-maximised systems dominant today.

ℹ️ Lessons from 5,000 Years
  1. Geology determines flux: Every tradition's flux preference mirrors its mineral resources. Soda where there is natron or halophytic plants; lead where there is galena; calcia where there is limestone and hot kilns.
  2. Single-flux systems are fragile: Pure lead, pure soda, and pure calcia glazes all suffer from narrow firing ranges, specific defect patterns, and limited colour response. History moved toward multi-flux systems wherever kiln technology allowed.
  3. Temperature unlocks earths: Below cone 2, alkalis and lead dominate because calcia and magnesia lack the activation energy to flux effectively. Above cone 6, earths become primary fluxes. The historical shift from earthenware to stoneware is fundamentally a shift from alkali/lead to calcium/magnesium flux systems.
  4. Aesthetics and chemistry co-evolve: Shino's soft surface requires extreme alkali. Celadon's jade depth requires high calcia. Crystal glazes require high zinc. The aesthetic intent and the flux chemistry are inseparable.

Flux Ratio ($R_{\text{flux}}$) — Deep Dive

⚡ Flux Ratio
\begin{equation} R_{\text{flux}} = \frac{\sum \text{fluxes}}{\SiO + \AlO + \BO + \sum \text{fluxes}} \label{eq:flux_ratio} \end{equation}

The flux ratio measures the molar proportion of network-modifying oxides relative to the total oxide content. It treats all fluxes as equivalent by moles—which is its strength (simplicity) and its weakness (it ignores flux identity).

Practical thresholds:

Limitation: Two glazes with identical $R_{\text{flux}}$ can behave completely differently. Glaze A with $R_{\text{flux}} = 0.25$, composed entirely of CaO, will be a stiff, durable, high-fire matte. Glaze B with $R_{\text{flux}} = 0.25$, composed entirely of Na2O, will be a fluid, crazing, chemically fragile gloss. The flux ratio provides no way to distinguish them.

R2O:RO Ratio — The Alkali-Earth Balance

⚡ R2O:RO Ratio
\begin{equation} \text{R}_2\text{O}:\text{RO} = \frac{\NaO + \KO + \LiO}{\CaOx + \MgOx + \BaOx + \SrOx + \ZnOx + \PbOx} \label{eq:r2o_ro} \end{equation}

This ratio captures the most fundamental division in the flux family: monovalent versus divalent. It has profound consequences across nearly every property category:

Shannon Entropy — Flux Diversity

Already described in the main report (Eq. \ref{eq:shannon}), but its strategic significance bears emphasis:

Shannon entropy is the only flux metric that captures how many fluxes are present and how evenly they are distributed, independent of total flux amount or alkali/earth balance. It is the missing dimension that transforms flux analysis from a single ratio into a compositional fingerprint.

Mean Field Strength & Mixed Alkali Effect

These are described in detail in the main report. In summary:

  • Mean Field Strength: Weighted average of Dietzel $F$ values. Higher values predict stiffer, more durable glasses. It is the flux-side complement to the former-side metric of NBO/T.
  • Mixed Alkali Effect (MAE): $4 x_{\text{Na}} x_{\text{K}}$, peaked at 50:50 Na:K. Predicts anomalous suppression of expansion, conductivity, and viscosity.

The Five Most Flux-Sensitive Properties

  1. Coefficient of Thermal Expansion: The single property most affected by flux choice. The spread between $\alpha_{\text{Na}_2\text{O}} = 39.5$ and $\alpha_{\text{MgO}} = 0.45$ means that rearranging the flux distribution—without changing total flux at all—can swing COE by $10 \times 10^{-7}\;/{}^\circ\text{C}$ or more.
  2. VFT Viscosity: The GRD model's $B$ and $C$ coefficients are strongly composition-dependent. Alkali-rich melts are dramatically more fluid than earth-rich melts at the same temperature and same total flux.
  3. Chemical Durability: Alkali glasses dissolve under acid and alkaline attack; divalent-rich glasses resist corrosion. The R2O:RO ratio is the single best predictor of durability.
  4. Ionic Conductivity: Dominated by Na+ mobility. Replacing just 0.1 moles of Na2O with K2O (invoking the MAE) can drop conductivity by two orders of magnitude.
  5. Crystallisation Tendency: Ca, Mg, and Zn are the primary drivers of devitrification. Alkalis suppress crystallisation by disrupting the long-range order needed for nucleation.

Flux Effects Not Captured by Any Single Metric

Several important flux-related phenomena cannot be reduced to a single axis:

Eutectic Depression (“Cocktail Effect”)
When multiple fluxes are combined, the system accesses multi-component eutectic compositions where the liquidus temperature is lower than any single-flux binary eutectic. This effect—captured qualitatively by Shannon entropy—is actually a function of specific flux combinations, not just count and evenness. The K2O–CaO–SiO2 ternary has different eutectics than Na2O–CaO–SiO2. A future flux toolkit should compute eutectic depression from multi-component phase diagram data.
Boron Ambiguity
B2O3 is classified as a glass former in Dietzel's scheme (field strength 1.34), but at levels above ∼0.3 moles it increasingly acts as a flux, lowering viscosity and melting temperature. The “boron anomaly”—the transition of B3+ from trigonal to tetrahedral coordination depending on alkali content—means that boron's flux-versus-former character depends on the alkali content. Current flux metrics either include or exclude B2O3 entirely; the reality is that its flux contribution is a smooth function of the surrounding composition.
Volatilisation Losses
Na2O, K2O, B2O3, PbO, and ZnO are volatile at high temperatures. The actual flux composition after firing differs from the input recipe. At cone 10, a glaze can lose 5–15% of its soda and most of its boron. No current Z-axis accounts for this.

Flux-Colorant Interactions

The same colouring oxide in different flux systems can produce completely different colours, intensities, and optical effects. This is one of the most practically important—and least quantified—aspects of flux chemistry. A potter who changes the flux ratio to fix crazing may inadvertently destroy the colour they were trying to preserve.

The Ligand Field Mechanism

Transition-metal colourants (Fe, Cu, Co, Mn, Cr, Ni) derive their colour from d-orbital splitting: the energy gap between $d$-electron energy levels is determined by the electrostatic field of surrounding oxygen ligands. Flux ions change this field by:

  1. Altering coordination geometry: In alkali-rich melts, transition metals tend toward higher coordination numbers (octahedral). In earth-rich melts, some adopt tetrahedral coordination. The colour shift between octahedral and tetrahedral is dramatic—Co2+ is pink in octahedral coordination and deep blue in tetrahedral.
  2. Changing bond distances: The larger the flux cation nearby, the more “stretched” the oxygen shell around the colourant, shifting absorption wavelengths.
  3. Controlling oxidation state: Alkali-rich melts are generally more oxidising (higher oxygen fugacity); earth-rich melts can be more reducing. This affects Fe2+/Fe3+ and Cu+/Cu2+ ratios directly.

Critical Flux-Colour Interactions

Iron in Calcium vs. Alkali: The Celadon Question

The jade-green colour of celadon is not simply “iron in reduction.” It is specifically Fe2+ in a high-calcium, moderate-silica glass matrix. The mechanism:

  • Ca2+'s high field strength stabilises Fe2+ in octahedral coordination, where its $d–d$ transition absorbs red-orange light and transmits blue-green.
  • In alkali-rich fluxes, the weaker electrostatic environment shifts Fe2+'s absorption band, producing amber instead of green.
  • The critical requirement is $\text{CaO} \gt 0.45$ in UMF under moderate reduction. Below this threshold, even in reduction, iron-bearing glazes tend amber rather than green.

Atlas implication: A “celadon potential” axis could be defined as $f(\text{CaO}, \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3, \text{atmosphere})$, predicting whether a given flux system will produce green vs. amber with iron.

Copper Red: The Most Flux-Sensitive Colour

Copper-red glazes (sang de boeuf, oxblood, flambé) require Cu+ nanoparticles (or Cu0 colloids) suspended in the glass matrix. This is achieved only under reducing conditions with a narrow compositional window:

  • Alkali required: $\text{Na}_2\text{O} + \text{K}_2\text{O} \gt 0.25$. Alkalis stabilise the colloidal copper by lowering the glass viscosity enough for copper to nucleate into nanoparticles during cooling, while preventing re-oxidation.
  • Boron helps: $\text{B}_2\text{O}_3 \approx 0.15–0.30$. Boron further lowers viscosity and is empirically essential for most reliable copper-red recipes.
  • Too much calcium kills it: $\text{CaO} \gt 0.40$ tends to produce liver/plum colours instead of bright red, because calcia's higher field strength alters the copper colloid surface energy.
  • Alumina matters: Low $\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3$ ($\lt 0.20$) favours red; high alumina favours green.

Atlas implication: “Copper red stability” could be a computed axis—a function of alkali content, boron, calcia, alumina, and the R2O:RO ratio.

The Zinc-Chromium Problem

When ZnO and Cr2O3 coexist in a glaze, they form the spinel ZnCr2O4, which is brown. This overrides chromium's normal green colour completely. This is not a gradual shift—it is an abrupt compositional switch:

  • $\text{ZnO} = 0$: Chromium glazes are green.
  • $\text{ZnO} \gt 0.03$: Brown spinel begins forming. Colour shifts toward muddy olive-brown.
  • $\text{ZnO} \gt 0.10$: Fully brown. No green remains.

This is a flux-composition phase stability issue, not a colour-mixing issue. The atlas should flag Zn+Cr co-presence as a colour hazard.

Proposed: Flux-Colour Prediction Engine

A meaningful extension of the atlas would be a colour-prediction module that, given a flux distribution and a colourant addition, estimates the resulting colour in CIE Lab coordinates:

  1. Map the flux vector to a “spectral environment” descriptor (based on mean field strength, alkali fraction, individual oxide effects).
  2. Look up the colourant's absorption spectrum in that environment (from a database of measured glass spectra, e.g., Bamford 1977, Weyl 1951).
  3. Compute the transmitted/reflected colour as a CIE Lab value.
  4. Display as a colour swatch overlaid on the 3D surface.

This would be the first glaze calculator to predict colour from chemistry, moving beyond the current approach of “add cobalt for blue” intuitions.

Tier 4: Flux Interaction Map

ℹ️ Proposed Feature
Flux Interaction Heatmap — a matrix showing pairwise synergy/antagonism between fluxes for a specific property.

Concept: For a chosen property $P$ (e.g., COE), compute the second-order interaction term for every flux pair:

\[I_{ij} = P(x_i + \delta, x_j + \delta) - P(x_i + \delta, x_j) - P(x_i, x_j + \delta) + P(x_i, x_j)\]

A positive $I_{ij}$ means the two fluxes synergise—their combined effect on $P$ is greater than the sum of their individual effects. A negative $I_{ij}$ means they antagonise—their combination partially cancels out.

Known interactions that would appear:

  • Na–K: Strong negative interaction for conductivity (MAE), moderate negative for expansion.
  • Ca–Mg: Eutectic synergy for melting; antagonism for surface texture (Ca = gloss, Mg = matte).
  • Zn–Ca: Competition for crystal nucleation sites.
  • Li–Na: Moderate interaction; lithia partially compensates soda's expansion.

The heatmap would be rendered as a $10 \times 10$ matrix with color-coded cells, selectable by property.

Tier 5: Flux-Aware Recipe Optimisation

ℹ️ Proposed Feature
Flux Optimiser — given target values for multiple properties, find the optimal flux distribution.

Concept: The user specifies constraints:

  • COE $\lt 7.0 \times 10^{-6}$ (no crazing on my body)
  • Viscosity $\log\eta \lt 3.5$ at cone 6 (must melt)
  • Food safety score $\gt 80$ (dinner-safe)
  • Matteness $\gt 50$ (I want a satin/matte surface)

The optimiser searches the 9-dimensional flux simplex (10 fluxes, summing to 1.0) for distributions that satisfy all constraints while minimising a user-selected objective (e.g., maximise entropy, minimise cost, minimise toxicity).

Algorithm: Constrained optimisation on the flux simplex:

\begin{align} \min_{\mathbf{f} \in \Delta^9} \quad & -H(\mathbf{f}) \quad\text{(maximise diversity)} \\ \text{subject to} \quad & \alpha(\mathbf{f}) \lt \alpha_{\max} \\ & \log\eta(\mathbf{f}, T_{\text{cone}}) \lt \eta_{\max} \\ & \text{FoodSafety}(\mathbf{f}) \gt S_{\min} \\ & \text{Matteness}(\mathbf{f}) \gt M_{\min} \\ & f_i \geq 0 \;\forall\, i, \quad \sum_i f_i = 1.0 \end{align}

where $\Delta^9$ is the 9-simplex (the set of all valid flux distributions). This is a non-linear constrained optimisation problem solvable by sequential quadratic programming (SQP) or interior-point methods. Since all property functions are smooth and fast to evaluate, convergence is typically reached in $\lt 100$ iterations.

Flux Ratio Design Patterns

This section provides practical recipes for common glaze-development tasks expressed as flux-ratio manipulations.

Pattern 1: The Craze Fix

Problem: Glaze crazes on the target clay body.

Diagnosis: Check the COE Z-axis. If $\alpha_{\text{glaze}} \gt \alpha_{\text{body}}$, the glaze is in tension.

Fix sequence (in order of impact):

  1. Add silica: Increase SiO2 by 0.2–0.5 in UMF. This is the most powerful single fix because silica has the lowest Appen expansion factor ($\alpha = 0.5$) and simultaneously stiffens the glass.
  2. Reduce soda: Drop Na2O by 0.05–0.10. Each 0.05 moles removed saves ∼2 units of COE.
  3. Replace soda with potash: Invoke the Mixed Alkali Effect. A 50:50 Na:K split gives maximum expansion suppression.
  4. Add magnesia: Replace some calcia with MgO. Each 0.10 moles substituted saves ∼1.2 units of COE.
  5. The lithia trick: Replace 0.10 Na2O with 0.02 Li2O. Equivalent flux power, 3 units less COE.

📝 Craze Fix Worked Example
Starting glaze UMF (crazing): Na2O 0.35, K2O 0.05, CaO 0.50, MgO 0.10.
COE $\approx 8.1 \times 10^{-6}$ (body = 7.5). Stress = $-25$ MPa (tension).

After fix: Na2O 0.15, K2O 0.15, Li2O 0.05, CaO 0.40, MgO 0.20, ZnO 0.05.
COE $\approx 5.8 \times 10^{-6}$. Stress = $+60$ MPa (compression). Shannon $H = 1.67$ (up from 0.95).

The fix achieved three things simultaneously: eliminated crazing, increased flux diversity (wider firing range), and improved chemical durability (less alkali).

Pattern 2: The Matte Transition

Goal: Convert a glossy glaze to a satin or matte surface.

Strategy: Shift the RO portion toward MgO (for buttery matte) or BaO (for cool satin):

  1. Hold total flux constant ($R_{\text{flux}}$ unchanged).
  2. Hold R2O:RO ratio constant (so melting temperature is stable).
  3. Within the RO portion, replace CaO with MgO in increments of 0.05.
  4. Monitor: matteness index, devitrification index, viscosity, COE at each step.
  5. Stop when matteness exceeds the target threshold.

Typical transition: Ca:Mg = 1:0 (gloss) → 0.6:0.4 (satin) → 0.4:0.6 (matte). The transition is gradual and predictable, making it ideal for interpolation-path exploration.

Pattern 3: The Eutectic Seeker

Goal: Lower the maturation temperature of a glaze without adding more total flux.

Strategy: Exploit eutectic depression by maximising flux diversity:

  1. Start from the current flux vector.
  2. Add small amounts (0.03–0.05 moles each) of 2–3 additional flux species not already present.
  3. Redistribute the existing fluxes to maintain $\sum = 1.0$.
  4. Monitor: total fluidity, Shannon entropy, VFT viscosity, eutectic distance.

The cocktail rule of thumb: Going from 2 fluxes to 5 fluxes (at the same total moles) typically lowers the effective maturation temperature by 20–50 °C. This allows cone 6 results from compositions that would normally require cone 8.

Pattern 4: The Durability Bridge

Goal: Make a glaze food-safe without destroying its aesthetic character.

Strategy: Durability is primarily a function of the alkali content and the R2O:RO ratio. The fix is to migrate flux mass from alkalis to earths:

  1. Reduce total R2O below 0.30 (ideally below 0.25).
  2. Increase CaO (strongest durability improver among earths).
  3. Add Al2O3 (increases Si:Al ratio, strengthens the glass network against leaching).
  4. Check: leach risk, food safety, hydration energy—all should improve.
  5. Verify: COE still in range, viscosity still adequate for healing.

Pattern 5: The Crystal Trigger

Goal: Create conditions for controlled crystal growth.

Strategy: Crystallisation requires three conditions: (a) a nucleation agent, (b) a wide crystal window ($T_m - T_g$), and (c) a slow-enough cooling rate. Flux manipulation controls (a) and (b):

  1. For zinc crystals (willemite): Increase ZnO to 0.20–0.30. Add $\SiO \gt 3.5$. Minimise Al2O3.
  2. For calcium crystals (wollastonite): Increase CaO to 0.50–0.70. Moderate $\SiO \approx 3.0$.
  3. For magnesium crystals (diopside, forsterite): MgO $\gt 0.25$, CaO $\gt 0.25$, moderate silica.
  4. In all cases, minimise alkali content (alkalis are “crystal poisons” that disrupt long-range order).
  5. Maximise the crystal window by increasing liquidus temperature relative to $T_g$.

The atlas's crystal window, devitrification index, and Hrubý parameter provide real-time feedback on each adjustment.

Implications for Flux Ratio Design

⚠️ The Feldspar Constraint
You cannot add soda without adding alumina and silica. If a glaze recipe uses soda feldspar as the Na2O source, increasing Na2O by 0.10 also increases Al2O3 by 0.10 and SiO2 by 0.60. This means the Si:Al ratio, the flux ratio, and the total oxide count all change simultaneously. UMF adjustments that look simple (“just add 0.10 Na2O”) are actually three-dimensional moves in composition space.

The same constraint applies to potash feldspar and spodumene. The only way to add pure alkali without alumina or silica is to use soda ash (Na2CO3), potassium carbonate, or lithium carbonate—reactive materials that can cause drying/mixing problems in raw glazes.

The dolomite constraint: Dolomite delivers Ca:Mg exactly 1:1. You cannot independently adjust the Ca:Mg ratio using dolomite alone. To get Ca:Mg = 3:1, you need dolomite plus additional whiting. To get Ca:Mg = 1:3, you need dolomite plus talc (but talc brings silica). Every material choice ripples across the entire UMF.

The boron constraint: All natural boron sources (colemanite, ulexite, Gerstley borate) also deliver calcium and/or sodium. Frits are the only way to deliver boron in a controlled, decoupled manner—which is why frits dominate commercial low-fire glaze production.

Proposed: Material-Aware Flux Optimiser

A truly useful flux optimiser (see §sec:proposed) should operate in batch space, not just UMF space:

  1. Accept a target flux vector in UMF.
  2. Accept a list of available raw materials (with their known UMF delivery formulas).
  3. Solve the linear system for the batch recipe that most closely achieves the target UMF.
  4. Report which flux targets are achievable with the available materials and which require substitutions or additions.
  5. Flag delivery conflicts: cases where achieving the target flux requires impossible material combinations (e.g., Ca:Mg = 5:1 but only dolomite is available for Mg).

This bridges the gap between the idealised UMF world (where all oxides are independent) and the constrained reality of the materials shelf.

The Defect Triangle

Most glaze defects can be mapped onto a triangle of fundamental causes:

  1. Too much flux: Running, dripping, excessive fluidity, colour washing out.
  2. Too little flux: Dry surface, pinholing, crawling, poor healing, rough texture.
  3. Wrong flux: Crazing (wrong expansion), colour shift (wrong ligand field), leaching (wrong durability), unwanted crystals (wrong nucleation).

The atlas's Z-axes map directly onto this triangle: $R_{\text{flux}}$ captures the too-much/too-little axis; the 6 remaining flux axes plus Shannon entropy capture the “wrong flux” dimension. A defect-diagnostic mode could highlight which Z-axes are in their danger zones for the currently selected composition.

The Flux Simplex — A Mathematical Perspective

Compositional Data on the Simplex

Because flux amounts in UMF sum to exactly 1.0, flux compositions are compositional data—they live on the $(d-1)$-simplex $\Delta^{d-1}$, where $d$ is the number of flux species. For 10 fluxes, the flux distribution occupies the 9-simplex: a 9-dimensional surface embedded in 10-dimensional space, constrained by $\sum_i f_i = 1$ and $f_i \geq 0$.

This has profound mathematical implications:

  1. Euclidean distances are misleading. Two compositions $\mathbf{f}$ and $\mathbf{g}$ that look close in Euclidean space may be compositionally very different when viewed on the simplex. The proper distance metric is the Aitchison distance:
    \begin{equation} d_A(\mathbf{f}, \mathbf{g}) = \left\| \text{clr}(\mathbf{f}) - \text{clr}(\mathbf{g}) \right\|_2 \label{eq:aitchison} \end{equation}
    where $\text{clr}(\mathbf{f})$ is the centred log-ratio transform: $\text{clr}(f_i) = \ln(f_i / g_m(\mathbf{f}))$, and $g_m$ is the geometric mean.
  2. Linear interpolation on the simplex is not straight-line interpolation. The Walk feature's UMF interpolation (Eq. \ref{eq:interpolation}) works because it operates on unnormalised oxide amounts. But if we want to interpolate within the flux distribution (holding total flux constant), the geometrically correct interpolation uses the power mean (perturbation) in the simplex:
    \begin{equation} \mathbf{f}(t) = \mathcal{C}\!\left[\mathbf{f}_A^{1-t} \odot \mathbf{f}_B^{t}\right] \label{eq:simplex_interp} \end{equation}
    where $\mathcal{C}[\cdot]$ is the closure operator (normalise to sum 1) and $\odot$ denotes component-wise operations.
  3. PCA on compositional data requires ILR/CLR transforms. Standard PCA on raw flux proportions produces spurious correlations because of the closure constraint. To discover the true variance structure of flux distributions across a database of glazes, the data must first be transformed to ILR (isometric log-ratio) coordinates, as developed by Aitchison (1986) and Egozcue et al. (2003).

Why This Matters for the Atlas

The current atlas treats flux proportions as ordinary numbers: it computes ratios, differences, and weighted sums directly. This works well for individual property calculations (the Appen model is linear in moles, not log-ratios). But for comparative and statistical operations—clustering glazes by flux similarity, computing principal components of flux variation across a database, finding the nearest neighbour in flux space—the simplex geometry must be respected.

Planned implementation (v4.0): The Compositional Data Analysis (CoDA) module will introduce ILR/CLR transforms, Aitchison distance, and simplex-aware interpolation for flux analysis.

Advanced Flux Topics

The Boron Problem

Boron is the most complicated oxide in the entire system. In Dietzel's field-strength classification, B3+ ($F = 1.34$) is definitively a network former—it builds BO3 triangles and BO4 tetrahedra that integrate into the glass network. Yet every potter knows that adding boron lowers the melting temperature, which is the defining trait of a flux.

The resolution lies in boron's dual coordination:

  • Trigonal BO3: Three-coordinated boron forms planar triangular units. These are structurally stiff and contribute to network rigidity. But the planar geometry means BO3 sheets are weakly bonded to adjacent silica tetrahedra, creating structural defects where the network is easily disrupted.
  • Tetrahedral BO4: Four-coordinated boron forms tetrahedra that integrate seamlessly with the SiO2 network. BO4 requires a charge-compensating alkali cation (exactly as Al2O3 does), so its abundance depends on the alkali content.

The boron anomaly: At low alkali content, boron is predominantly BO3 (trigonal). As alkali content increases, more boron converts to BO4 (tetrahedral). There is a maximum in BO4 fraction at approximately one mole of alkali per mole of boron, after which further alkali begins creating NBOs on silicon instead. This creates a complex, non-monotonic relationship between boron content, alkali content, and effective flux behaviour.

Current atlas treatment: The atlas excludes B2O3 from the flux sum in the flux ratio calculation. This is a simplification that works for glazes with small amounts of boron ($\lt 0.2$) but becomes inaccurate at higher levels.

Proposed improvement: A “fractional flux” model where the fluxing contribution of boron is computed as a function of the alkali content:

\[f_{\text{B, eff}} = [\BO] \times \left(1 - \frac{[\text{R}_2\text{O}]}{[\BO]}\right)^+ \]
where $(x)^+ = \max(0, x)$. This assigns zero flux character to boron when alkali is sufficient to convert all B to BO4, and increasing flux character when boron exceeds the alkali charge-compensation capacity.

Flux Competition for Alumina Charge Compensation

When Al2O3 enters the glass network in tetrahedral coordination, each Al3+ requires a charge-balancing cation. This cation is “consumed” from the flux pool—it can no longer act as a network modifier. The competition follows a preference order:

\[\text{Na}^+ \gt \text{K}^+ \gt \text{Li}^+ \gt \text{Ca}^{2+} \gt \text{Mg}^{2+}\]

This means:

  • In peralkaline compositions (excess alkali over alumina), all alumina is charge-compensated and the remaining alkali acts as modifier. The flux system works as expected.
  • In peraluminous compositions (ASI $\gt 1.0$, alumina exceeds alkali), some alumina cannot be charge-compensated and enters octahedral coordination or is compensated by divalent cations. This removes alkaline-earth flux from the modifier pool, making the glaze behave as if it had less flux than the UMF suggests.

Consequence: The effective flux amount depends not just on the raw UMF values but on the Al2O3 content and the ASI. A flux ratio of 0.25 in a peralkaline glaze (ASI = 0.6) may correspond to an effective flux ratio of only 0.15 in a peraluminous glaze (ASI = 1.2) because half the alkali is locked up charge-compensating alumina.

Proposed improvement: An “effective flux” metric that subtracts the flux consumed by alumina charge compensation:

\[R_{\text{flux,eff}} = \frac{\sum \text{fluxes} - [\AlO]}{\text{total oxides}} \quad \text{when ASI} \lt 1\]

\[R_{\text{flux,eff}} = \frac{\sum \text{fluxes} - \sum \text{R}_2\text{O}}{\text{total oxides}} \quad \text{when ASI} \gt 1\]

Phase Separation & Immiscibility

Not every molten glaze is a single homogeneous liquid. Under certain flux distributions, the melt spontaneously separates into two (or more) coexisting liquid phases that differ in composition and physical properties. This phenomenon—liquid–liquid immiscibility—is the engine behind some of the most prized visual effects in ceramic history. It is also the source of some of the most baffling defects.

The Thermodynamic Basis

Glass-forming melts are stabilised by entropy of mixing. At high temperature, entropy dominates and the melt is homogeneous. As temperature decreases, the enthalpy of mixing (which can be positive if certain oxide pairs “dislike” being mixed) can overcome the entropy contribution. Below a critical temperature called the consolute temperature, the system enters a miscibility gap—a region of the phase diagram where a single-phase liquid is thermodynamically unstable with respect to two separate liquids.

The two mechanisms of separation are:

  1. Nucleation and growth: The system is metastable; separation requires random fluctuations to create droplets of the minority phase. Droplets grow by diffusion and Ostwald ripening. Result: isolated spherical droplets in a continuous matrix.
  2. Spinodal decomposition: The system is unstable to any fluctuation. Composition waves grow at a characteristic wavelength set by gradient-energy penalties. Result: an interconnected, worm-like microstructure that coarsens with time.

Which mechanism operates depends on the quench path: inside the spinodal curve, decomposition is spontaneous; between the spinodal and the binodal, nucleation and growth dominates.

The Barium Matte Mechanism

Barium matte glazes achieve their distinctive silky-smooth surface through a different immiscibility route: the SiO2–BaO binary has a broad miscibility gap. When BaO exceeds $\sim 0.15$ with adequate silica and low alumina, the melt separates into a barium-rich and a silica-rich phase on cooling. The two phases solidify with different surface energies, producing the characteristic “buttery” matte texture that is neither a crystal matte (like dolomite) nor simply an under-fired surface.

Flux implication: Replacing CaO with BaO in a mid-fire transparent glaze can trigger a sudden matte transition when the immiscibility boundary is crossed—a discontinuity that UMF analysis alone does not predict.

Glaze–Body Interface & Flux Migration

A glaze is not a painting on a wall—it is a graded chemical system that reacts with the ceramic body beneath it. The boundary between glaze and body is not a sharp line but a diffusion zone typically $20–200\;\text{}$ thick where flux ions migrate, crystals nucleate, and new phases form. Understanding this interface is essential because:

  1. The interface determines adhesion—a weak interface causes shivering or crawling at the body–glaze boundary.
  2. Flux migration across the interface changes the effective composition of both the glaze and the body.
  3. Interface reactions produce intermediate phases (mullite needles, anorthite crystals, cristobalite at the body surface) that affect thermal expansion matching.
  4. Toxic flux elements (Pb, Ba) can migrate through the glaze to the surface, affecting food safety.

The Diffusion Mechanism

Flux migration follows Fick's laws of diffusion, driven by concentration gradients between the flux-rich glaze and the flux-poor body. The key variables are:

  • Concentration gradient ($\Delta C$): Higher flux content in the glaze relative to the body drives faster migration. A high-flux glaze (Na2O = 0.40) on a low-flux stoneware body creates a steep gradient.
  • Temperature and time: Diffusion rates follow an Arrhenius relationship: $D = D_0 \exp(-E_a / RT)$. Every 50 °C increase roughly doubles the diffusion coefficient. Longer soak times at peak temperature allow deeper penetration.
  • Ionic radius and charge: Small, monovalent ions diffuse fastest. The mobility hierarchy is:
    \[\text{Li}^+ \gt \text{Na}^+ \gt \text{K}^+ \gt \text{Ca}^{2+} \gt \text{Mg}^{2+} \gt \text{Ba}^{2+}\]
    Lithium is so mobile that it can diffuse entirely through a $200\;\text{}$ glaze layer in a typical cone 6 firing.
  • Melt viscosity: Lower viscosity (more flux) accelerates diffusion. The system is self-reinforcing: flux accumulation at the interface further lowers viscosity there, accelerating further migration.

What Happens at the Interface

Mullite Crystal Growth

At high-fire temperatures ($\gt 1200\;\text{°C}$), the body clay decomposes into mullite (Al6Si2O13) and glassy silica. If flux from the glaze penetrates the upper body layer, it dissolves the nascent mullite on the body side while promoting mullite crystal growth into the glaze layer. These mullite needles anchor the glaze to the body mechanically—the “roots” of a well-adhered glaze.

ℹ️ The Mullite Anchor Effect
Well-fitted stoneware glazes show 5–$20\;\text{}$ mullite needles growing from the body into the glaze base. These needles:
  • Increase the effective surface area at the interface by 3–10×, dramatically improving adhesion.
  • Raise the local Al2O3 content in the lower glaze, which increases viscosity and suppresses further flux migration—a self-limiting mechanism.
  • Create a graded expansion profile (body → mullite zone → glaze) that distributes thermal stress over the transition rather than concentrating it at a sharp boundary.
Calcia-rich glazes promote strong mullite anchoring because Ca2+ acts as a mineraliser for mullite growth. Alkali-rich glazes may dissolve mullite too aggressively, yielding a glassy interface with poor mechanical interlock.

Anorthite Layer Formation

When a calcia-rich glaze ($\CaOx \gt 0.40$) meets a high-alumina body (e.g., stoneware or porcelain), calcium diffusing into the body reacts with alumina and silica to form anorthite (CaAl2Si2O8)—a feldspar mineral. This anorthite interlayer:

  • Has a thermal expansion coefficient intermediate between glaze and body.
  • Is mechanically strong and chemically durable.
  • Acts as a diffusion barrier, slowing further flux migration.

Flux significance: This is why calcia is the “safest” primary flux for stoneware. It forms beneficial interface phases. Soda, by contrast, tends to dissolve into the body without forming protective interlayer crystals.

Cristobalite Shell Formation

In the uppermost layer of the body (within $\sim 50\;\text{}$ of the interface), flux penetration from the glaze can convert amorphous silica in the body into cristobalite—a high-temperature polymorph of SiO2 with a sharp volume change at $220\;\text{°C}$ (the α-β inversion). This creates a local expansion mismatch that can either:

  • Help: Put the glaze into slight compression, closing craze lines. (This is a common mechanism for “self-healing” glazes on silica-rich bodies.)
  • Hurt: If the cristobalite layer is too thick, the $220\;\text{°C}$ inversion produces enough stress to crack the body at the interface (dunting).

The cristobalite shell thickness is controlled by the amount and type of flux that penetrates: alkalis (especially Na2O and Li2O) are potent mineralisers for cristobalite formation; calcia promotes mullite over cristobalite.

Flux Migration Profiles

The concentration of each flux as a function of depth from the glaze surface follows a characteristic error-function profile (the solution to Fick's second law for diffusion from a constant source):

⚡ Flux Diffusion Profile
\[C(x, t) = C_{\text{surface}} \cdot \operatorname{erfc}\!\left(\frac{x}{2\sqrt{Dt}}\right)\]

where $C(x,t)$ is the concentration of a flux oxide at depth $x$ from the glaze surface after firing time $t$, $D$ is the effective diffusion coefficient, and $\operatorname{erfc}$ is the complementary error function.

⚠️ Lithium's Disappearing Act
Lithium diffuses so rapidly that in a long high-fire soak ($\gt 30$ min above 1200 °C), a significant fraction of the Li2O originally in the glaze migrates into the body. The fired glaze composition is measurably different from the batch recipe. This is why lithia-containing glazes sometimes craze unpredictably: the actual glaze expansion is higher than calculated because Li2O (the lowest-expansion flux) has partially escaped. Potters call this “lithium burnout,” but it is really lithium migration, not volatilisation.

Reverse Migration: Body → Glaze

Migration is not one-directional. Body constituents can migrate into the glaze, especially:

  • Iron: Fe2+/3+ from the body can diffuse into the glaze base, causing brown “toasting” or warm colour development at the glaze–body junction. This is responsible for the warm amber tone at the foot of many high-fire glazes, and the intentional “flashing” effects on woodfired ware where iron vapour from one pot colours the glaze of its neighbour.
  • Carbon: Organic carbon trapped in the body releases CO/CO2 that must escape through the molten glaze. If the glaze seals too early (low-melting flux like Na2O or PbO), carbon becomes trapped as bubbles (bloating) or reduces iron at the interface (carbon trapping in shino glazes).
  • Silica: At very high fire, silica from the body dissolves into the glaze, effectively increasing the glaze Si:Al ratio and shifting its properties. This is a major factor in wood-fired kilns where prolonged cone 10–12 firings (>24 hours) “grow” the glaze from the body surface (natural ash glaze + body silica dissolution).

Practical Consequences for Flux Design

  1. Expansion matching must account for migration: A glaze with nominal $\alpha = 7.0 \times 10^{-6}$ K-1 may have an effective expansion of $7.5$ at the body interface (depleted in Li, enriched in Fe). Calculating expansion for the fired surface composition rather than the batch composition would improve craze/shiver prediction.
  2. Soak time is a flux variable: Longer soaks allow deeper migration, effectively reducing the near-surface flux content. “More is better” for healing pinholes but risks lithia loss and cristobalite formation.
  3. Interface compatibility spans: Not all body–glaze combinations are compatible. A high-barium glaze on a low-alumina earthenware body may not form a protective interlayer, leading to poor adhesion. A “compatibility matrix” mapping $\{$glaze flux profile$\} \times \{$body type$\}$ would be a valuable atlas feature.
  4. Food safety depends on interface integrity: Lead and barium can migrate to the surface through diffusion in the glassy matrix, especially in under-fired or under-stabilised glazes. The “depth-of-burial” of toxic flux ions below the surface determines leachability.

Proposed Atlas Features for Interface Analysis

  • Migration Risk Index: A Z-axis that estimates the fraction of each flux expected to migrate into the body for a given firing schedule. Based on tabulated $D$ values and the diffusion equation above.
  • Post-Migration Composition: A “corrected UMF” that adjusts for predicted diffusion losses (Li, Na) and body gains (Fe, Si). This would replace the static UMF with a dynamic, firing-aware composition.
  • Interface Phase Predictor: Using the CaO–Al2O3–SiO2 phase diagram, predict whether the interface will produce mullite, anorthite, cristobalite, or remain glassy, based on the flux that arrives at the body surface.
  • Adhesion Score: A composite metric combining mullite anchor potential (favoured by high Ca, moderate flux diffusion, high-Al body) with expansion mismatch. A low adhesion score flags compositions at risk of shivering or crawling from the body.

Multi-Objective Optimisation & Pareto Frontiers

Real glaze design is never about optimising a single property. The potter simultaneously needs:

  • Low COE (no crazing) and adequate fluidity (no pinholes).
  • High durability (food safe) and particular surface texture (satin, matte, glossy).
  • Correct colour response and wide firing range.

These objectives conflict: increasing fluidity (more alkali) raises COE and lowers durability; increasing durability (more silica, more alumina) stiffens the melt and risks crawling. The mathematics of multi-objective optimisation provides a rigorous framework for navigating these trade-offs.

The Pareto Frontier Concept

Given two objectives $f_1$ (e.g., minimise COE) and $f_2$ (e.g., maximise fluidity), a glaze composition $\mathbf{x}$ is Pareto-optimal if no other composition $\mathbf{y}$ exists such that $f_1(\mathbf{y}) \leq f_1(\mathbf{x})$ and $f_2(\mathbf{y}) \geq f_2(\mathbf{x})$ simultaneously (with at least one strict inequality). The set of all Pareto-optimal compositions forms the Pareto frontier—a curve (or surface, for $\gt 2$ objectives) that represents the best achievable trade-offs.

ℹ️ What the Pareto Frontier Tells You
  • Every point on the frontier is “best”: You cannot improve one objective without sacrificing another.
  • Points below the frontier are suboptimal: A better flux distribution exists.
  • The shape of the frontier reveals the trade-off structure: A concave frontier means trade-offs are harsh (small gains cost a lot); a convex frontier means trade-offs are gentle (compromise solutions are nearly as good as extremes).
  • The potter chooses where on the frontier to operate: This is a design decision, not a mathematical one.

Pareto Frontiers in Flux Space

Computing the Frontier

For a set of $N$ glaze compositions (drawn from the Stull grid or from a database), computing the Pareto frontier is algorithmically straightforward:

  1. Evaluate objectives $f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_k$ for each composition.
  2. Sort by $f_1$.
  3. Sweep, maintaining a running maximum of $f_2$. Any composition whose $f_2$ sets a new maximum is Pareto-optimal.
  4. For $k \gt 2$ objectives, use non-dominated sorting (NSGA-II or similar evolutionary algorithm).

The atlas's existing 133 Z-axes can serve as the objective functions directly. The user would select two or more Z-axes as objectives, and the atlas would compute and display the Pareto frontier overlaid on the Stull grid.

  2. 3D Pareto Surface: For three objectives, the frontier becomes a surface displayed in the existing 3D viewport. The user can rotate and explore the trade-off surface interactively.
  3. What-If on the Frontier: Combined with the flux-slider tool (§sec:proposed), the user could “walk” along the Pareto frontier, watching the flux fingerprint change as they trade one objective for another in real time.
  4. Constraint Filters: Before computing the frontier, apply hard constraints (e.g., “no barium,” “no lead,” “flux ratio $\gt 0.20$”) to restrict the feasible set.
📝 Pareto in Practice — The Craze-Free Satin Problem
A potter wants a satin-matte surface at cone 6 that does not craze. The objectives are:
  • Minimise COE ($\alpha \lt 6.5 \times 10^{-6}$ K-1)
  • Achieve satin surface (devitrification index $D_I \gt 0.4$ or barium matte $\BaOx \gt 0.12$)
The unconstrained minima are incompatible: low COE favours high MgO (which gives true mattes, not satin) and low alkali; satin texture from BaO requires enough alkali to flux the barium. The Pareto frontier reveals a narrow corridor at approximately:
Na2O 0.10–0.15, K2O 0.10–0.15, CaO 0.25–0.35, MgO 0.05–0.10, BaO 0.12–0.18
The atlas could highlight this corridor directly on the 3D Stull grid and allow the potter to fine-tune within it using interactive sliders.

Beyond Two Objectives: Desirability Functions

When many objectives compete ($k \gt 3$), visualising the Pareto frontier becomes impractical. An alternative is the Derringer–Suich desirability function, which maps each objective to a $[0,1]$ satisfaction score and combines them with a geometric mean:

⚡ Overall Desirability
\[D_{\text{overall}} = \left(\prod_{i=1}^{k} d_i^{w_i}\right)^{1/\sum w_i}\]

where $d_i$ is the desirability score for objective $i$ (0 = unacceptable, 1 = ideal), and $w_i$ is the importance weight assigned by the potter.

Each $d_i$ is defined by three points: the worst acceptable value ($d=0$), the target value ($d=1$), and the shape exponent (linear, concave, or convex scaling). The potter sets these intuitively:

Property$d=0$$d=1$Weight
COE ($\times 10^{-6}$)$\gt 8.0$$\lt 6.0$3 (critical)
Fluidity$\lt 0.3$$\gt 0.7$2 (important)
Durability$\lt 0.4$$\gt 0.8$3 (critical)
Satin texture$D_I \lt 0.2$$D_I \gt 0.5$1 (desired)

The overall desirability $D_{\text{overall}}$ becomes a single Z-axis that encodes the potter's entire set of requirements. The atlas could display $D_{\text{overall}}$ as a colour overlay on the Stull grid, with hot spots showing where all requirements are simultaneously well-satisfied. This collapses multi-objective optimisation into a single, intuitive visual search.
