Introduction: Why Glass Has Color

A ceramic glaze, in its base state, is a colorless or near-colorless glass. The optical bandgap of a typical stoneware glass ($E_g \approx 3.0‚Äì3.5$ eV) places the absorption edge in the near-UV, well below the visible spectrum. The glass transmits all visible wavelengths equally and appears transparent or, when applied over a white body, simply white.

Color arises when this transparency is selectively broken ‚Äî when certain wavelengths of visible light are absorbed and others are transmitted or reflected. In ceramic glazes, three mechanisms produce color:

  1. Ligand field (crystal field) absorption: Transition-metal cations (Fe, Co, Cu, Mn, Cr, Ni, V) embedded in the glass network have partially filled $d$-electron shells. The surrounding oxide ligands split the $d$-orbital energy levels, creating allowed electronic transitions in the visible spectrum. Each transition absorbs light at specific wavelengths, producing the complementary color. This is the dominant color mechanism in ceramic glazes.
  2. Charge-transfer absorption: Electron transfer between adjacent cations of different oxidation states (Fe$^{2+} \to$ Fe3+ intervalence charge transfer, or O$^{2-} \to$ Fe3+ ligand-to-metal charge transfer) produces broad, intense absorption bands, typically in the UV-blue region. This mechanism produces amber/brown colors in iron-bearing glasses.
  3. Colloidal absorption (Mie scattering): Nanoscale metallic particles (Cu0, Au0, Ag0) or semiconductor nanocrystals (CdS, CdSe) interact with light through surface plasmon resonance, producing intense colors that depend on particle size and shape. This is the mechanism behind copper red, gold ruby (Purple of Cassius), and silver yellow stain.

This report focuses primarily on mechanism (1), with treatment of (2) and (3) where they are essential to understanding specific colorant systems.

Crystal Field Theory Fundamentals

The Five $d$-Orbitals

Transition metals have partially filled $3d$ electron shells. In a free ion (no surrounding ligands), all five $d$-orbitals are degenerate ‚Äî they have the same energy. The five orbitals are:

OrbitalShapeOrientation
$d_{xy}$Four-lobed, planarLobes between $x$ and $y$ axes
$d_{xz}$Four-lobed, planarLobes between $x$ and $z$ axes
$d_{yz}$Four-lobed, planarLobes between $y$ and $z$ axes
$d_{x^2 - y^2}$Four-lobed, planarLobes along $x$ and $y$ axes
$d_{z^2}$Two-lobed + ringLobes along $z$ axis, torus in $xy$ plane

The crucial distinction: $d_{xy}$, $d_{xz}$, and $d_{yz}$ (the $t_{2g}$ set) have lobes that point between the Cartesian axes. $d_{x^2-y^2}$ and $d_{z^2}$ (the $e_g$ set) have lobes that point directly along the axes.

Crystal Field Splitting

When a transition-metal cation is placed inside a glass network, it is surrounded by negatively charged oxide ions (O2-). These ligands create an electrostatic field that breaks the degeneracy of the five $d$-orbitals.

‚ö° Crystal Field Splitting Energy
\[ \Delta = E(e_g) - E(t_{2g}) \quad [\text{octahedral}] \]
\[ \Delta_t = E(t_2) - E(e) \quad [\text{tetrahedral}] \]
The crystal field splitting energy $\Delta$ determines which wavelengths of light are absorbed. $\Delta$ is measured in /cm (wavenumbers) or eV.

Octahedral Coordination (6-fold)

In octahedral coordination, six O2- ligands sit along the $\pm x$, $\pm y$, $\pm z$ directions. The $e_g$ orbitals ($d_{x^2-y^2}$ and $d_{z^2}$) point directly at the ligands and experience strong electrostatic repulsion. The $t_{2g}$ orbitals ($d_{xy}$, $d_{xz}$, $d_{yz}$) point between the ligands and experience less repulsion. Result: $e_g$ is pushed up in energy; $t_{2g}$ is pushed down.

üî¨ The Color Connection
An electron in the lower $t_{2g}$ set can absorb a photon of energy $\Delta$ and jump to the upper $e_g$ set. This $d‚Äìd$ transition removes light of wavelength $\lambda = hc/\Delta$ from the transmitted beam. The wavelengths not absorbed are what reach the eye, producing the observed color.

If $\Delta$ corresponds to red light absorption (‚àº15,000 /cm, $\lambda \approx 670$ nm), the glass appears green/blue (the complementary color). If $\Delta$ corresponds to blue-violet absorption (‚àº22,000 /cm, $\lambda \approx 450$ nm), the glass appears yellow/orange.

Tetrahedral Coordination (4-fold)

In tetrahedral coordination, four O2- ligands sit at alternate corners of a cube. Now the $t_2$ orbitals (the subscript notation changes slightly) point more toward the ligands, while the $e$ orbitals point away. The splitting is inverted compared to octahedral, and smaller:

‚ö° Tetrahedral Splitting
\[ \Delta_t \approx \frac{4}{9}\,\Delta_{\text{oct}} \]
Tetrahedral splitting is roughly $4/9$ of octahedral splitting for the same metal-ligand combination.

This smaller splitting shifts the absorption band to longer wavelengths (lower energy), producing dramatically different colors. The classic example: Co2+ in octahedral coordination appears pink; in tetrahedral coordination, it appears an intense, saturated blue.

Factors Controlling $\Delta$

The crystal field splitting $\Delta$ ‚Äî and hence the color ‚Äî depends on four factors:

  1. Cation identity: Different metals have different inherent $d$-orbital energies. $3d$ metals have smaller $\Delta$ than $4d$ metals, which have smaller $\Delta$ than $5d$ metals.
  2. Oxidation state: Higher oxidation state ‚Üí higher charge ‚Üí stronger field ‚Üí larger $\Delta$. Example: Cr3+ ($\Delta \approx 17,400$ /cm, green) vs. Cr6+ ($\Delta$ so large it absorbs in the UV, producing yellow via charge-transfer).
  3. Coordination geometry: Octahedral $\Delta$ is ‚àº2.25√ó larger than tetrahedral $\Delta$ for the same system. This is the primary reason Co2+ changes from pink to blue.
  4. Ligand field strength: Different ligands create different splitting. The spectrochemical series ranks ligands by their splitting power:
    \[\text{I}^- \lt \text{Br}^- \lt \text{Cl}^- \lt \text{F}^- \lt \text{OH}^- \lt \text{O}^{2-} \lt \text{H}_2\text{O} \lt \text{NH}_3 \lt \text{CN}^-\]
    O2- is a moderate-field ligand, which is why oxide glasses produce moderate $d‚Äìd$ splittings and hence colors spread across the full visible spectrum.

The Tanabe‚ÄìSugano Diagrams

For ions with more than one $d$-electron, the situation is complicated by electron‚Äìelectron repulsion. The Tanabe‚ÄìSugano diagrams (1954) plot all possible electronic state energies as a function of $\Delta/B$ (the ratio of crystal field splitting to the Racah electron-repulsion parameter $B$). These diagrams predict the number, positions, and relative intensities of all possible $d‚Äìd$ absorption bands for any given $d^n$ configuration in octahedral symmetry.

The Stull Atlas does not compute Tanabe‚ÄìSugano diagrams directly, but understanding them explains why:

  • Some colorants produce one broad absorption band (Co2+ tetrahedral)
  • Others produce two or three bands (Cr3+ octahedral)
  • Band positions shift with composition (changing $\Delta$)
  • Band intensities differ by orders of magnitude (symmetry selection rules)

Selection Rules and Intensity

Two quantum-mechanical selection rules govern $d‚Äìd$ transitions:

Laporte rule: In a centrosymmetric environment (octahedral), transitions between orbitals of the same parity ($d \to d$) are formally forbidden. This makes octahedral $d‚Äìd$ bands weak. Tetrahedral sites lack a center of symmetry, so the Laporte rule is partially relaxed ‚Äî tetrahedral bands are ‚àº10‚Äì100√ó more intense.

Spin selection rule: Transitions that require a change in total spin quantum number ($\Delta S \neq 0$) are forbidden. These ‚Äúspin-forbidden‚Äù transitions are extremely weak (often invisible). All $d‚Äìd$ transitions in Mn2+ ($d^5$, high-spin, all electrons unpaired) are spin-forbidden, which is why Mn2+ produces only a pale pink color at high concentrations.

‚ÑπÔ∏è Why Cobalt Blue Is So Intense
Co2+ in tetrahedral coordination benefits from both relaxed selection rules: the tetrahedral site has no center of symmetry (Laporte-allowed), and the main $^4A_2 \to\, ^4T_1(P)$ transition is spin-allowed ($\Delta S = 0$). Combined, these produce the most intense $d‚Äìd$ absorption band of any common ceramic colorant ‚Äî roughly 50√ó stronger than the equivalent octahedral band. This is why as little as 0.25% CoO produces a brilliant, saturated blue.

Iron ‚Äî The Universal Colorant

Iron is the most important colorant in ceramic history. It is present as an impurity in virtually all natural clay bodies and feldspars, and has been deliberately used as a primary colorant for at least 3,000 years. The extraordinary range of iron colors ‚Äî from pale straw yellow to forest green to rich brown to jet black ‚Äî arises from the interplay of oxidation state, coordination, concentration, and atmosphere.

Fe3+ ‚Äî The Oxidation Color

Fe3+ has five $d$-electrons, one in each orbital (high-spin configuration). Every possible $d‚Äìd$ transition requires a spin flip ($\Delta S = 1$), making all transitions spin-forbidden. This means Fe3+ $d‚Äìd$ bands are extremely weak ‚Äî too weak to produce strong body color.

The dominant absorption mechanism for Fe3+ is instead the ligand-to-metal charge transfer (LMCT) band:

\[\text{O}^{2-}(2p) \to \text{Fe}^{3+}(3d): \quad \lambda_{\text{max}} \approx 250\text{--}380\;\text{nm}\]

This intense UV band has a long tail extending into the visible blue-violet region ($400‚Äì500$ nm), absorbing blue and violet light. The result: Fe3+ glasses appear amber to yellow-brown, because blue/violet is removed and red/yellow is transmitted.

üü´ The Amber Glass Bottle
Every amber glass beer bottle gets its color from Fe3+ LMCT absorption. The amber color protects light-sensitive compounds (hops acids) from UV degradation. This is Fe3+ in an oxidised soda-lime glass ‚Äî the simplest iron color system.

At higher concentrations ($\gt 5\%$ Fe2O3), Fe3+‚ÄìFe3+ pair interactions (exchange coupling) and Fe3+ in octahedral sites begin contributing visible absorption, shifting the color from amber toward brown and eventually near-opaque brown-black (tenmoku/kaki territory).

Fe2+ ‚Äî The Reduction Color

Fe2+ ($d^6$, high-spin) has one spin-allowed transition in octahedral coordination:

\[^5T_{2g} \to\, ^5E_g: \quad \Delta \approx 10,000\text{--}11,500\;\text{/cm} \quad (\lambda \approx 870\text{--}1000\;\text{nm})\]

This transition is in the near-infrared, not the visible spectrum. However, the long-wavelength tail of this broad band extends into the red region ($600‚Äì700$ nm), weakly absorbing red light. The result: Fe2+ transmits green and blue light, producing colors from pale aqua to blue-green to celadon green.

üî¨ The Celadon Mystery Solved
The classic celadon green of Song Dynasty Longquan ware is not a ‚Äúgreen‚Äù absorption ‚Äî it is a red absorption. Fe2+ subtracts red from white light, leaving a blue-green residual that the eye perceives as celadon. The color deepens with:
  • Higher Fe2+ fraction (stronger reduction)
  • Higher total iron (more absorbing centers)
  • Thicker glaze layer (longer optical path)
  • Lower optical basicity $\Lambda$ (shifts equilibrium toward Fe2+... wait, actually higher $\Lambda$ promotes reduction)

The Song potters achieved celadon green by:

  1. Using iron-bearing local clays (1 %‚Äì3 % Fe2O3)
  2. Firing in heavy reduction atmosphere
  3. Applying thick glaze layers (1‚Äì3 mm)
  4. Slow cooling to maintain Fe2+ state

Fe2+/Fe3+ Intervalence Charge Transfer

When both Fe2+ and Fe3+ coexist in adjacent sites in the glass network, a third absorption mechanism emerges:

\[\text{Fe}^{2+} + \text{Fe}^{3+} \to \text{Fe}^{3+} + \text{Fe}^{2+}\]

This intervalence charge transfer (IVCT) band appears at ‚àº14,000‚Äì15,000 /cm ($\lambda \approx 680$ nm) ‚Äî squarely in the visible red region. The IVCT band is very broad and moderately intense. It dominates the color of glasses containing both oxidation states in significant amounts.

Colors produced:

  • Low total Fe + moderate IVCT: olive green (celadon's ‚Äúdark side‚Äù)
  • Moderate total Fe + strong IVCT: brown (the classic stoneware brown)
  • High total Fe + strong IVCT: tenmoku / kaki / saturate iron
  • Very high total Fe (>8%): near-opaque dark brown to black

Iron Redox Control

The Fe2+/Fe3+ ratio in the fired glass is controlled by three variables, as described in Equation 33 of the equations report:

‚ö° Iron Redox Equilibrium
\[ \frac{[\text{Fe}^{2+}]}{[\text{Fe}^{3+}]} = f(\Lambda, \; T, \; p_{\text{O}_2}) \]

Optical basicity ($\Lambda$): Higher $\Lambda$ shifts equilibrium toward Fe2+. Each 0.1-unit increase in $\Lambda$ roughly doubles the Fe2+ fraction. Practical implications:

  • Adding alkali flux (increases $\Lambda$) promotes Fe2+ formation even in oxidation
  • Adding silica (decreases $\Lambda$) promotes Fe3+ dominance
  • High-alumina glazes (low $\Lambda$) resist reduction ‚Äî Fe3+ persists

Temperature ($T$): Higher firing temperature promotes Fe2+. The redox equilibrium ‚Äúfreezes in‚Äù during cooling at approximately $T_g + 100$¬∞C. Faster cooling preserves a higher Fe2+ fraction.

Special Iron Effects

Tenmoku (Â§©ÁõÆ)

Tenmoku glazes contain 5‚Äì12% Fe2O3 in a moderately fluxed base. The defining characteristic is a thick, viscous melt layer containing both Fe2+ and Fe3+ in high concentrations. The color is a rich, deep brown-black from Fe2+‚ÄìFe3+ IVCT.

Compositional requirements:

  • Fe2O3: 6‚Äì12 wt%
  • CaO: 5‚Äì10 wt% (calcia promotes iron as a network participant rather than a precipitating phase)
  • Moderate $\Lambda$ (0.52‚Äì0.58): Enough basicity for partial reduction, not so much as to fully reduce iron
  • Moderate viscosity ($\log\eta = 3.5‚Äì5.0$ at $T_{\text{cone}}$): Fluid enough to heal, viscous enough to retain texture

Oil Spot (Ê≤πÊª¥)

Oil-spot tenmoku features circular spots of iron crystal (hematite or magnetite) on a dark brown background. The spots form when:

  1. Rising iron-enriched bubbles burst at the surface during peak temperature
  2. Fe2O3 or Fe3O4 microcrystals nucleate in the iron-rich puddles during cooling
  3. The glaze viscosity is high enough to prevent the crystals from re-dissolving

Atlas equations involved: VFT viscosity (Eq. 11), surface tension (Eq. 26), bubble risk (from viscosity), $T_{rg}$ (Eq. 39).

Hare's Fur (ÂÖîÊØ´)

Hare's fur tenmoku features fine, elongated streaks of iron crystal radiating down the vessel wall. The streaks form when:

  1. Iron oxide crystals nucleate at the glaze surface during cooling
  2. The crystals are carried downward by gravity-driven flow of the viscous melt
  3. Crystal growth continues during descent, creating needle-like streaks

The critical parameter is the viscosity-temperature relationship: the glaze must be fluid enough to flow ($\log\eta \lt 5$) but viscous enough to preserve the streaks ($\log\eta \gt 3$). The VFT working range (Eq. 12) maps this window.

Iron Red / Kaki (Êüø)

Iron red/kaki represents the extreme oxidation end of the iron color spectrum. At 8‚Äì15% Fe2O3 in strong oxidation:

  • Nearly all iron is Fe3+
  • Hematite (Fe2O3) nanocrystals precipitate during cooling
  • These nanocrystals scatter red light (Mie scattering), producing a brick-red, orange, or persimmon color
  • The color depends on crystal size: 50‚Äì100 nm particles scatter red; larger particles scatter orange/yellow

Cobalt ‚Äî The Archetypal Blue

Cobalt is the most powerful and reliable ceramic colorant. As little as 0.1% CoO produces a visible blue tint; 0.5% produces a rich, saturated blue. The color is extraordinarily stable across a wide range of firing temperatures, atmospheres, and base compositions. Cobalt blue is one of the most ancient and universally recognised colours in human culture.

Historical Context

  • Ancient Egypt (‚àº1550 BCE): Cobalt blue glass beads from the 18th Dynasty. The cobalt source was likely alum from the Western Desert oases.
  • China, Tang Dynasty (‚àº750 CE): First confirmed use of cobalt blue on ceramic ware (imported cobalt).
  • Persia (‚àº1200 CE): Development of distinctive cobalt-blue-on-white fritware.
  • China, Yuan/Ming Dynasty (1300‚Äì1600): The golden age of blue-and-white porcelain using imported Persian cobalt (rich in Fe and Mn impurities, creating the characteristic ‚Äúheaped and piled‚Äù effect).
  • Europe, 18th century: Th√©nard's Blue / Cobalt Blue pigment (CoAl2O4 spinel) synthesised as the first modern inorganic blue pigment.

The Physics of Cobalt Blue

üîµ Why Cobalt Blue Is Unique
Co2+ in tetrahedral coordination produces a triple absorption band centered at:
\[^4A_2(F) \to\, ^4T_1(P): \quad \nu \approx 15,000\text{--}17,000\;\text{/cm} \quad (\lambda \approx 590\text{--}670\;\text{nm})\]

This broad band absorbs yellow-orange-red light, transmitting the blue-violet portion of the spectrum. The band is:

  1. Spin-allowed ($\Delta S = 0$): All four unpaired electrons maintain their spin orientation
  2. Laporte partially allowed (tetrahedral sites lack inversion symmetry)
  3. Very intense: Molar absorptivity $\varepsilon \approx 600$ L/mol ‚Äî roughly 50√ó stronger than octahedral $d‚Äìd$ transitions

This extraordinary intensity explains why cobalt is the most powerful ceramic colorant per unit concentration.

Cu2+ ‚Äî Turquoise and Egyptian Blue

Cu2+ ($d^9$, one electron ‚Äúhole‚Äù in the $d$-shell) has a single spin-allowed $d‚Äìd$ transition:

\[^2E_g \to\, ^2T_{2g} \quad (\text{octahedral}): \quad \Delta \approx 12,500\;\text{/cm} \quad (\lambda \approx 800\;\text{nm})\]

This band absorbs in the near-IR with a strong tail into the red region, transmitting blue-green light. In most alkaline glass compositions, Cu2+ adopts a distorted octahedral or square-planar coordination (Jahn‚ÄìTeller distortion, because $d^9$ in octahedral geometry has an orbitally degenerate ground state that spontaneously distorts).

üü§ Egyptian Blue ‚Äî Humanity's First Synthetic Pigment
Egyptian Blue (CaCuSi2O6, the mineral cuprorivaite) was synthesised in Egypt from approximately 3100 BCE by heating together:
  • Silica sand (SiO2)
  • Limestone (CaCO3)
  • Copper ore (malachite, Cu2CO3(OH)2)
  • Natron flux (Na2CO3)
at ‚àº900 ¬∞C. The resulting crystalline phase contains Cu2+ in square-planar coordination within silicate sheets, producing a vivid sky-blue color. This is Cu2+ at its most intense ‚Äî the square-planar crystal field produces a larger splitting than octahedral, shifting the absorption deeper into the near-IR and leaving a purer blue transmission.

Egyptian Blue also exhibits a remarkable property discovered only in 2009: near-infrared luminescence. When illuminated with visible light, it emits at ‚àº910 nm ‚Äî useful for art authentication and potentially for photovoltaic down-conversion.

Cu+ ‚Äî The Invisible Intermediate

Cu+ ($d^{10}$) has a completely filled $d$-shell ‚Äî no $d‚Äìd$ transitions are possible. It is therefore colorless in glass and acts as a ‚Äúreservoir‚Äù species for the Cu$^{2+} \rightleftharpoons$ Cu$^+ \rightleftharpoons$ Cu0 redox cascade.

Cu+ can produce color indirectly:

  • As the precursor to Cu0 nanoparticle nucleation (copper red)
  • Through Cu+‚ÄìCu2+ charge transfer at high concentrations (brown)
  • As CuCl (nantokite) in lead glasses, producing the distinctive ‚Äúdichroic‚Äù effect in Lycurgus Cup-type glasses

Cu0 ‚Äî Copper Red (Surface Plasmon Resonance)

üî¨ The Physics of Copper Red
Copper red is fundamentally different from all other ceramic colors. It does not arise from $d‚Äìd$ crystal field transitions or charge transfer. Instead, it arises from the interaction of light with metallic copper nanoparticles (2‚Äì20 nm diameter) suspended in the glass matrix.

When a photon encounters a metallic nanoparticle smaller than its wavelength, the oscillating electric field of the light wave drives the conduction electrons of the particle into collective oscillation. At a specific frequency ‚Äî the surface plasmon resonance (SPR) frequency ‚Äî this oscillation is resonant, and the particle absorbs and scatters light extremely strongly.

For copper nanoparticles in a glass matrix ($n \approx 1.5$), the SPR occurs at:

\[\lambda_{\text{SPR}} \approx 570\;\text{nm} \quad (\text{green-yellow})\]

The nanoparticles absorb green-yellow light, transmitting red. The result is the characteristic deep, blood-red color of copper red glazes.

Key dependencies:

  • Particle size: 1‚Äì5 nm ‚Üí pale pink (few atoms, weak SPR). 5‚Äì20 nm ‚Üí rich red (optimal SPR). >50 nm ‚Üí metallic/opaque (scattering dominates).
  • Particle density: Too few ‚Üí pale. Too many ‚Üí opaque ‚Äúliver‚Äù red/brown.
  • Matrix refractive index: Higher $n$ shifts SPR to longer wavelengths (redder red). Lead glasses ($n \approx 1.6$) produce deeper reds than borosilicate ($n \approx 1.47$).

Requirements for Successful Copper Red

  1. Strong reduction atmosphere: $\log p_{\text{O}_2} \lt -10$. The melt must reduce Cu$^{2+} \to$ Cu$^+ \to$ Cu0. This requires carbon monoxide or hydrogen in the kiln atmosphere.
  2. High optical basicity: $\Lambda \gt 0.58$. The base glass must be basic enough to thermodynamically favor reduction. High-alkali, high-calcia, low-alumina compositions work best.
  3. Tin as nucleating agent: 1‚Äì2% SnO2 is typically added. During reduction, Sn$^{4+} \to$ Sn2+ provides electrons for Cu2+ reduction and acts as a heterogeneous nucleation site for Cu0 particles.
  4. Controlled cooling: Too fast ‚Üí particles too small (pale pink). Too slow ‚Üí particles too large (liver/metallic). A brief hold at ‚àº1000 ¬∞C during cooling promotes optimal particle growth.
  5. Low total copper: 0.3‚Äì1.5% CuO. Higher concentrations produce opaque ‚Äúliver‚Äù red or metallic effects.
  6. Moderate viscosity: $\log\eta = 3‚Äì5$ at peak temperature. Too fluid ‚Üí particles grow too large. Too viscous ‚Üí nucleation suppressed.
‚ö†Ô∏è Why Copper Red Fails
The most common failure modes:
  1. Insufficient reduction: Cu stays as Cu2+ (green) or Cu+ (colorless). The kiln never achieved the required $p_{\text{O}_2}$.
  2. Re-oxidation during cooling: Cu0 particles dissolve back to Cu2+ if oxygen leaks into the kiln during the critical $T_g$‚Äì1000 ¬∞C range.
  3. Over-reduction: Excessive carbon atmosphere reduces Cu0 particles so aggressively that they grow too large, producing a muddy gray-brown metallic surface.
  4. Wrong base composition: $\Lambda$ too low (acidic glass) thermodynamically prevents Cu0 formation. High-alumina glazes are particularly resistant to copper red.

Manganese ‚Äî Purple, Brown, and Black

Manganese is the third most commonly used ceramic colorant after iron and cobalt. Its color chemistry is complex because it has three accessible oxidation states in glass, each with different spectral properties.

Mn2+ ‚Äî The Phantom Colorant

Mn2+ ($d^5$, high-spin) is isoelectronic with Fe3+: exactly five $d$-electrons, one in each orbital. Every $d‚Äìd$ transition is spin-forbidden. The resulting bands are extraordinarily weak ‚Äî roughly 100√ó weaker than a spin-allowed transition. At low concentrations, Mn2+ is essentially invisible.

At high concentrations ($\gt 5$%), a very pale pink appears from the cumulative effect of multiple weak spin-forbidden bands clustered around $21,000‚Äì25,000$ /cm ($\lambda \approx 400‚Äì480$ nm). This pink is familiar from rhodonite (MnSiO3) and rhodochrosite (MnCO3) minerals.

Mn3+ ‚Äî The Purple Maker

Mn3+ ($d^4$) is the species responsible for manganese purple in glass. It has a spin-allowed transition:

\[^5E_g \to\, ^5T_{2g}: \quad \Delta \approx 20,000\;\text{/cm} \quad (\lambda \approx 500\;\text{nm})\]

This absorbs green-yellow light, producing a deep purple to amethyst color. The transition is further enhanced by a Jahn‚ÄìTeller distortion: $d^4$ in octahedral geometry is orbitally degenerate and spontaneously elongates two axial bonds, splitting the single $d‚Äìd$ band into two components. This broadens the absorption, deepening the color.

The ‚Äúmanganese purple‚Äù of medieval and Renaissance European glass ‚Äî the deep violet-purple seen in cathedral windows ‚Äî is Mn3+. It requires an oxidising atmosphere to maintain the 3+ state, which is why these glasses were fired in wood-burning furnaces with good air supply.

Mn4+ ‚Äî Dark Brown and Black

At very high oxidation potentials, Mn can reach the 4+ state ($d^3$), which is isoelectronic with Cr3+. Mn4+ produces intense dark brown to black colors through strong, broad absorption bands. In practice, Mn4+ is rare in glass because the oxidation potential required is extreme.

More commonly, the dark brown/black of high-manganese glazes arises from Mn3O4 (hausmannite) or MnO¬∑SiO2 crystallite precipitation during cooling, rather than dissolved Mn4+.

Chromium ‚Äî Green, Pink, and the Tin Connection

Chromium is a powerful but tricky colorant. In isolation, it produces green. Combined with tin, it produces pink (chrome-tin pink). At high concentrations or high temperatures, it can produce yellow or red through chromate formation. These wild color swings make chromium one of the most composition-sensitive colorants.

Electronic Structure

Cr3+ ‚Äî Chrome Green

Cr3+ ($d^3$) has a particularly stable electronic configuration: three electrons occupy the three $t_{2g}$ orbitals, one each, with all spins parallel. This half-filled $t_{2g}$ set is very stable, and Cr3+ has an exceptionally strong preference for octahedral coordination (the highest crystal field stabilisation energy of any common $3d$ ion: CFSE $= 1.2\,\Delta_{\text{oct}}$).

Two spin-allowed absorption bands:

\[\begin{aligned} ^4A_{2g} \to\, ^4T_{2g}: &\quad \Delta \approx 17,400\;\text{/cm} \quad (\lambda \approx 575\;\text{nm}) \quad \text{[absorbs yellow-orange]} \\ ^4A_{2g} \to\, ^4T_{1g}: &\quad \nu \approx 24,700\;\text{/cm} \quad (\lambda \approx 405\;\text{nm}) \quad \text{[absorbs violet]} \end{aligned}\]

With both yellow-orange and violet absorbed, the transmitted light is green ‚Äî specifically, a rich, slightly blue-green (unlike copper green, which is more yellow-green).

Cr3+ green is extremely stable: It does not change with atmosphere (Cr3+ is the stable state in most glass compositions), varies only slightly with base composition, and is unaffected by the presence of most other colorants.

The green of chromium in glass is closely related to the green of emerald (Cr3+ in beryl, Be3Al2Si6O18) and the red of ruby (Cr3+ in corundum, Al2O3). The difference is in $\Delta$: the stronger alumina crystal field in ruby pushes $\Delta$ higher, shifting the absorption into the blue-green and transmitting red. In glass (weaker field), $\Delta$ is lower, and the color is green.

Chrome-Tin Pink (Sphene)

üî¨ The Chrome-Tin Pink Mechanism
When Cr2O3 is added to a glaze containing SnO2 (tin oxide), the glaze turns pink instead of green. This transformation is one of the most commercially important color effects in ceramics and has been used since the 18th century (S√®vres, Meissen).

The mechanism:

  1. During firing, Cr3+ ions substitute for Sn4+ (or Ti4+) in the cassiterite (SnO2) or sphene (CaTiSiO5) crystal lattice. The substitution is possible because Cr3+ (0.62 √Ö) and Sn4+ (0.69 √Ö) have similar ionic radii.
  2. Inside the SnO2 lattice, the Cr3+ ion experiences a stronger crystal field than it would in the glass matrix. The octahedral site in SnO2 has shorter Cr‚ÄìO bond lengths and stronger covalent overlap.
  3. The stronger field increases $\Delta$ to ‚àº18,000‚Äì20,000 /cm, shifting the first absorption band from yellow-orange toward green-yellow. The transmitted color shifts from green toward pink/rose.

The pink color is therefore not from a different oxidation state of chromium ‚Äî it is the same Cr3+ ion in a different crystal field environment.

Requirements for chrome-tin pink:

  • Cr2O3: 0.1‚Äì0.5% (trace amounts; more ‚Üí too dark, may go green)
  • SnO2: 3‚Äì8% (major component; need excess Sn for Cr incorporation)
  • No zinc! ZnO destroys chrome-tin pink by preferentially incorporating into the SnO2 lattice, preventing Cr3+ substitution. This is one of the most important incompatibilities in ceramic colorant chemistry.
  • CaO: Beneficial (promotes sphene formation)
  • Low temperature preferred (cone 6 or below; at high temperatures, Cr3+ dissolves out of the crystal into the glass, reverting to green)

Cr6+ ‚Äî Chrome Yellow

At very high temperatures or in strongly alkaline compositions ($\Lambda \gt 0.65$), Cr3+ can be oxidised to Cr6+, which forms the tetrahedral chromate ion CrO42-. Cr6+ has $d^0$ configuration (no $d$-electrons), so there are no $d‚Äìd$ transitions. The intense yellow-orange color comes from a ligand-to-metal charge transfer band:

\[\text{O}^{2-}(2p) \to \text{Cr}^{6+}(3d^0): \quad \lambda_{\text{max}} \approx 370\;\text{nm}\]

This produces the familiar ‚Äúchrome yellow‚Äù color. However:

‚ö†Ô∏è Cr6+ Toxicity
Hexavalent chromium (Cr6+) is a known human carcinogen (IARC Group 1). Its formation in ceramic glazes is a serious health and safety concern. Cr6+ is water-soluble and highly bioavailable, unlike Cr3+ which is essentially non-toxic.

To prevent Cr6+ formation:

  • Keep $\Lambda$ below 0.60 (avoid strongly basic compositions)
  • Do not fire above cone 8 with chrome
  • Avoid combining chrome with barium or lead (promote Cr6+ stability)

Nickel ‚Äî The Composition Chameleon

Nickel is unusual among ceramic colorants because its color is extremely sensitive to base glass composition. The same NiO addition can produce brown, green, yellow, blue, gray, or purple depending on the surrounding oxide environment. This makes nickel both fascinating and frustrating for the potter.

Electronic Structure

Ni2+ ($d^8$) is the only common oxidation state in ceramic glasses. It has three spin-allowed transitions in octahedral coordination:

Because nickel is so composition-sensitive, it can be used as a diagnostic colorant: adding 1% NiO to an unknown base glaze and observing the fired color provides information about the coordination environment and the crystal field strength of the glass.   • Brown stoneware: 2‚Äì3% NiO in a calcia-based stoneware glaze produces a warm, earthy brown.
  • Crystal glazes: NiO promotes nucleation of zinc silicate crystals, affecting crystal morphology without dramatically changing the matrix color.

Colorant Interactions & Incompatibilities

When two or more colorants are present simultaneously, the result is not simply the sum of their individual colors. Three types of interaction occur:

Additive (Non-Interacting)

The simplest case: two colorants absorb at different wavelengths without affecting each other's electronic structure. The resulting color is the subtractive mixture.

üìù Additive Example: Cobalt + Iron
  • Co2+ absorbs at ‚àº600‚Äì670 nm (red-orange)
  • Fe3+ absorbs at ‚àº400‚Äì450 nm (violet-blue)
  • Both absorptions occur independently
  • Result: the glass transmits only the narrow green window between the two bands ‚Üí deep olive-blue or slate

Structural Incompatibilities

Some colorants compete for the same structural sites:

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Incompatibilities
  1. Chrome + Tin in the presence of Zinc: As discussed in the chromium section, ZnO prevents the Cr3+ substitution into SnO2 that produces chrome-tin pink. Adding even 2% ZnO can destroy the pink and produce a muddy olive-brown.
  2. Chrome + Tin at high temperature (> cone 8): The SnO2 crystal lattice dissolves at high temperature, releasing Cr3+ back into the glass where it reverts to green. Chrome-tin pink is strictly a low-to-mid-fire effect.
  3. Cobalt + high ZnO: The Co2+ color shifts from blue toward green-blue (turquoise) as ZnO increases, because Zn2+ modifies the local coordination environment.
  4. Copper red + any oxidation: Even trace oxygen leaks during cooling destroy Cu0 nanoparticles, reverting the color to green or colorless.

üî¨ The Depth of Color
Every color in a ceramic glaze is ultimately a story about electrons. An electron absorbs a photon, jumps to a higher energy level, and the missing photon leaves a gap in the spectrum that the eye perceives as color. Crystal field theory gives us the vocabulary to tell that story precisely ‚Äî to predict which photons will be absorbed, how strongly, and how the surrounding glass network modulates the energies.

The Stull Atlas, through its composition-dependent models of optical basicity, NBO/T, viscosity, and refractive index, provides the quantitative bridge between the potter's recipe and the physics of light absorption. Understanding that bridge transforms color from a mystery into a science ‚Äî though, mercifully, never completely: the kiln always has the last word.
