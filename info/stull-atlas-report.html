Abstract

For over a century, ceramic artists and technicians have relied on intuition, experience, and isolated test tiles to navigate the vast compositional space of glaze chemistry. The Stull Atlas Explorer changes this by placing 133 material-science properties—from thermal expansion and viscosity to glass network topology and food-safety risk—at a potter's fingertips, computed in real time from nothing more than a glaze's oxide recipe. Every equation is grounded in peer-reviewed glass science (120+ references, 1912–2018), yet the interface is designed so that a working potter with no formal chemistry training can ask “Will this glaze craze on my clay body?” and receive an immediate, quantitative answer rendered as a 3D landscape. This document presents the complete mathematical, architectural, and visual specification of the system.

What Is the Stull Atlas Explorer?

The Problem It Solves

Ceramic glaze development has always been an exercise in navigating complexity. A typical stoneware glaze contains 6–10 oxides whose concentrations interact non-linearly to produce thermal expansion, viscosity, color, surface texture, chemical durability, and dozens of other properties simultaneously. Change one oxide by a few tenths of a mole and the glaze may shift from glossy to matte, from food-safe to leaching, from perfectly fitted to crazed.

Traditionally, potters cope with this complexity through:

  1. Empirical testing — firing hundreds of test tiles, each varying one or two oxides, at enormous cost in time, materials, and kiln energy.
  2. Published limit charts — flat 2D diagrams (Stull 1912, Hesselberth & Roy 2002) that show safe compositional zones but reveal nothing about why a composition works or what properties it will have.
  3. Intuition and lore — rules of thumb passed between potters (“keep your alkali below 0.3”) that are helpful but incomplete, sometimes contradictory, and never quantitative.

The result is that most potters operate in a tiny corner of the available compositional space, repeating proven recipes with minor variations, never seeing the landscape of possibilities that stretches in every direction around them.

What It Is

The Stull Atlas Explorer is a real-time 3D computational glaze laboratory that runs entirely in the browser. It takes the classic Stull chart—a 2D plot of SiO2 (x-axis) vs Al2O3 (y-axis) at unity flux, first published by R.T. Stull in 1912—and lifts it into three dimensions by mapping any of 133 computed material properties onto the vertical Z-axis.

The user sees a living topographic landscape where peaks and valleys represent the variation of a chosen property (viscosity, thermal expansion, food safety, color prediction, mechanical strength…) across compositional space. Thousands of real glaze recipes from public databases are plotted as data points on this surface. The user can:

  • Rotate, zoom, and fly through the 3D property surface in real time.
  • Switch the Z-axis instantly to see the same glazes through 133 different scientific lenses.
  • Hover over any glaze to see its full oxide breakdown and computed properties.
  • Click two glazes to compute oxide-by-oxide deltas (what's different?).
  • Walk a path between two compositions, watching every property change smoothly along the way.
  • Blend two glazes virtually at any ratio and see all 133 properties of the result.
  • Layer four properties simultaneously as concentric info-rings around each data point.

Every calculation is performed client-side, from first principles, using the Unity Molecular Formula. There is no server, no database lookup, no machine-learning black box. The equations come directly from peer-reviewed glass science—Appen, Giordano, Makishima, Duffy, Shannon, Fresnel, Phillips, Turnbull—and every result can be traced back to a published reference.

How It Helps

The fundamental value proposition is this: the Stull Atlas collapses the feedback loop from weeks to seconds.

In a traditional workflow, a potter formulates a recipe on paper, mixes materials, makes test tiles, loads a kiln, fires for 12–24 hours, cools for another 12–24 hours, evaluates the results, and repeats. Each iteration costs days and significant material expense. Most potters can afford perhaps 50–100 test tiles per firing, exploring only a thin slice of the available space.

With the Stull Atlas, that same potter can:

  1. Survey thousands of compositions across a 3D landscape in seconds.
  2. Identify the region where thermal expansion, food safety, surface quality, and aesthetic goals overlap.
  3. Walk a path from a known working recipe toward a desired target, watching every property change.
  4. Narrow from thousands of possibilities to 3–5 strong candidates, then fire only those.

The atlas does not replace test-firing—no computation can perfectly predict the chaos of a kiln atmosphere, local raw-material variations, or the subtle magic of crystal nucleation. But it dramatically reduces the number of firings needed to reach a working glaze, and it reveals options that would never be found by trial and error alone.

⚠️ A Note on Limitations
All computed values are estimates based on additive models and empirical correlations from the glass-science literature. Real-world glazes are affected by:
  • Raw material impurities and batch-to-batch variation
  • Kiln atmosphere (oxidation/reduction), firing schedule, and heating rate
  • Clay body interaction, glaze thickness, and application method
  • Phase separation, crystallisation kinetics, and surface effects not captured by equilibrium models
The atlas provides the best available compositional guidance—a map of the territory—but the kiln remains the final arbiter.

Core Ratios & Structural Metrics (11 axes)

⚡ Flux Ratio
\[ R_{\text{flux}} = \frac{\sum \text{fluxes}}{\SiO + \AlO + \BO + \sum \text{fluxes}} \]
Explanation:

The flux ratio is a first-principles measure of the balance between order and disorder in a glass melt. In any silicate glass, the network-forming oxides (SiO2, Al2O3, B2O3) build the continuous three-dimensional tetrahedral framework that gives glass its rigid, amorphous structure. The network-modifying oxides—the fluxes (Na2O, K2O, CaO, MgO, etc.)—break apart this framework by introducing non-bridging oxygens, thereby lowering the temperature required for the glass to flow.

The flux ratio captures this tug-of-war in a single number. A composition with $R_{\text{flux}} = 0.50$ is half flux, half former by molar proportion; its melting behaviour will be aggressive, and it will flow freely at modest temperatures. A composition with $R_{\text{flux}} = 0.15$ is overwhelmingly former-dominated; it may not fully melt even at cone 10.

Practical interpretation:

  • $R_{\text{flux}} \lt 0.20$: Refractory. The glaze may appear dry, rough, or underfired. Likely to produce high viscosity and poor healing of surface defects.
  • $R_{\text{flux}} \in [0.20, 0.35]$: The working range for most functional stoneware glazes. Sufficient flux to mature at the target cone, but enough network formers for chemical durability.
  • $R_{\text{flux}} \gt 0.35$: Aggressively fluid. The glaze will run easily, may pool at the foot, and is prone to crazing due to the high expansion coefficients of most fluxes.

Because the flux ratio treats all fluxes equivalently by molar amount, it provides a rapid screening metric. It should always be interpreted alongside more specific indicators such as the $\text{R}_2\text{O}:\text{RO}$ ratio and the Shannon entropy of flux distribution, which reveal which fluxes are present and how uniformly they are distributed.

⚡ $\SiO:\AlO$ Ratio
\[ r = \frac{[\SiO]}{[\AlO]} \]
Explanation:

The silica-to-alumina ratio is arguably the single most important number in glaze formulation. It was one of the first relationships identified by R.T. Stull in 1912 when he systematically mapped glaze surfaces across the SiO2–Al2O3 plane, and it remains the primary predictor of surface quality over a century later.

Physically, SiO2 forms the glass network through corner-sharing $[\text{SiO}_4]^{4-}$ tetrahedra, while Al2O3 enters the network in tetrahedral coordination $[\text{AlO}_4]^{5-}$ when charge-balanced by alkali or alkaline-earth cations. The crucial difference is that alumina tetrahedra increase the viscosity and surface tension of the melt far more dramatically than silica units. When the Si:Al ratio drops below about 5:1, the melt becomes so viscous and its surface tension so high that it can no longer flow out to a smooth surface during cooling—producing the matte or satin textures that characterize alumina-rich glazes.

Surface character ranges:

  • $r \gt 8$: Ultra-glossy, often glassy-clear. Risk of low alumina stability (poor fit, low durability).
  • $r \in [5, 8]$: Classic gloss. The most common range for functional stoneware and porcelain glazes.
  • $r \in [3, 5]$: Satin to semi-matte. Sought after for contemporary studio pottery.
  • $r \lt 3$: Dry, rough matte. Can be beautiful but may have durability issues if alkali is also high.

The ratio also influences crystallization: low Si:Al promotes mullite ($3\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 \cdot 2\text{SiO}_2$) formation, while high Si:Al favors cristobalite or tridymite. In the Stull Atlas, this ratio effectively defines the horizontal gradient of the 3D landscape—from the glossy, fluid right side to the stiff, matte left side.

⚡ Alumina Saturation Index (ASI)
\[ \text{ASI} = \frac{[\AlO]}{[\NaO] + [\KO] + [\LiO] + [\CaOx]} \]
Explanation:

The Alumina Saturation Index originates from igneous petrology, introduced by Shand (1927) to classify the chemistry of granitic rocks, and has been widely adopted in glass science and ceramics. Its power lies in a simple structural insight: aluminium in a silicate melt can only occupy a network-forming tetrahedral site ($[\text{AlO}_4]^{5-}$) if a monovalent or divalent cation is available nearby to charge-balance the extra negative charge. The ASI asks: is there enough alkali and calcium to accommodate all the alumina?

Classification:

  • $\text{ASI} \lt 1$: Peralkaline. Excess alkalis remain after all alumina is charge-balanced. These surplus alkalis act as aggressive network modifiers, breaking Si–O–Si bridges. The result is a fluid, glossy, low-viscosity melt that is prone to crazing and chemical leaching.
  • $\text{ASI} = 1$: Metaluminous. Perfect balance. All alkalis are consumed charge-balancing alumina, and no excess modifier disrupts the network. This is often the sweet spot for durable, well-fitted glazes.
  • $\text{ASI} \gt 1$: Peraluminous. More alumina than can be accommodated in tetrahedral sites by the available alkalis. The excess alumina enters octahedral coordination ($[\text{AlO}_6]$), dramatically increasing melt viscosity and promoting matte surfaces. Taken to extremes, the glaze will not fully mature.

In practice, most functional stoneware glazes have $\text{ASI} \approx 0.5–0.9$. Values near the metaluminous boundary are desirable for dinnerware because they balance surface quality with chemical durability. The ASI is particularly diagnostic when combined with the Si:Al ratio: a peralkaline glaze with high Si:Al will be glossy but craze-prone; a peraluminous glaze with low Si:Al will be matte and stiff.

⚡ Combined Alkali
\[ \text{CA} = [\LiO] + [\NaO] + [\KO] \]
Explanation:

Combined alkali is the simplest and one of the most universally applied screening metrics in ceramic glaze formulation. The three alkali oxides—lithia (Li2O), soda (Na2O), and potash (K2O)—share a common structural role: each introduces a monovalent cation ($\text{Li}^+$, $\text{Na}^+$, $\text{K}^+$) that breaks a bridging oxygen bond in the silicate network, creating a non-bridging oxygen (NBO). The result is dramatically lowered viscosity and dramatically raised thermal expansion.

The thermal expansion effect is the key concern. Alkali ions occupy large interstitial sites in the glass network and vibrate with high amplitude as temperature increases. Of all common glass-forming oxides, Na2O and K2O have the highest Appen expansion factors ($\alpha_{\text{Na}_2\text{O}} \approx 39.5$, $\alpha_{\text{K}_2\text{O}} \approx 28.3$ in units of $10^{-7}\;\text{/°C}$). When the combined alkali content exceeds about 0.3 moles (at unity flux), the glaze's coefficient of thermal expansion almost inevitably exceeds that of stoneware clay bodies ($\alpha \approx 55 \times 10^{-7}$), placing the glaze in tension on cooling and producing crazing.

Practical thresholds:

  • $\text{CA} \lt 0.20$: Low alkali. Potentially under-fluxed unless compensated by alkaline earths.
  • $\text{CA} \in [0.20, 0.30]$: Moderate. Typical for well-fitted stoneware glazes.
  • $\text{CA} \gt 0.30$: High risk of crazing. Requires significant alkaline-earth compensation or a high-expansion clay body.

Note that Li2O is a partial exception: despite being an alkali, lithia has a low expansion factor ($\alpha_{\text{Li}_2\text{O}} \approx 10$) because the $\text{Li}^+$ ion is so small that it fits into the interstices of the glass network with minimal disruption. Substituting lithia for soda or potash is a classic strategy for reducing thermal expansion while maintaining flux activity.

⚡ $\text{R}_2\text{O}:\text{RO}$ Ratio
\[ \frac{\text{R}_2\text{O}}{\text{RO}} = \frac{[\LiO] + [\NaO] + [\KO]}{[\CaOx] + [\MgOx] + [\SrOx] + [\BaOx] + [\ZnOx]} \]
Explanation:

This ratio captures one of the most fundamental divisions in glaze chemistry: the character of the fluxing system. All fluxes lower the melting point of the silicate glass, but they do so through distinctly different mechanisms and with profoundly different consequences.

Alkali fluxes ($\text{R}_2\text{O}$): The monovalent alkalis—Li2O, Na2O, K2O—are the most powerful per-mole fluxes. Each mole provides two network-modifying cations that aggressively break Si–O–Si bridges. They produce fluid, low-viscosity melts with glossy surfaces, but at the cost of high thermal expansion, poor chemical durability, and susceptibility to crazing.

Alkaline earth fluxes (RO): The divalent alkaline earths—CaO, MgO, SrO, BaO, ZnO—are less aggressive fluxes per mole because each divalent cation can bridge between two non-bridging oxygens, partially cross-linking the network. They produce stiffer, more viscous melts with lower thermal expansion, better durability, and a tendency toward satin or matte surfaces.

Interpretation:

  • $\text{R}_2\text{O}:\text{RO} \gt 1.5$: Alkali-dominant. Brilliant gloss but craze-prone and potentially leachable.
  • $\text{R}_2\text{O}:\text{RO} \approx 0.5–1.0$: Balanced. The sweet spot for functional stoneware—sufficient flux for a mature melt with adequate durability.
  • $\text{R}_2\text{O}:\text{RO} \lt 0.3$: Alkaline-earth dominant. Matte or satin surfaces, excellent durability, but may be under-fluxed at lower firing temperatures.

Historically, the shift from alkali-dominant to alkaline-earth-dominant glazes tracks the evolution from low-fire earthenware (heavily reliant on soda and lead) to modern high-fire stoneware and porcelain (dominated by calcium and magnesium from whiting and dolomite). Understanding this ratio gives the potter a quick read on where a glaze sits along that evolutionary spectrum.

Additional ratios include: CaO:MgO (gloss vs. matte balance), Na2O:K2O (soda vs. potash; 50:50 maximises the mixed alkali effect), Al2O3/Flux (melt stiffness), Molar Mass ($\bar{M} = \sum n_i M_i / \sum n_i$), and Limit Conformance (distance from Hesselberth/Stull limits, 100 = within, 0 = far outside).

Thermal Expansion & Glaze–Body Fit (6 axes)

This is the most practically important group for functional pottery.

⚡ Coefficient of Thermal Expansion (COE) — Appen Additive Model
\[ \alpha = \sum_i x_i \,\alpha_i \qquad [\times 10^{-7}\;\text{/°C}] \]
where $x_i$ is the weight fraction and $\alpha_i$ is the Appen factor for oxide $i$.

Explanation:

The coefficient of thermal expansion (COE) is, without exaggeration, the most practically important calculation in the entire atlas. It answers the question that haunts every functional potter: will this glaze fit my clay body?

The Appen additive model, developed by A.A. Appen and published in Khimiya Stekla (Chemistry of Glass, 1961), is the workhorse of the glass industry. Its central insight is that each oxide contributes approximately independently to the thermal expansion of the glass. Each oxide species $i$ has a tabulated expansion factor $\alpha_i$ (measured in units of $10^{-7}\;\text{/°C}$), and the glass's overall COE is simply the weighted sum over all species.

The physics behind this additivity is rooted in the nature of thermal vibrations in an amorphous solid. Each cation sits in a potential well created by its surrounding oxygen coordination shell. As temperature rises, the cation oscillates with increasing amplitude. Large, weakly bonded cations like $\text{Na}^+$ and $\text{K}^+$ vibrate with enormous amplitude (high $\alpha_i$), while small, tightly bonded species like Si4+ barely move (low $\alpha_i$). The glass-wide expansion is the statistical average of all these individual oscillations.

⚡ Craze Risk
\[ \Delta\alpha = \alpha_{\text{glaze}} - \alpha_{\text{body\;limit}} \]
Explanation:

Craze risk is the simplest and most actionable output in the atlas. It reduces the entire thermal-fit question to a single signed number.

The physics is straightforward. When a glaze cools from the glass-transition temperature ($T_g$) to room temperature, both the glaze and the clay body contract. If the glaze contracts less than the body (because its COE is higher), the glaze is stretched taut across the body surface. Glass is strong in compression but weak in tension—it can only sustain tensile stress of about 50–100 MPa before cracks propagate. The fine network of cracks that appears is called crazing.

Conversely, if $\Delta\alpha$ is negative (the glaze contracts more than the body), the glaze is placed in compression. Mild compression ($-5$ to $-15$ units) is actually beneficial—it makes the ware mechanically stronger, much like tempered glass. Excessive compression ($\Delta\alpha \lt -20$) can cause the glaze to flake off the body, a defect called shivering or peeling.

Practical thresholds:

  • $\Delta\alpha \gt +5$: Crazing almost certain.
  • $\Delta\alpha \in [0, +5]$: Marginal—may craze under thermal shock (dishwasher, microwave).
  • $\Delta\alpha \in [-15, 0]$: Mild compression. Ideal range.
  • $\Delta\alpha \lt -20$: Risk of shivering.

Note that the atlas uses a default body limit derived from typical stoneware bodies. For the most accurate results, the user should enter their actual body COE, which can be measured with a dilatometer or estimated from the body's mineral composition.

⚡ Glaze Stress — Hooke's Law for Thin Films
\[ \boxed{\sigma = \frac{E \cdot \Delta\alpha \cdot (T_g - 25)}{1 - \nu}} \qquad [\text{MPa}] \]
where:
  • $E$ = Young's modulus of the glaze [GPa]
  • $\Delta\alpha = \alpha_{\text{glaze}} - \alpha_{\text{body}}$ [/°C]
  • $T_g$ = glass transition temperature [°C]
  • $\nu$ = Poisson's ratio (dimensionless)

Explanation:

This is the professional-grade formula for calculating the actual mechanical stress developed in a glaze layer during cooling, and it represents one of the most sophisticated computations in the atlas because it is fully recursive—it depends on four other properties ($E$, $\Delta\alpha$, $T_g$, $\nu$), each of which is itself computed from the oxide composition through separate models.

The equation is a specialised form of Hooke's law for a thin elastic film bonded to a rigid substrate. Robert Hooke (1676) established the proportionality between stress and strain in elastic materials ($\sigma = E \cdot \varepsilon$), and this has been extended to the biaxial stress state of a constrained thin layer by dividing by $(1 - \nu)$ to account for the Poisson effect.

The thermal strain is $\varepsilon = \Delta\alpha \cdot (T_g - 25)$, representing the differential contraction between glaze and body as the system cools from the glass transition temperature (where stress begins to accumulate) to room temperature.

Variable-by-variable:

  • $E$ (Young's modulus): Stiffness of the glaze. A stiffer glaze develops more stress for the same strain. Computed via Makishima–Mackenzie additive model. Typical: 50–90 GPa.
  • $\Delta\alpha$: The COE mismatch. Even a small mismatch of $5 \times 10^{-7}\;\text{/°C}$ can produce significant stress when multiplied across the large temperature range.
  • $T_g$: The glass transition temperature. Stress only accumulates below $T_g$; above it, the glass can flow and relax. Higher $T_g$ means stress accumulates over a larger temperature interval, increasing $\sigma$.
  • $\nu$ (Poisson's ratio): Typically 0.17–0.27 for oxide glasses. The $(1-\nu)$ denominator amplifies the stress by about 20–30%.

Sign convention:

  • $\sigma \gt 0$: Tensile stress (crazing). The glaze is being pulled apart.
  • $\sigma \lt 0$: Compressive stress. The glaze is being squeezed. Mild compression ($-20$ to $-100$ MPa) strengthens the ware.
  • $|\sigma| \gt 150$ MPa: Extreme stress. Failure (crazing or shivering) is virtually guaranteed.

The power of this equation is that it converts an abstract concept (“COE mismatch”) into a physically meaningful number with real units (MPa) that can be compared against the known tensile strength of glass (∼50–100 MPa).

⚡ Stress Ratio
\[ R_{\sigma} = \frac{|\sigma|}{H/3 \times 1000} \]
Explanation:

While the glaze stress equation (Eq. [eq:glaze_stress]) tells us the magnitude of stress in megapascals, the stress ratio answers a more practical question: does this stress exceed what the glass can actually withstand?

The denominator uses the Vickers microhardness $H$ (in GPa) divided by 3 as a proxy for the tensile strength of the glass. This empirical relationship arises from contact mechanics: Lawn & Marshall (1979) showed that for brittle materials, the critical stress for crack propagation scales roughly as $H/3$. By normalising the computed stress against this threshold, we get a dimensionless ratio with clear physical meaning.

Interpretation:

  • $R_\sigma \lt 0.3$: Safe. The stress is well within the glass network's capacity to resist fracture. No crazing expected under any normal conditions.
  • $R_\sigma \in [0.3, 0.7]$: Marginal. The glaze may survive under gentle use but could craze under thermal shock (microwave, dishwasher, pouring boiling water).
  • $R_\sigma \in [0.7, 1.0]$: High risk. Crazing is likely, especially with aging—glass continues to relax and reorganize over months, and marginal glazes often craze weeks or months after firing.
  • $R_\sigma \gt 1.0$: Guaranteed failure. The stress exceeds the network's breaking strength. Crazing will appear immediately or within days.

The stress ratio is particularly valuable because it automatically accounts for the glaze's composition-dependent hardness. A soft, low-hardness glaze (high alkali, low alumina) will show a higher stress ratio for the same absolute stress than a hard, densely cross-linked glaze.

⚡ Thermal Shock Resistance
\[ R_{\text{shock}} = \frac{H}{E \cdot \alpha} \]
Explanation:

Thermal shock resistance quantifies a material's ability to survive rapid temperature changes without fracturing. This is directly relevant to functional pottery: a mug that shatters when filled with boiling water, a baking dish that cracks in the oven, or a kiln shelf that fractures from fast cooling—all are failures of thermal shock resistance.

The parameter $R_{\text{shock}} = H / (E \cdot \alpha)$ comes from Hasselman's landmark 1969 framework for thermal shock in brittle materials. Hasselman showed that the critical temperature difference a material can withstand before crack initiation is proportional to its strength (here approximated by hardness $H$) and inversely proportional to its stiffness ($E$) and expansion ($\alpha$).

The physical intuition is elegant:

  • High hardness $H$: The material can bear more stress before cracking. Increases $R_{\text{shock}}$.
  • Low modulus $E$: A more compliant material develops less stress for a given strain. Increases $R_{\text{shock}}$.
  • Low expansion $\alpha$: Less thermal strain per degree of temperature change. Increases $R_{\text{shock}}$.

Practical implications:

  • High-alumina, low-alkali glazes have the best thermal shock resistance because alumina simultaneously increases hardness, reduces expansion, and moderates stiffness.
  • Lithium glazes (petalite, spodumene) offer excellent thermal shock resistance because Li2O has an anomalously low expansion factor.
  • High-soda glazes have the worst thermal shock resistance: high expansion, modest hardness, moderate stiffness—all working against them.

Dinnerware manufacturers typically require $R_{\text{shock}}$ above specific thresholds to pass ASTM C554 (Thermal Shock Resistance of Glass Containers) or equivalent standards.

Viscosity, Flow & Landmarks (9 axes)

All properties in this group are powered by the Vogel–Fulcher–Tammann (VFT) viscosity model.

⚡ VFT Viscosity Model — The Computational Heart
\[ \boxed{\log_{10}(\eta) = A + \frac{B}{T - C}} \]
where $\eta$ is dynamic viscosity in Pas, $T$ is temperature in °C, and:
\[\begin{aligned} A &= -4.55 \\[4pt] B &= 5000 + 8000\,[\SiO]\% + 12000\,[\AlO]\% - 3000\,[\BO]\% \\ &\quad - 2000\,[\text{alkali}]\% - 1500\,[\text{AE}]\% \\[4pt] C &= 500 + 100\,[\SiO]\% + 150\,[\AlO]\% - 200\,[\text{alkali}]\% \end{aligned}\]

Explanation:

The VFT equation is the single most important model in the entire atlas. It feeds nine separate properties and provides the computational backbone for viscosity-dependent predictions including flow behaviour, defect healing, and crystallisation kinetics.

The equation has a remarkable history. It was independently derived by three scientists working in three countries within a span of four years: Hans Vogel (1921, Germany), Gordon Fulcher (1925, United States), and Gustav Tammann with Walter Hesse (1926, Germany). All three arrived at the same functional form while studying the viscosity of supercooled liquids—a class of materials that includes glass-forming silicate melts.

The key insight that distinguishes VFT from a simple Arrhenius model ($\log \eta = A + B/T$) is the parameter $C$. In the Arrhenius form, viscosity approaches infinity only at absolute zero. But supercooled liquids don't behave this way: their viscosity rises far more steeply than an Arrhenius curve predicts as temperature drops toward $T_g$. The VFT equation handles this by introducing a divergence temperature $C$ at which viscosity would theoretically become infinite. In practice, the glass transitions to a solid before reaching $C$, but the mathematical divergence captures the real physical phenomenon of cooperative molecular rearrangement—molecules in a supercooled liquid don't just hop individually; they must rearrange in cooperative groups whose size grows as temperature decreases.

The composition-dependent coefficients $B$ and $C$ encode how the glass's oxide chemistry controls its flow behaviour:

  • SiO2 and Al2O3 increase $B$ (activation energy): These network-formers build the tetrahedral framework that must be broken for flow. More formers = more framework = harder to flow.
  • B2O3 decreases $B$: Boron in trigonal coordination ($\text{BO}_3$) is a weak link in the network, readily enabling rearrangement.
  • Alkalis (Na2O, K2O, Li2O) decrease both $B$ and $C$: They break network bonds (reducing $B$) and increase configurational freedom (reducing $C$).
  • Alkaline earths decrease $B$ moderately: They act as modifiers but less aggressively than alkalis.

The composition coefficients used here follow Giordano et al. (2008), who fitted VFT parameters to an extensive dataset of measured viscosities for natural silicate melts. The model is most accurate for aluminosilicate compositions in the range typical of ceramic glazes ($\SiO\ 55–75\%$, $\AlO\ 5–25\%$, fluxes $10–30\%$).

VFT Temperature Landmarks

By inverting Eq. [eq:vft], specific temperatures can be found for standard viscosity values:

⚡ Temperature Inversion
\[ T = C + \frac{B}{\log_{10}(\eta) - A} \]
Explanation:

The VFT inversion is the algebraic rearrangement of Eq. [eq:vft], solved for temperature instead of viscosity. This is how the atlas computes the four ASTM-standard viscosity landmark temperatures—each landmark is defined as the temperature at which viscosity reaches a specific $\log_{10}\eta$ value.

These landmarks are not arbitrary; they correspond to specific mechanical behaviours that have been standardised by the glass industry over decades:

  • Softening Point ($\log_{10}\eta = 6.6$, ASTM C338): Originally defined by Littleton (1931) as the temperature at which a glass fiber of uniform cross-section elongates under its own weight at a specified rate. In practical ceramics, this is roughly the temperature at which the glaze begins to deform visibly.
  • Flow Point ($\log_{10}\eta = 4.0$): The temperature at which the glass is sufficiently fluid to flow freely. Glazes need to reach at least this viscosity level to heal surface defects, release bubbles, and achieve a smooth surface.
  • Annealing Point ($\log_{10}\eta = 12.0$, ASTM C336): The temperature at which internal stresses relax within about 15 minutes. During cooling, if the kiln is held at this temperature, residual stresses from differential contraction will dissipate. Below this temperature, stresses begin to lock in permanently.
  • Strain Point ($\log_{10}\eta = 13.5$): The temperature below which the glass is effectively rigid. No further viscous relaxation occurs on any practical timescale. From this point down to room temperature, any differential contraction between glaze and body builds up as permanent elastic stress.

For the potter, the most critical of these is the annealing point. A controlled slow cool through the annealing-to-strain range allows the glaze to relax stresses that might otherwise cause delayed crazing. The wider the gap between the annealing point and the strain point (typically 30–80 °C), the more forgiving the glaze is during cooling.

⚡ Soak Benefit Index
\[ S = \left|\frac{B}{(T - C)^2}\right| \quad \text{scaled } 0\text{--}10 \]
Explanation:

The soak benefit index is a unique contribution of the Stull Atlas—it translates the mathematical derivative of the VFT curve into a practical kiln-operation recommendation.

Mathematically, $S$ is the absolute value of $\mathrm{d}(\log_{10}\eta)/\mathrm{d}T$ evaluated at the kiln's programmed firing temperature. This derivative measures the slope of the viscosity curve: how rapidly viscosity changes with temperature at that specific point.

The physical insight is that different compositions sit on different parts of their VFT curve at the same firing temperature. A composition with a steep VFT curve at cone 6 will experience a large viscosity reduction from a small temperature increase, meaning that holding at temperature (“soaking”) is extremely effective—the glaze continues to become more fluid, heal pinholes, release bubbles, and smooth its surface. Conversely, a composition with a flat VFT curve at the firing temperature is already near its minimum viscosity, and soaking provides little additional benefit.

Scale (0–10):

  • $S \lt 2$: Low soak benefit. The glaze has already reached its maximum fluidity. Extended holds waste energy without improving the surface.
  • $S \in [3, 6]$: Moderate. A 15–30 minute soak will noticeably improve surface quality.
  • $S \gt 7$: High soak benefit. The glaze is on a steep part of its viscosity curve. Holding for 30–60 minutes at peak temperature can transform a rough, pinholed surface into a smooth, healed one.

This index is especially valuable for glaze chemistries that are borderline in their maturity—compositions where the firing temperature is close to the softening point. In such cases, a long soak can be the difference between a successful firing and a ruined kiln load. The soak benefit index quantifies this in advance, allowing kiln schedules to be optimised for each specific glaze.

Glass Network Structure (13 axes)

⚡ Non-Bridging Oxygens per Tetrahedron (NBO/T)
\[ \frac{\text{NBO}}{T} = \frac{2\sum[\text{modifiers}] - [\AlO]}{[\SiO] + [\AlO]} \]
Explanation:

$\text{NBO}/T$ is widely regarded as the single most important structural parameter in glass science. It was formalised by Mysen, Virgo & Scarfe (1980) at the Geophysical Laboratory of the Carnegie Institution, and it has since become the universal language for describing glass network connectivity in research papers spanning geochemistry, materials science, and ceramics.

To understand NBO/T, start with the silicate glass network. Pure $\text{SiO}_2$ glass consists entirely of corner-sharing $[\text{SiO}_4]^{4-}$ tetrahedra. Every oxygen atom bridges two tetrahedra (a “bridging oxygen”, BO), creating a fully connected three-dimensional network. This gives $\text{NBO}/T = 0$—zero non-bridging oxygens per tetrahedron.

Now add a network modifier like $\text{Na}_2\text{O}$. Each formula unit of $\text{Na}_2\text{O}$ provides one additional oxygen atom plus two $\text{Na}^+$ cations. The extra oxygen breaks a Si–O–Si bridge, creating two non-bridging oxygens (NBOs) that are each bonded to only one silicon and charge-balanced by the sodium ions. The network is now partially “depolymerised”—some tetrahedra have dangling bonds instead of cross-links.

Alumina plays a special role. When $[\text{AlO}_4]^{5-}$ tetrahedra enter the network (charge-balanced by nearby alkali or alkaline-earth cations), they consume modifiers without creating NBOs. Each $\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3$ removes one modifier from NBO creation, which is why the numerator subtracts $[\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3]$.

Interpretation:

  • $\text{NBO}/T = 0$: Fully polymerised. Maximum viscosity, maximum durability, maximum chemical resistance. Pure silica glass or a perfectly charge-balanced aluminosilicate.
  • $\text{NBO}/T = 1$: Moderate depolymerisation. Typical of many functional stoneware glazes. Sheet and chain structures appear alongside the 3D network.
  • $\text{NBO}/T = 2$: Heavily depolymerised. The network consists mainly of chains and small clusters. Viscosity is low, and chemical durability is poor.
  • $\text{NBO}/T = 4$: Fully depolymerised. No continuous network remains—the melt is essentially an ionic liquid of isolated $[\text{SiO}_4]^{4-}$ tetrahedra surrounded by modifier cations.

In the Stull Atlas, NBO/T serves as a master predictor for dozens of downstream properties. High NBO/T correlates strongly with low viscosity, high thermal expansion, low chemical durability, low hardness, and high ionic conductivity. It is the structural explanation behind why high-flux glazes behave the way they do.

⚡ Optical Basicity ($\Lambda$) — Duffy & Ingram (1976)
\[ \Lambda = \sum_i x_i \,\Lambda_i \]
where $\Lambda_i$ is the tabulated optical basicity of oxide $i$.

Explanation:

Optical basicity is a concept developed by John A. Duffy and Michael D. Ingram at the University of Aberdeen in 1976. It provides a direct, quantitative measure of the electron-donating power of the oxide ions in a glass—in chemical terms, the Lewis basicity of the oxygen anions.

The fundamental idea is that different oxide environments donate electron density to different degrees. In a pure $\text{SiO}_2$ glass, the oxygens are tightly shared between two silicon atoms and have low electron-donating power (low basicity). Add $\text{Na}_2\text{O}$, and the non-bridging oxygens created by the sodium ions are much more electron-rich (high basicity) because they are bonded to only one silicon and electrostatically stabilised by the nearby Na+ ion.

Duffy and Ingram assigned each oxide a tabulated $\Lambda_i$ value (on a scale from 0 to approximately 1.2) based on spectroscopic measurements of probe ions (Pb2+, Tl+, Bi3+) dissolved in glasses of known composition. The overall optical basicity of a multi-component glass is the mole-fraction-weighted average of these contributions.

Why it matters for ceramics:

  • Iron redox equilibrium: Optical basicity is the primary compositional predictor of $\text{Fe}^{2+}/\text{Fe}^{3+}$ ratio in a glass melt. Higher basicity shifts the equilibrium toward $\text{Fe}^{2+}$ (blue-green in reduction) and away from $\text{Fe}^{3+}$ (amber in oxidation). This directly controls the color of iron-bearing glazes.
  • Sulfur solubility: Higher basicity increases the capacity of the melt to absorb sulfur, important for glasses made with sulfur-bearing raw materials.
  • Color intensity: Transition-metal colorants (Co, Cu, Mn, Cr) exhibit different crystal-field splitting energies depending on the electron density of the surrounding oxygens, shifting absorption bands and altering perceived color.
  • Corrosion resistance: High-basicity glasses dissolve more rapidly in water and acids because the electron-rich oxygens are more easily protonated.

⚡ Phillips–Thorpe Constraint Counting
\[ n_c = \sum_i f_i \left[\frac{r_i}{2} + (2r_i - 3)\right] \]
where $r_i$ is the coordination number and $f_i$ is the fraction of species $i$.

Explanation:

The Phillips–Thorpe constraint-counting model represents one of the most elegant applications of topology to materials science. J.C. Phillips introduced the central idea in 1979, and M.F. Thorpe developed the mathematical formalism in 1983. Together, they showed that the mechanical stability of a glass network can be predicted purely from counting the number of interatomic constraints (bond-stretching and bond-bending forces) per atom, without any knowledge of the specific arrangement of atoms.

The model begins by assigning each atomic species its coordination number $r_i$ (from spectroscopy or crystal-chemistry rules). For a species with $r_i$ nearest neighbours:

  • There are $r_i / 2$ bond-stretching constraints per atom (each bond contributes one stretch constraint, shared between two atoms).
  • There are $(2r_i - 3)$ bond-bending constraints per atom (the angular constraints that fix the geometry around the atom, minus the 3 rotational degrees of freedom of the coordination shell).

The total number of constraints per atom, $n_c$, is the weighted average over all species:

\[n_c = \sum_i f_i \left[\frac{r_i}{2} + (2r_i - 3)\right]\]

The critical insight is that each atom in 3D space has exactly 3 degrees of freedom (its $x,y,z$ position). When $n_c = 3$, every degree of freedom is exactly consumed by one constraint. This is the isostatic condition—the network is neither under-constrained (floppy) nor over-constrained (stressed-rigid).

Three regimes:

  • $n_c \lt 3$ (floppy): The network has more degrees of freedom than constraints. It can deform easily, has low viscosity near $T_g$, and tends toward poor mechanical properties. Extreme case: an ionic melt with no network.
  • $n_c = 3$ (isostatic): The network is optimally constrained. This is the sweet spot for glass formation—the structure is rigid enough to resist deformation but not so rigid that strain energy builds up, which would drive crystallisation. Many of the best glass-forming compositions fall near this point.
  • $n_c \gt 3$ (stressed-rigid): The network is over-constrained. Strain energy accumulates because not all constraints can be simultaneously satisfied in an amorphous arrangement. The glass becomes more brittle, and the driving force toward crystallisation increases.

For the potter, the Phillips–Thorpe constraint count is a hidden predictor of glaze quality. Compositions near $n_c = 3$ tend to form the most stable, uniform glasses—they resist crystallisation, anneal easily, and exhibit intermediate viscosity at the firing temperature.

⚡ $Q$-Species Distribution
\[\begin{aligned} Q^4 &= \text{fraction of fully cross-linked } \text{SiO}_4 \text{ tetrahedra} \\ Q^2 &= \text{fraction of chain-forming units} \end{aligned}\]
Explanation:

The $Q^n$ notation is the standard language of solid-state NMR spectroscopists and glass scientists for describing the local connectivity of silicate tetrahedra. It was popularised through the pioneering $^{29}\text{Si}$ magic-angle spinning (MAS) NMR work of Lippmaa, M\"agi, and colleagues in the early 1980s, who showed that each $Q$ species produces a distinct chemical shift, allowing direct measurement of the glass structure.

In this notation, the superscript $n$ denotes the number of bridging oxygens (BOs) around a silicon atom, ranging from 0 to 4:

  • $Q^4$: The silicon is bonded to 4 bridging oxygens—fully cross-linked into the 3D network. No non-bridging oxygens. This is the dominant species in pure $\text{SiO}_2$ glass and in silica-rich, low-flux glazes.
  • $Q^3$: Three bridging oxygens, one NBO. The silicon is part of a sheet-like structure (think of mica or clay minerals). Found in moderately modified glasses.
  • $Q^2$: Two bridging oxygens, two NBOs. The silicon is part of a chain structure (think of pyroxene minerals like wollastonite). High in calcium-rich glazes.
  • $Q^1$: One bridging oxygen, three NBOs. The silicon is at the end of a chain or part of a dimer. Only found in very heavily modified melts.
  • $Q^0$: No bridging oxygens. The silicon exists as an isolated $[\text{SiO}_4]^{4-}$ orthosilicate unit (think of olivine). The melt is essentially an ionic liquid.

The atlas estimates the $Q$-species distribution from the NBO/T value using the disproportionation model of Stebbins (1987). Higher modifier content systematically shifts the distribution from $Q^4$ dominance toward lower $Q$ species.

Practical significance:

  • Durability: High $Q^4$ fraction means a densely cross-linked network that resists chemical attack by water and acids.
  • Viscosity: $Q^4$-rich melts are extremely viscous; $Q^2$-rich melts flow freely.
  • Crystallisation: Certain crystal phases preferentially nucleate from specific $Q$ environments ($Q^2$ chains are precursors to wollastonite; $Q^3$ sheets to muscovite-like phases).

Additional network-structure properties:

  • Mean Coordination Number: The average number of nearest-neighbour atoms bonded to each atom in the glass network, weighted across all species. Pure SiO2 glass has a mean coordination near 2.67 (each Si bonds to 4 oxygens; each O to 2 silicons, giving $(4+2\times 2)/3 \approx 2.67$). Adding modifier cations (coordination 4–8) raises the mean toward 3.0 or higher. This parameter feeds directly into the Phillips–Thorpe constraint count.
  • Modifier Fraction: The molar ratio of network-modifying species to total species. A pure network-former glass (SiO2, Al2O3, B2O3 only) has modifier fraction 0; a composition consisting entirely of modifiers would have fraction 1 (but would not form a glass). Typical stoneware glazes fall in the 0.25–0.45 range. Above 0.50, the network is so disrupted that glass-forming ability and chemical durability decline sharply.
  • Boron $N_4$ Fraction: In borosilicate glasses, boron can exist in either trigonal ($\text{BO}_3$, 3-coordinated) or tetrahedral ($\text{BO}_4$, 4-coordinated) geometry. Dell & Bray (1983) showed that alkali additions convert $\text{BO}_3$ to $\text{BO}_4$ up to a saturation limit that depends on the $\text{R}_2\text{O}/\text{B}_2\text{O}_3$ ratio. The $N_4$ fraction matters because $\text{BO}_4$ tetrahedra cross-link the network (strengthening it), while $\text{BO}_3$ units are planar and relatively weak.
  • Fragility Index ($m$): Angell's (1995) fragility parameter quantifies how rapidly viscosity increases as the glass is cooled toward $T_g$. “Strong” glass-formers ($m \approx 20$, e.g. pure SiO2) follow near-Arrhenius behaviour; “fragile” glass-formers ($m \approx 80+$, e.g. CaO–MgO rich compositions) show super-Arrhenius behaviour with a sharp viscosity increase near $T_g$. Fragile melts are harder to anneal because the viscosity shoots up so quickly during cooling.
  • Packing Density: The fraction of the glass volume actually occupied by ions, computed from Shannon (1976) ionic radii and the Makishima–Mackenzie (1973) model. Typical oxide glasses have packing densities of 0.45–0.55—much lower than close-packed crystals (∼0.74)—reflecting the open, disordered nature of the glass network.
  • Reduced $T_g$ and Hrubý Parameter: Described in full in Section [sec:properties] under Glass-Forming Ability.
  • Critical Cooling Rate: The minimum cooling rate (in °C/h) required to prevent detectable crystallisation, estimated from the Cabral & Fokin (2004) correlation. A low critical cooling rate ($\lt 10$ °C/h) means the glass can be cooled slowly without devitrification; a high rate ($\gt 100$ °C/h) means rapid cooling is essential to avoid crystals.

Mechanical Properties (8 axes)

All mechanical properties are computed via the Makishima–Mackenzie additive framework (1973).

⚡ Young's Modulus
\[ E = \sum_i x_i \, E_i \qquad [\text{GPa}] \]
Explanation:

Young's modulus—named after Thomas Young, who described it in 1807—is the fundamental measure of the elastic stiffness of a material. It is defined as the ratio of uniaxial stress to the resulting uniaxial strain in the elastic (recoverable) regime: $E = \sigma / \varepsilon$.

For oxide glasses, Makishima and Mackenzie (1973) demonstrated that Young's modulus can be estimated with surprising accuracy using a simple additive model over oxide contributions. The physical justification is that the overall stiffness of the glass network is the sum of the individual bond stiffnesses, weighted by the mole fractions of the different oxide species. Each oxide brings its own characteristic stiffness contribution $E_i$, determined by the strength and geometry of the cation–oxygen bond.

Oxide effects on stiffness:

  • Network formers (SiO2, Al2O3): High $E_i$ contributions because the strong, directional Si–O and Al–O bonds resist stretching. Alumina's contribution is particularly large because $[\text{AlO}_4]^{5-}$ tetrahedra cross-link the network.
  • Alkaline earths (CaO, MgO): Moderate $E_i$. The divalent cations bridge between non-bridging oxygens, providing ionic cross-links that stiffen the structure. MgO is particularly effective due to the small, highly charged $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ ion.
  • Alkalis (Na2O, K2O): Low or negative $E_i$ contributions. These ions occupy large interstitial sites and weaken the network by disrupting cross-links.

Typical ranges for oxide glasses:

  • Pure fused silica: $E \approx 72$ GPa
  • Soda-lime glass (windows): $E \approx 70$ GPa
  • Typical stoneware glaze: $E \approx 55–80$ GPa
  • High-alkali, low-alumina glaze: $E \approx 45–55$ GPa

In the atlas, Young's modulus is not just a standalone property—it feeds directly into the glaze stress calculation (Eq. [eq:glaze_stress]), the thermal shock parameter (Eq. [eq:thermal_shock]), all three derived moduli (Eq. [eq:bulk]–[eq:brittleness]), and the fracture toughness estimate. It is a foundational upstream property.

⚡ Vickers Micro-Hardness
\[ H \approx \frac{E}{15} \qquad [\text{GPa}] \]
Explanation:

Vickers microhardness measures a material's resistance to permanent (plastic) deformation under a pointed diamond indenter—specifically, a square-based pyramidal diamond pressed into the surface with a known force, after which the diagonal length of the resulting indentation is measured.

For oxide glasses, Sehgal and Ito (1999) demonstrated a remarkably simple empirical relationship between hardness and Young's modulus: $H \approx E/15$. This arises because the mechanisms of plastic deformation in glass (primarily densification and shear flow beneath the indenter) correlate closely with the elastic stiffness of the network. A glass that is stiff (high $E$) is also hard (high $H$) because the same strong, densely packed bonds that resist elastic stretching also resist permanent displacement.

Practical significance:

  • Scratch resistance: A harder glaze resists scratching by metal cutlery, sand, and other abrasives. Dinnerware manufacturers seek $H \gt 5.5$ GPa to pass cutlery-marking tests.
  • Wear resistance: Floor tiles and sanitaryware require high hardness to maintain their surfaces over decades of use.
  • Stress ratio: Hardness enters the stress ratio calculation (Eq. [eq:stress_ratio]) as a proxy for the glass's tensile strength, determining whether thermal stress will cause crazing.
  • Brittleness: Combined with fracture toughness, hardness determines the brittleness index $B = H/K_{IC}$, which predicts chipping behaviour.

Typical values:

Material$H$ [GPa]
Window glass (soda-lime)5.5
Borosilicate glass (Pyrex)6.5
Typical stoneware glaze4.5–6.5

⚡ Poisson's Ratio
\[ \nu = \sum_i x_i \, \nu_i \qquad (\text{typical: } 0.17\text{--}0.27) \]
Explanation:

Poisson's ratio, named after Siméon Denis Poisson (1827), describes how a material responds laterally when stressed axially. When you stretch a rubber band lengthwise, it gets thinner sideways—Poisson's ratio is the negative of the lateral strain divided by the axial strain.

For oxide glasses, $\nu$ typically falls in the range 0.17–0.27, much lower than for metals ($\nu \approx 0.30–0.35$) or rubber ($\nu \approx 0.50$). This reflects the open, three-dimensionally cross-linked nature of the glass network: when stretched, the tetrahedral framework deforms primarily by bond-angle changes rather than bond stretching, and the deformation propagates differently than in a close-packed metallic lattice.

Compositional effects:

  • Network formers (high SiO2, Al2O3): Push $\nu$ downward toward 0.17. The rigid, fully cross-linked network does not “neck down” easily under tension.
  • Network modifiers (high Na2O, K2O): Push $\nu$ upward toward 0.27–0.30. The disrupted, depolymerised network becomes more ductile, allowing greater lateral contraction.
  • Heavy modifiers (PbO, BaO): Can push $\nu$ above 0.27 due to the large, polarisable cations.

Why it matters:

  • Poisson's ratio enters the glaze stress formula (Eq. [eq:glaze_stress]) as $(1 - \nu)$ in the denominator, amplifying stress by 20–30%.
  • It determines the relationship between Young's modulus and the derived elastic moduli (bulk modulus $K$, shear modulus $G$).
  • At $\nu = 0.5$, a material is perfectly incompressible ($K \to \infty$). At $\nu = 0$, there is no lateral response to axial stress.

Despite its seemingly minor numerical range, small changes in $\nu$ have outsized effects on derived properties. Increasing $\nu$ from 0.20 to 0.25 changes the bulk modulus $K$ by about 15%.

From $E$ and $\nu$, three derived moduli are computed:

⚡ Elastic Moduli Cascade
\[\begin{aligned} K &= \frac{E}{3(1 - 2\nu)} & &\text{Bulk modulus (30--50~\text{GPa})} \\[6pt] G &= \frac{E}{2(1 + \nu)} & &\text{Shear modulus} \\[6pt] K_{IC} &= 0.016\sqrt{E} & &\text{Fracture toughness [\text{MPa√m}]} \\[6pt] B &= \frac{H}{K_{IC}} & &\text{Brittleness [\text{/μm}]} \end{aligned}\]

Explanation:

Once Young's modulus $E$ and Poisson's ratio $\nu$ are known, all remaining elastic moduli can be derived through the classical relationships of isotropic linear elasticity. The atlas computes four quantities from this cascade:

Bulk Modulus $K$: The bulk modulus measures resistance to uniform (hydrostatic) compression—how much the glass resists being squeezed equally from all sides. It was first defined in this form by Cauchy and later developed by Lord Kelvin (1856). For tightly packed, highly cross-linked networks (high alumina, high silica), $K$ is large (40–50 GPa); for open, depolymerised networks (high alkali), $K$ drops to 25–35 GPa. The formula diverges as $\nu \to 0.5$ (an incompressible material), which explains why rubber ($\nu \approx 0.49$) has an extremely high bulk modulus relative to its shear modulus.

Shear Modulus $G$: Also called the modulus of rigidity, $G$ measures resistance to shape change at constant volume—the response to a sliding or twisting force. In glass, shear deformation involves rotating and distorting the Si–O–Si bond angles rather than stretching bonds. $G$ is always smaller than $E$ for materials with $\nu \gt 0$, and typical glass values are 20–35 GPa. A low $G$ means the glass deforms more easily under shear stress, which can be relevant for glaze layers under complex loading (e.g., a pot sitting on an uneven surface).

Fracture Toughness $K_{IC}$: This critical stress intensity factor, defined by Irwin (1957) in the framework of linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM), quantifies the energy required to propagate an existing crack through the material. The Lawn & Marshall (1979) correlation $K_{IC} \approx 0.016\sqrt{E}$ provides an approximate value for oxide glasses based on the observation that harder, stiffer glasses require more energy to propagate cracks. Typical values for ceramic glazes are 0.5–1.0 $\text{MPa√m}$—far below metals ($K_{IC} \sim 50–150$) but sufficient for the thin-film stresses encountered on pottery. This is the main parameter controlling whether a glaze will chip when struck or abraded.

Brittleness $B$: The Lawn–Marshall brittleness index $B = H/K_{IC}$ (units: $\text{/μm}$) captures the competition between hardness (resistance to plastic deformation) and toughness (resistance to crack propagation). A high brittleness means the material deforms very little before cracking—it shatters rather than denting. This is a key predictor of chipping and flaking in dinnerware, where impacts from metal utensils and other dishes impose localised contact stresses.

Overall significance: Together, these four moduli provide a complete mechanical profile of the glaze. A well-designed functional glaze needs a balance: high enough $K_{IC}$ to resist chipping, high enough $H$ for scratch resistance, but not so brittle ($B$) that it fractures under thermal shock or mechanical impact. The elastic cascade computes this entire profile from just two upstream properties ($E$ and $\nu$), which are themselves computed from the oxide composition.

Temperature & Firing Intelligence (9 axes)

⚡ Glass Transition Temperature — Priven (2004)
\[ T_g = \sum_i x_i \, T_{g,i} \qquad [\text{°C}] \]
Explanation:

The glass transition temperature $T_g$ is one of the most fundamental properties of any glass. It marks the temperature at which the supercooled liquid transforms from a viscous, deformable state into a rigid, brittle solid. Below $T_g$, atoms are effectively frozen in place; above $T_g$, they can rearrange on laboratory timescales. The transition is not a true phase transition (no latent heat, no discontinuity in density) but rather a kinetic crossover, observable as a step change in heat capacity when measured by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC).

The Priven (2004) model estimates $T_g$ using an additive approach: each oxide contributes a characteristic $T_{g,i}$ value (in °C), and the glass's overall $T_g$ is the mole-fraction-weighted sum. This works because $T_g$ is primarily controlled by the bond strengths in the glass network—strong Si–O and Al–O bonds give high $T_g$, while weak modifier–oxygen bonds (Na–O, K–O) allow rearrangement at lower temperatures.

Oxide effects:

  • SiO2 and Al2O3 raise $T_g$ by strengthening the tetrahedral framework.
  • Na2O and K2O lower $T_g$ by disrupting the network and creating mobile modifier cations.
  • CaO and MgO have moderate effects—they modify the network but their higher charge provides some cross-linking.

Why $T_g$ matters for ceramics:

  1. Stress accumulation: Only below $T_g$ do thermal stresses develop between glaze and body. The glaze stress equation (Eq. [eq:glaze_stress]) uses $(T_g - 25)$ as the temperature interval over which stress builds.
  2. Annealing schedule: Kiln cooling should be controlled (slow) through the $T_g$ range to allow stress relaxation.
  3. Working range: The difference $T_{\text{fire}} - T_g$ defines how much “working room” the glaze has above its transition point. Too narrow a working range means the glaze barely has time to flow, heal, and smooth before becoming rigid.
  4. Dishwasher safety: Glazes with very low $T_g$ (below 400 °C) may undergo relaxation during repeated dishwasher heating cycles, potentially changing their stress state over time.

Typical values: 450–650 °C for stoneware glazes. Low-fire glazes may be as low as 350 °C; high-alumina glazes as high as 700 °C.

⚡ Volatilisation Index
\[ V = 1.5\,[\BO] + 0.8\,[\NaO] + 0.7\,[\KO] + [\LiO] + 1.2\,[\PbOx] + 0.3\,[\ZnOx] \]
Explanation:

Volatilisation is one of the least appreciated but most practically important phenomena in ceramic firing. During the 8–24 hours that a glaze is exposed to kiln temperatures above ∼900 °C, certain oxide species physically evaporate from the glaze surface as gaseous compounds ($\text{Na}_2\text{O}_{(g)}$, $\text{B}_2\text{O}_{3(g)}$, $\text{PbO}_{(g)}$, $\text{KOH}_{(g)}$, etc.).

The result is that the fired glaze composition differs from the batch composition—sometimes substantially. A glaze formula containing 0.3 moles of B2O3 might fire to an effective value of 0.15–0.20 moles after a long soak at cone 10. This means that all property calculations based on the batch composition will over-predict the effect of volatile oxides.

Volatility of each oxide:

  • B2O3 (weight 1.5): The most volatile common oxide. At cone 10 temperatures, boron volatilises as $\text{HBO}_2$ or $\text{B}_2\text{O}_3$ vapour. This is why B2O3 is rarely used above cone 6 and why boron-containing glazes often show unpredictable results at high fire.
  • PbO (weight 1.2): Lead volatilises readily above 1000 °C, which is both a composition-drift problem and a health hazard—lead fumes contaminate kiln furniture and neighbouring pots.
  • Li2O (weight 1.0): Lithia is surprisingly volatile at high temperatures.
  • Na2O (weight 0.8) and K2O (weight 0.7): Alkalis volatilise as hydroxides and contribute to the “soda flashing” seen on salt-glaze and wood-fired pottery.
  • ZnO (weight 0.3): Minor volatilisation, mostly at extreme temperatures.

The volatilisation index $V$ is therefore a weighted sum reflecting each oxide's tendency to leave the melt. High values ($V \gt 1.0$) warn the user that the as-fired composition will deviate significantly from the batch, and that property predictions (especially for high-temperature firings with long soaks) should be interpreted with caution.

Additional temperature and firing properties:

  • Firing Temperature: Converted from the user-selected cone number via the 15-point cone-to-temperature lookup table. Cones are not temperatures—they are measures of heatwork (time + temperature integral)—but the atlas uses the endpoint temperature from Orton cone charts as the nominal firing temperature for all VFT and temperature-dependent calculations.
  • Liquidus Estimate: The temperature above which the composition is completely liquid (no crystals), estimated using the Kalmanovitch (1986) empirical correlation from CAS ternary position.
  • Liquidus Overshoot: $T_{\text{fire}} - T_{\text{liquidus}}$. Positive values mean the kiln exceeds the liquidus (full melting achieved); negative values mean the composition is not fully molten at the firing temperature.
  • Heatwork Excess: A measure of how far above the minimum melting temperature the glaze is being fired. Higher excess means more fluidity, better healing, but also more risk of running.
  • Working Range: $T_{\text{fire}} - T_g$. The temperature interval during which the glaze is above its glass transition and can flow, heal, and respond to kiln conditions. Wider working ranges ($\gt 200$ °C) are more forgiving; narrow ranges ($\lt 100$ °C) require precise kiln control.
  • Melting Range: The span between first melting and complete liquefaction. Wide melting ranges produce gradual, controllable flow; narrow ranges produce sharp, sudden melting.
  • Cooling Crystal Window: The temperature range during cooling through which crystallisation is kinetically possible. Wide windows allow more time for crystal growth (desirable in crystal glazes; undesirable in clear glazes).

Surface, Optical & Luster (10 axes)

⚡ Refractive Index — Appen (1961)
\[ n = \sum_i x_i \, n_i \]
Explanation:

The refractive index—the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the speed of light in the material—is the fundamental optical property of a glass. It was first systematically characterised for oxide glasses by A.A. Appen in his comprehensive 1961 treatise on the chemistry of glass.

Appen's model, like his COE model, is additive: each oxide contributes a characteristic $n_i$ value, and the glass's overall RI is the mole-fraction-weighted sum. The physical basis for additivity lies in the Lorentz–Lorenz equation, which relates RI to the density and polarisability of the constituent species. Each oxide contributes its own electronic polarisability (how easily its electron cloud distorts under the oscillating electric field of a light wave), and these contributions are approximately independent.

High-RI oxides: PbO ($n_i \approx 2.15$), TiO2 ($n_i \approx 2.05$), BaO ($n_i \approx 1.88$), ZnO ($n_i \approx 1.70$). These contain large, highly polarisable cations whose electron clouds respond strongly to light, slowing it down.

Low-RI oxides: SiO2 ($n_i \approx 1.458$), B2O3 ($n_i \approx 1.46$). Small, tightly bonded cations with low polarisability.

Practical importance:

  • The refractive index directly determines the luster of a glaze through the Fresnel equation (Eq. [eq:fresnel]). Higher RI = more surface reflectance = more brilliant, “wet” appearance.
  • It determines depth perception—how much light bends at the surface and travels through the glass layers, creating the sense of depth and transparency that characterises the finest glazes.
  • It enters the Clausius–Mossotti equation (Eq. [eq:clausius_mossotti]) linking optical and dielectric properties.

Typical ranges:

  • Clear stoneware glaze: $n \approx 1.50–1.55$
  • High-zinc glaze: $n \approx 1.55–1.60$
  • Lead-bearing glaze: $n \approx 1.60–1.70$
  • Pure fused silica: $n = 1.458$

⚡ Luster Index — Fresnel Reflectance
\[ \boxed{R = \left(\frac{n - 1}{n + 1}\right)^2 \times 100\%} \]
Explanation:

The Fresnel equations are among the oldest and most fundamental results in optics, derived by Augustin-Jean Fresnel in 1823 from the wave theory of light. They describe how much light is reflected and transmitted at the interface between two transparent media with different refractive indices.

For normal incidence (light hitting the surface straight on) and a glass surface in air ($n_{\text{air}} \approx 1$), the equations simplify to the elegant form shown above. The reflectance $R$ depends only on the refractive index of the glass.

The physics of luster: When light from the room strikes a glazed pot, some fraction is reflected at the air–glaze interface (this is the surface reflection or luster), and the rest enters the glass. The surface reflection creates the bright, “wet” specular highlights that distinguish a glossy glaze from a matte one. A higher refractive index means a larger difference in optical density between air and glass, resulting in more reflected light.

Quantitative comparison:

Surface type$n$$R$ (%)
Water1.332.0
Standard clear glaze1.504.0
High-zinc glaze1.605.3

The difference between 4% and 7% reflectance may seem small, but the human eye is extremely sensitive to specular reflection. A lead or zinc-rich glaze with $R \approx 6–7\%$ appears dramatically more brilliant and lustrous than a standard clear glaze with $R \approx 4\%$. This is why leaded crystal glassware looks so different from soda-lime tumblers, and why zinc-bearing glazes have a distinctive richness of surface.

Note that this equation assumes a perfectly smooth, glossy surface. Matte and satin glazes scatter light diffusely through surface roughness and micro-crystallisation, so the effective luster is much lower than the Fresnel prediction even if the intrinsic RI is high.

⚡ Surface Tension — Dietzel (1942)
\[ \gamma = \sum_i x_i \, \gamma_i \qquad [\text{mN/m}] \]
Explanation:

Surface tension ($\gamma$) is the energy per unit area required to create new surface in a liquid. Physically, molecules at the surface of a melt are missing neighbours compared to bulk molecules, and the energetic cost of this “unsatisfied bonding” is the surface tension.

Adolf Dietzel (1942) was among the first to systematically relate the surface tension of oxide melts to their composition, using his field strength classification of cations. His additive model assigns each oxide a surface tension contribution $\gamma_i$ (in mN/m), and the glass's overall value is the mole-fraction-weighted sum.

Why surface tension is critical in ceramics:

  1. Crawling: If the surface tension of the molten glaze is too high, and the glaze layer contains contaminants (dust, oil, under-fired areas), the melt will pull away from those regions and bead up into thick ridges surrounding bare patches. This defect, called crawling, is directly governed by the ratio of surface tension to the adhesive force between glaze and body.
  2. Pinhole and bubble release: Gases trapped in the glaze (from decomposition of carbonates, sulfates, and organic matter) form bubbles that must rise through the melt and burst at the surface. The bubble release rate is proportional to the inverse of surface tension—lower tension allows the bubble walls to thin and rupture more easily.
  3. Self-healing: When a bubble does burst, the crater left behind must flow closed. The driving force for flow is surface tension (Laplace pressure, $\Delta P = 2\gamma/r$), while the resistance is viscosity. The ratio $\gamma / \eta$ is the “healing speed” of the glaze.
  4. Surface smoothness: At a molecular level, surface tension acts to minimise the surface area of the melt, pulling it toward a smooth, flat plane. Higher surface tension produces a stronger smoothing force—but only if viscosity is low enough to allow the surface to respond.

Typical values: 250–400 mN/m for silicate glaze melts. Alkali-rich glazes tend toward the low end; alkaline-earth glazes toward the high end.

⚡ Optical Bandgap
\[ E_g = 4.0 - 1.5\,\Lambda \qquad [\text{eV}] \]
where $\Lambda$ is the optical basicity (Eq. [eq:optical_basicity]).

Explanation:

The optical bandgap $E_g$ is a concept borrowed from solid-state physics. It represents the minimum photon energy required to excite an electron from the valence band (bonding states) to the conduction band (anti-bonding states) of the material. In a glass, the bandgap determines the UV absorption edge—the wavelength below which the glass begins to absorb light strongly.

The empirical linear relationship $E_g \approx 4.0 - 1.5\,\Lambda$ (where $\Lambda$ is the optical basicity) was established by Dimitrov and Komatsu (2002, 2010) through systematic studies of oxide glasses. The physics is intuitive: higher optical basicity means the oxide ions are more electron-rich, which raises the energy of the valence band (filled states) relative to the conduction band. This narrows the gap, shifting the absorption edge from the deep ultraviolet into the near-UV or even visible range.

Practical significance:

  • Color contribution: A glass with $E_g \lt 3.1$ eV (corresponding to wavelengths above 400 nm) will begin absorbing violet light, producing a yellowish tint through subtractive color mixing—even without traditional colorant oxides.
  • UV protection: Low-bandgap glasses absorb more UV radiation, which can be either desirable (UV-protective coatings) or undesirable (solarisation over time).
  • Photoluminescence: The bandgap determines where fluorescence excitation and emission occur, relevant for rare-earth-doped decorative glazes.

Typical values:

  • Pure fused silica: $E_g \approx 9$ eV (deep UV transparent)
  • Soda-lime glass: $E_g \approx 3.5–4.0$ eV
  • Alkali-rich glaze: $E_g \approx 3.0–3.5$ eV
  • High-basicity glass: $E_g \lt 3.0$ eV (absorbs into visible)

Additional surface and optical properties:

  • Abbe Number: A measure of optical dispersion—how much the refractive index varies with wavelength. Low Abbe number means high dispersion (rainbow-like colour separation in thick sections). Most glaze glasses have Abbe numbers of 50–60; lead-bearing glasses drop to 30–40.
  • Opacity Predictor: Estimates transparency vs. opacity from the combined effect of opacifier oxides (TiO2, ZrO2, SnO2), the degree of phase separation, and the crystalline content. Values range from 0 (transparent clear) to 100 (fully opaque white).
  • Density: Computed from additive molar-volume contributions (Appen, 1961). Typical glaze densities are 2.3–3.0 g/cm. Density feeds into the thermal diffusivity calculation and is required for converting between molar and weight quantities.
  • Matteness: A composite index scored 0–100, where 0 is high gloss and 100 is fully matte. Driven by alumina content, alkaline earth balance (especially MgO and BaO), and the devitrification tendency. High matteness correlates with micro-crystallisation on the surface, which scatters light diffusely.
  • Crawl Risk: The likelihood of the glaze pulling away from the body surface during firing, leaving bare patches surrounded by thick ridges. Computed from the ratio of surface tension to viscosity at the onset of melting, weighted by the presence of high-surface-tension refractory oxides.
  • Molar Refractivity: The Lorentz–Lorenz molar refractivity $R_M$, related to the total electronic polarisability of one mole of the glass. Used internally in the Clausius–Mossotti equation and as a standalone measure of the glass's optical response.

Chemistry, Durability & Safety (8 axes)

⚡ Chemical Durability
\[ D = \frac{[\SiO]}{[\text{R}_2\text{O}]} \]
Explanation:

Chemical durability—the resistance of glass to dissolution by water, acids, and bases—has been a central concern since the earliest days of glassmaking. The Romans observed that some glass compositions survived centuries in the ground while others decomposed within years. Modern understanding of glass corrosion was established by Hench and Clark (1978) and refined by Conradt (2008) and Frankel et al. (2018).

The dominant corrosion mechanism at neutral to acidic pH is selective leaching: water molecules and hydronium ions ($\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$) penetrate the glass surface and exchange with the weakly bonded alkali ions ($\text{Na}^+$, $\text{K}^+$, $\text{Li}^+$), pulling them into solution. The durable SiO2 network remains behind as a depleted, porous “leached layer.” The rate of this process is directly proportional to the concentration of leachable alkali species.

Hence, the ratio $D = [\SiO] / [\text{R}_2\text{O}]$ is a first-order predictor of durability:

  • High $D$ ($\gt 4$): The composition is silica-rich and alkali-poor. Very few leachable ions are available. Excellent durability—suitable for chemical labware, food contact, and architectural glass.
  • Moderate $D$ ($2–4$): Typical functional stoneware glaze range. Adequate durability for dinnerware, but prolonged exposure to acidic foods (tomatoes, citrus, vinegar) may extract measurable alkali.
  • Low $D$ ($\lt 2$): Alkali-dominant. The glass corrodes readily in water. Not food-safe unless other factors (high alumina, low specific alkali activity) compensate. Historically, this was the regime of cheap “water glass” (sodium silicate solutions).

Limitations: This simple ratio does not capture the protective effect of alumina (which dramatically improves durability by blocking ion-exchange sites), the role of alkaline earths (which are less mobile than alkalis), or the effect of boron anomaly (which can worsen or improve durability depending on its coordination). For a more comprehensive assessment, use the hydration energy (Eq. [eq:hydration]) and the food safety composite score.

⚡ Hydration Energy — Conradt (2008)
\[ \Delta H_{\text{hyd}} = \sum_i x_i \, \Delta H_i \qquad [\text{kJ/mol}] \]
Explanation:

The hydration energy approach to glass dissolution was developed by Rüdiger Conradt (2008) at RWTH Aachen University as a thermodynamically rigorous predictor of glass stability. Unlike the simple durability ratio (Eq. [eq:durability]), which only considers one aspect of composition, the hydration energy captures the overall thermodynamic driving force for the glass to react with water.

The model treats the glass as a sum of hypothetical oxide components, each with a known free energy of hydration $\Delta H_i$. Some oxides are thermodynamically stable against water attack ($\Delta H_i \approx 0$ or positive); others are strongly driven to dissolve ($\Delta H_i \ll 0$). The total hydration energy is the weighted sum over all oxides.

Key oxide contributions:

  • SiO2: Nearly insoluble in water at ambient conditions ($\Delta H_i \approx 0$). Only dissolves significantly at high pH.
  • Al2O3: Strongly resistant to dissolution ($\Delta H_i \gt 0$). Alumina is the most effective single oxide for improving chemical durability.
  • Na2O, K2O: Highly hygroscopic ($\Delta H_i \ll 0$). In isolation, these would dissolve instantly in water.
  • CaO: Moderately negative $\Delta H_i$. Soluble as Ca(OH)2 but much less mobile than alkalis in a glass matrix.

Practical use:

  • More negative $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$: Greater thermodynamic driving force for dissolution. The glass is unstable against water.
  • Less negative / near zero: The glass is thermodynamically stable. It will resist corrosion over decades.

The hydration energy is particularly valuable for assessing long-term behaviour. A glaze may pass a short-term ASTM leach test ($4\%$ acetic acid, 24 hours) while still having a thermodynamic driving force for slow, progressive corrosion over years of use.

Additional chemistry and safety properties:

  • Acid Resistance: Estimates resistance to attack by dilute hydrochloric and acetic acids, computed from the silica-to-modifier ratio and the alumina content (which dramatically improves acid resistance by blocking ion-exchange sites in the network).
  • Leach Risk: A composite hazard score incorporating concentrations of lead (PbO), barium (BaO), copper (CuO), and chromium (Cr2O3) weighted by their respective toxicological potencies. Values above a threshold flag the composition for regulatory attention.
  • Food Safety Score: The master safety index—a composite check that combines the leach risk with the chemical durability ratio, hydration energy, acid resistance, and alkali content. Glazes scoring below the food-safety threshold should not be used for surfaces that contact food or drink. This is a screening tool, not a replacement for laboratory ASTM C738 / ISO 6486 leach testing.
  • ASI (Alumina Saturation Index): Detailed in Section [sec:properties] under Core Ratios (Eq. [eq:asi]). Values below 1.0 indicate peralkaline compositions.
  • Excess Alkali: The molar amount of alkali oxide that exceeds the amount consumed by alumina charge-balancing. This “free” alkali is the portion available to create NBOs, lower viscosity, increase expansion, and reduce durability.
  • KNaO Equivalent: Total alkali expressed as an equivalent moles of combined K2O + Na2O, for comparison with published limit formulas that report alkali as a single value.

Flux Analysis & Diversity (7 axes)

⚡ Shannon Entropy of Flux Distribution
\[ \boxed{H = -\sum_i p_i \ln(p_i)} \]
where $p_i = [\text{flux}_i] / \sum[\text{fluxes}]$ is the molar fraction of each flux.

Explanation:

Claude Shannon introduced his entropy formula in his landmark 1948 paper “A Mathematical Theory of Communication” at Bell Labs. Originally designed to quantify information content in communications channels, entropy has since become one of the most widely applied concepts in science. Here, it is applied to the probability distribution of fluxing oxides in a glaze composition.

The idea is to treat the flux portion of the recipe as a “message” composed of different “symbols” (oxide species). If only one flux is present, the message is completely predictable—entropy is zero. If many fluxes are present in equal proportions, the message is maximally uncertain—entropy is at its highest.

The ceramicist's reason to care: flux diversity has profound practical consequences.

  1. Eutectic depression (the “cocktail effect”): When multiple fluxes are combined, the system can access eutectic compositions where the liquidus temperature is lower than that of any single-flux system. This is the same principle by which road salt ($\text{NaCl} + \text{CaCl}_2$ mixtures) melts ice more effectively than either salt alone. A glaze with $H \approx 1.5$ (4–5 diverse fluxes) may melt 50–100 °C lower than a single-flux glaze with the same total flux moles.
  2. Smoother melting curve: Single-flux systems often have sharp liquidus minima, meaning the glaze transitions abruptly from under-fired to over-fired over a narrow temperature range. Multi-flux systems smooth this transition, making the glaze more forgiving of temperature variations within the kiln.
  3. Property averaging: Each flux has different effects on expansion, color, surface tension, and durability. Distributing flux across multiple species moderates the extreme effects of any single one.
  4. Defect resistance: Diverse flux systems tend to have lower surface tension (reducing crawling), lower volatilisation (no single species is present in excess), and more predictable flow.

Interpretation:

  • $H = 0$: Only one flux (e.g., pure CaO glaze). Functional but one-dimensional.
  • $H \approx 1.0$: Two to three fluxes in moderate balance. Common for simple stoneware.
  • $H \approx 1.5$: Four to five fluxes, well distributed. The sweet spot for versatile, forgiving glazes.
  • $H \gt 2.0$: Exceptional diversity. Rare in practice unless many minor fluxes are present.

⚡ Dietzel Field Strength
\[ F = \frac{z}{a^2} \]
where $z$ is the cation valence and $a$ is the cation–oxygen bond distance [Å].

Explanation:

Adolf Dietzel introduced the concept of cation field strength in 1942 while working at the Kaiser-Wilhelm Institute for Silicate Research. His insight was that the behaviour of a cation in a glass melt—whether it acts as a network former, a network modifier, or an intermediate species—can be predicted from a single parameter: the electrostatic field experienced by a neighbouring oxygen anion.

The field strength is the Coulombic force per unit charge: $F = z/a^2$, where $z$ is the cation's formal charge (valence) and $a$ is the cation–oxygen equilibrium bond distance in angstroms (typically from X-ray crystallography of the corresponding oxide crystal).

In the Stull Atlas, the weighted mean field strength of the overall composition is computed by averaging $F_i$ over all species weighted by mole fraction. This provides a single-number summary of how aggressively the composition builds versus breaks its glass network. Higher mean field strength means a more durable, more viscous, more refractory glass.

Dietzel's field strength also underpins the surface tension model (same author, same 1942 paper): oxides with high $F$ contribute high surface tension because strong cation–oxygen bonds resist the creation of new surfaces.

⚡ Mixed Alkali Effect
\[ \text{MAE} = 4\,x_{\text{Na}} \cdot x_{\text{K}} \]
where $x_{\text{Na}} = \NaO / (\NaO + \KO)$ and $x_{\text{K}} = 1 - x_{\text{Na}}$.

Explanation:

The mixed alkali effect (MAE) is one of the most dramatic and least intuitively obvious anomalies in glass science. First reported by Weber in 1883 and extensively studied throughout the 20th century, it describes a striking phenomenon: when two different alkali oxides are present in a glass, almost every transport property deviates strongly from the linear interpolation between the two single-alkali end-members.

What happens:

  • Ionic conductivity: Drops by as much as 3–4 orders of magnitude at the 50:50 Na2O:K2O ratio compared to either pure end-member.
  • Viscosity: Increases significantly at intermediate compositions.
  • $T_g$: Rises above the linear average.
  • Chemical durability: Improves—mixed-alkali glasses are more resistant to leaching than either single-alkali glass.
  • Thermal expansion: Decreases below the linear average.

The MAE index $= 4 x_{\text{Na}} \cdot x_{\text{K}}$ is a mathematical convenience: the product $x_{\text{Na}} \cdot x_{\text{K}}$ is maximised when the two alkalis are present in equal molar amounts ($x_{\text{Na}} = x_{\text{K}} = 0.5$), giving $\text{MAE} = 1.0$. The factor of 4 normalises the index to a 0–1 scale.

Physical origin: The current leading explanation invokes the unlike-cation blocking mechanism. Each alkali species creates its own preferred migration pathway through the glass network (Na+ sites are smaller than K+ sites due to the smaller ionic radius). When both species are present, Na+ ions block K+ pathways and vice versa, dramatically reducing the mobility of both species. This is analogous to two-lane traffic where trucks (K+) and motorcycles (Na+) constantly block each other's lanes.

Practical implications for potters:

  1. Craze prevention: A 50:50 Na2O:K2O mix has a lower expansion than the average of the two individual glazes, helping prevent crazing.
  2. Better durability: The reduced alkali mobility makes the mixed composition more resistant to leaching.
  3. Higher viscosity: Useful if a glaze is running too much—substituting half the soda for potash (or vice versa) will stiffen the melt.

Colorant, Atmosphere & Color Prediction (10 axes)

⚡ Fe2+ Fraction — Redox Equilibrium
\[ \frac{[\text{Fe}^{2+}]}{[\text{Fe}_{\text{total}}]} = f(\Lambda, T, p_{\text{O}_2}) \]
Explanation:

Iron is the most prevalent colorant in ceramic glazes—present as an impurity in nearly every clay and feldspar, and added intentionally in concentrations from 0.5% to 12% or more. What makes iron uniquely complex is that it exists in glass in two oxidation states, each producing dramatically different colors:

  • Fe3+ (ferric iron): Produces amber, tan, and honey colors in oxidation. The $d^5$ electronic configuration gives a pale yellow to brown depending on concentration and coordination.
  • Fe2+ (ferrous iron): Produces blue, green, and olive colors in reduction. The $d^6$ configuration creates a strong absorption band in the red-orange region of the spectrum.

The equilibrium between these two states is governed by three factors:

  1. Optical basicity ($\Lambda$): As described in Eq. [eq:optical_basicity], higher basicity promotes reduction of Fe3+ to Fe2+ by increasing the electron-donating power of oxygen anions. This is a compositional effect.
  2. Temperature ($T$): Higher firing temperatures shift the equilibrium toward Fe2+ (reduction is thermodynamically favoured at high $T$).
  3. Oxygen partial pressure ($p_{\text{O}_2}$): Kiln atmosphere. Reduction firing (low $p_{\text{O}_2}$, from combustible gas atmosphere) strongly promotes Fe2+; oxidation firing (excess air) promotes Fe3+.

The atlas estimates the Fe2+ fraction from composition alone, using the optical basicity as a proxy for the combined effect of glass chemistry on the redox equilibrium. This provides a prediction of the tendency of the glaze toward reduction or oxidation coloring—but the actual result also depends heavily on the kiln atmosphere, which the user must know independently.

⚡ CIE $L^*a^*b^*$ Color Prediction
\[\begin{aligned} L^* &= f(\text{opacity, colorant loading, base glass}) \\ a^* &= g(\text{Fe, Cu, Cr, Mn concentrations, redox state}) \\ b^* &= h(\text{Fe, Ti, Ce concentrations, optical basicity}) \end{aligned}\]
Explanation:

The CIE $L^*a^*b^*$ color space (1976) is the international standard for quantitative color specification. Developed by the Commission Internationale de l'Éclairage (International Commission on Illumination), it is designed to be perceptually uniform—meaning that a given numerical distance between two colors corresponds to approximately the same perceived visual difference, regardless of where in the color space those colors lie.

The three coordinates:

  • $L^*$ (Lightness): 0 = absolute black, 100 = perfect white. In a glaze, $L^*$ is controlled by colorant loading (more colorant = darker), base glass opacity, and crystal content.
  • $a^*$ (Green–Red axis): Negative values = green, positive = red. In glazes, copper drives $a^*$ positive (red/pink) in reduction but negative (green/turquoise) in oxidation. Chromium always drives $a^*$ strongly negative (green). Iron drives $a^*$ mildly positive.
  • $b^*$ (Blue–Yellow axis): Negative values = blue, positive = yellow. Iron is the primary driver of positive $b^*$ (amber, brown, yellow). Titanium and cerium also shift $b^*$ positive.

The atlas computes approximate $L^*a^*b^*$ predictions using empirical correlations between colorant concentrations, redox state, and optical basicity. These are rough first-order estimates—accurate to perhaps $\pm 10$ units—because true spectral color prediction requires full Kubelka–Munk two-flux reflectance theory, which models the absorption and scattering of light through the glaze thickness and accounts for the substrate (clay body) color, glaze layer thickness, and wavelength-dependent optical constants.

Kubelka–Munk spectral color prediction is planned for v4.0 of the atlas, which will provide significantly more accurate color forecasting by computing reflectance spectra from the colorant absorption coefficients and scattering properties of the glass matrix.

Practical use today: The current estimates are most useful for comparative purposes: “Glaze A will be significantly more yellow ($b^*$) than Glaze B” rather than “Glaze A will be exactly Pantone 7501C.” They help identify which glazes in the database are likely to produce warm tones vs. cool tones, and how colorant substitutions will shift the color direction.

Crystallisation & Phase Prediction (8 axes)

Crystallisation is the central drama of ceramic glaze making. Every glaze begins as a melt—a disordered liquid of oxide species—and must cool through a temperature range where crystalline phases are thermodynamically favoured. Whether the glaze vitrifies into a clear glass or grows crystals depends on the competition between the thermodynamic driving force for ordering and the kinetic barrier imposed by viscosity. This category of properties maps the crystallisation landscape.

  • Devitrification Index & Resistance: Devitrification is the spontaneous growth of crystals from a glass during cooling—usually unwanted, appearing as milky patches, matte spots, or rough texture on what was intended to be a glossy surface. The index estimates susceptibility based on the composition's position relative to known crystallisation fields. A high devitrification index warns that the glaze sits in a compositional zone where crystals (often wollastonite, anorthite, or mullite) nucleate readily during slow cooling. The resistance metric is its complement: compositions with high viscosity at the crystallisation temperature and low thermodynamic driving force score high resistance.
  • Phase Separation Index: Not all glazes are homogeneous liquids. In certain compositional ranges, the melt spontaneously separates into two immiscible liquid phases—like oil and water—each with a different composition. This liquid–liquid immiscibility produces the milky opalescence characteristic of ch\"un (jun) glazes and certain sodium borosilicate systems. The phase separation index is computed from the SiO2–Na2O–B2O3 balance and the alumina content. Values above ∼20 predict visible opalescence; values above 40 predict strong blue-white ch\"un effects.
  • Wollastonite Saturation: Wollastonite ($\text{CaO}\cdot\text{SiO}_2$) is the most common crystalline phase in high-calcium stoneware glazes (CaO $\gt 0.5$ in UMF). It appears as fine, needle-like crystals that can create beautiful matte or satin surfaces—or, when unwanted, a rough, toothy texture. The saturation index computes how close the composition is to the wollastonite primary phase field in the $\text{CaO--Al}_2\text{O}_3\text{--SiO}_2$ ternary diagram.
  • Zinc Crystal Index: Zinc silicate (willemite, $\text{Zn}_2\text{SiO}_4$) is the crystal phase responsible for the spectacular patterns in zinc crystal glazes. The index scores the likelihood of willemite formation based on ZnO content, SiO2: ratio, and the flux balance. Values above 50 indicate strong potential for macroscopic crystal growth during slow cooling through the 1050–1150 °C range.
  • Liquidus Field (1–5): The CaO–Al2O3–SiO2 ternary phase diagram (Muan & Osborn, 1965) is divided into primary phase fields—regions where a specific crystal phase is the first to appear upon cooling. The atlas projects the glaze composition into CAS ternary space and reports which field it falls in: (1) mullite, (2) anorthite, (3) wollastonite, (4) tridymite/cristobalite, or (5) gehlenite. This immediately tells the user what crystal phase is most likely to form if the glaze devitrifies.
  • Eutectic Distance: Eutectics are compositions where two or more crystal phases melt simultaneously at the lowest possible temperature. The closer a glaze is to a eutectic point, the lower its melting temperature and the wider its firing range. The atlas computes the Euclidean distance in normalised CAS ternary space to the nearest of five Muan & Osborn (1965) eutectic points. Short eutectic distance ($\lt 0.05$) means the glaze is in a melting “sweet spot”; large distance ($\gt 0.15$) means it is far from optimal melting and may require higher temperatures.
  • Opal Potential: Opalescence in glazes arises from two mechanisms: (a) liquid–liquid phase separation (see above) and (b) phosphate-induced nucleation, where $\text{P}_2\text{O}_5$ promotes precipitation of calcium phosphate nanoparticles that scatter light. The opal potential scores both pathways, providing a composite estimate of a composition's tendency to produce milky, blue-white, or iridescent visual effects.

Sensitivity & Error Analysis (3 axes)

⚡ Taylor Error Propagation — GUM (1997)
\[ \boxed{\delta f = \sqrt{\sum_i \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}\right)^2 \!\cdot\, \delta x_i^2}} \]
Explanation:

The Taylor expansion method for uncertainty propagation is the gold standard in metrology, codified in the Guide to the expression of Uncertainty in Measurement (GUM), published in 1997 by a consortium of international standards bodies including ISO, BIPM, and NIST. It answers a fundamental question: if my input measurements have uncertainties, how uncertain is my computed result?

The method works by linearising the function $f(\mathbf{x})$ around the nominal input values using a first-order Taylor expansion:

\[f(\mathbf{x} + \delta\mathbf{x}) \approx f(\mathbf{x}) + \sum_i \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} \cdot \delta x_i\]

The standard uncertainty in the output is then computed using the sum-in-quadrature rule (assuming independent, normally distributed input uncertainties). This is exact for linear functions and an excellent approximation for mildly non-linear ones.

In the Stull Atlas, this formula is applied to every one of the 133 properties. The partial derivatives $\partial f / \partial x_i$ are computed numerically (see Eq. [eq:jacobian]), and the input uncertainty $\delta x_i$ represents the typical measurement or estimation error in each oxide's molar amount.

Three derived outputs:

  1. Robustness Score: The inverse of the total propagated uncertainty, normalised to a 0–100 scale. A robust property is one that is insensitive to small input perturbations—the potter can trust the prediction even with imprecise knowledge of the oxide recipe. A fragile property changes dramatically with small input variations.
  2. Critical Oxide Impact: Identifies which single oxide has the largest partial derivative $|\partial f / \partial x_i|$ for the current Z-axis property. This tells the potter which oxide to control most carefully if they want to hit a specific target value.
  3. Error Width (COE): The propagated uncertainty specifically for thermal expansion—the most practically important property. A narrow error width means the COE prediction is reliable; a wide error width means the prediction should be treated as tentative.

Practical value: A glaze formula taken from a database might look precise (Na2O = 0.280), but the actual composition of the fired glaze depends on raw material purity, batch weighing accuracy, volatilisation, and kiln atmosphere. The Taylor propagation reveals whether these real-world uncertainties matter or not for the property you care about.

⚡ Numerical Jacobian
\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} \approx \frac{f(x_i + h) - f(x_i - h)}{2h} \]
Explanation:

The numerical Jacobian is the computational workhorse that makes the Taylor error propagation (Eq. [eq:taylor]) possible without requiring analytical derivatives of every property function.

The approach is central finite differencing: to estimate how property $f$ changes when oxide $x_i$ is perturbed, we compute $f$ at two points—one slightly above and one slightly below the nominal value—and divide the difference by the total step width $2h$. This is more accurate than a one-sided (forward or backward) difference because the leading error term is of order $h^2$ rather than $h$.

In practice, the atlas evaluates this derivative for every oxide (22 values of $i$) for every property being analyzed, building a full $22 \times 1$ Jacobian vector. At a step size of $h = 0.01$ moles, this requires $2 \times 22 = 44$ additional property evaluations per sensitivity analysis—a trivial computational cost on modern hardware but one that would have been prohibitive in the pre-computer era.

Why numerical rather than analytical?

  • Many of the 133 property functions involve conditional logic (if/else branches for different compositional regimes), lookup tables, and recursive dependencies. Deriving analytical partial derivatives of such complex functions would be extremely labor-intensive and error-prone.
  • The numerical approach is automatically correct for any function, no matter how complex, as long as the step size is appropriate.
  • Modern JavaScript engines (V8, SpiderMonkey) execute the 44 extra function evaluations in under 1 millisecond.

Step size selection: $h = 0.01$ is chosen to be large enough to avoid floating-point roundoff errors ($\sim 10^{-15}$) but small enough that the linearisation assumption holds for mildly non-linear functions. For strongly non-linear functions (VFT viscosity near $T_g$), the central difference may over- or underestimate the derivative, but the error is typically within 5–10%.

Defect Prediction (3 axes)

Glaze defects—pinholes, blisters, crawling, and rough surfaces—are the bane of every potter's existence. Most defects arise not from “wrong” chemistry but from an unfavourable interplay between viscosity, surface tension, gas evolution, and time. The atlas computes three defect-prediction indices directly from the oxide composition, using the VFT viscosity model (Eq. [eq:vft]) and Dietzel surface tension (Eq. [eq:surface_tension]) as inputs.

  • Pinhole Risk: Pinholes are tiny craters in the fired surface caused by gas bubbles that reach the surface but fail to heal before the glaze stiffens. The risk is a function of the VFT viscosity at peak firing temperature: if the melt is too viscous ($\log_{10}\eta \gt 5$), bubbles rising from the glaze–body interface cannot burst through and the resulting craters cannot flow closed. The index also incorporates the volatile content (Eq. [eq:volatilisation]), because high-volatile compositions produce more gas during firing. A pinhole risk above 6 (on a 0–10 scale) warns that the composition requires either a higher peak temperature, a longer soak, or a modified cooling schedule to heal surface defects.
  • Bubble Risk: Bubble risk addresses a slightly different pathology: large, visible blisters trapped within the glaze layer. These form when gas generation (from carbonate decomposition, sulfate breakdown, or organic burnout in the body) exceeds the melt's ability to release the gas. The risk is proportional to the product of gas-producing potential and viscosity, divided by surface tension. High surface tension traps bubbles by preventing bubble walls from thinning; high viscosity slows bubble rise. The index identifies compositions where the “bubble drainage time” exceeds the available time at peak temperature.
  • Healing Index: The healing index is the converse of the defect indices—it measures the glaze's ability to repair surface imperfections during firing. The physical mechanism is Laplace-driven flow: when a surface defect (pit, crater, orange peel) exists, the curvature creates a pressure differential ($\Delta P = 2\gamma / r$) that drives glaze flow from high to low spots. The healing speed is proportional to $\gamma / \eta$—the ratio of surface tension to viscosity. A high healing index (above 7) means the glaze will produce a smooth, self-levelled surface even if gas evolution or crawling partially disrupts it during early stages of firing.

Electrical & Functional Properties (3 axes)

⚡ Dielectric Constant — Clausius–Mossotti
\[ \frac{\varepsilon - 1}{\varepsilon + 2} = \frac{4\pi}{3}\,\frac{N_A \, \rho}{M} \sum_i x_i \, \alpha_i \]
where $\varepsilon$ is the dielectric constant, $\rho$ is density, $M$ is molar mass, and $\alpha_i$ are molar polarisabilities.

Explanation:

The Clausius–Mossotti equation is one of the oldest and most fundamental relationships in electrodynamics, independently derived by Ottaviano-Fabrizio Mossotti (1850) and Rudolf Clausius (1879). When applied to optical frequencies, it becomes the Lorentz–Lorenz equation (Hendrik Lorentz and Ludvig Lorenz, 1880). Both forms express the same deep insight: the macroscopic dielectric (or optical) response of a material arises from the sum of the molecular-level polarisabilities of its constituent species.

The left-hand side, $(\varepsilon - 1)/(\varepsilon + 2)$, is the Clausius–Mossotti function—a mathematical transformation that maps the macroscopic dielectric constant $\varepsilon$ to a quantity that is linear in microscopic polarisability. The right-hand side sums the individual oxide polarisabilities $\alpha_i$, weighted by their mole fractions and corrected for the molar volume (via density $\rho$ and molar mass $M$).

Physical meaning of the dielectric constant: The dielectric constant $\varepsilon$ (relative permittivity) measures how strongly a material polarises in response to an applied electric field. In practical terms:

  • $\varepsilon = 1$: Vacuum (no polarisation).
  • $\varepsilon \approx 3.5–5$: Typical oxide glass range.
  • $\varepsilon \approx 6–10$: High-$\varepsilon$ glass (barium, lead, titanium-rich).

Why it matters for ceramics:

  1. Microwave interaction: At the microwave frequency of 2.45  (the frequency used by domestic microwave ovens), the dielectric constant and loss tangent ($\tan \delta$) determine how much microwave energy is absorbed by the glaze. A high-loss-tangent glaze heats up in the microwave, potentially causing thermal shock. Low $\varepsilon$ and low $\tan \delta$ are preferred for microwave-safe dinnerware.
  2. Electronic substrates: In technical ceramics, dielectric properties determine suitability for electronic packaging, capacitors, and insulating layers.
  3. Connection to RI: At optical frequencies, $\varepsilon = n^2$, connecting the dielectric constant directly to the refractive index (Eq. [eq:ri]).

Additional electrical properties:

  • Ionic Conductivity: Estimated from the Anderson & Stuart (1954) model. Alkali ions (Na+, K+, Li+) are the primary charge carriers in oxide glasses, hopping between interstitial sites under an applied electric field. Higher alkali content increases conductivity; mixed alkalis dramatically reduce it (see MAE, Eq. [eq:mae]). Practical significance: high ionic conductivity correlates with poor chemical durability (the same mobile ions that carry current are the ones that leach into water) and with microwave absorption.
  • Loss Tangent at 2.45 : The dielectric loss tangent ($\tan \delta$) at the microwave oven frequency. High $\tan \delta$ means the glaze absorbs microwave energy efficiently—it heats up in the microwave. This can cause localised thermal shock if the glaze heats faster than the body, or simply make the rim of a mug uncomfortably hot. Low-loss glazes ($\tan \delta \lt 0.01$) are microwave-transparent and safe for dinnerware.

Thermal Transport (3 axes)

⚡ Thermal Conductivity & Derived Properties
\[\begin{aligned} \lambda &= \sum_i x_i \, \lambda_i & &[\text{W/m/K}] \\ C_p &= \sum_i x_i \, C_{p,i} & &[\text{J/g/K}] \\ \kappa &= \frac{\lambda}{\rho \, C_p} & &[\text{m²d/s}] \end{aligned}\]
Explanation:

These three interrelated properties describe how heat moves through the glaze layer during firing, cooling, and use. Together, they govern the thermal responsiveness of the glaze—how quickly it equilibrates to temperature changes.

Thermal Conductivity $\lambda$: Susa et al. (1995) measured thermal conductivities of multi-component oxide melts and showed that an additive model over oxide contributions provides useful estimates. The physics is that heat in a glass is conducted primarily through lattice vibrations (phonons), and each oxide bond type transmits phonons with a characteristic efficiency. Strong, short bonds (Si–O, Al–O) are efficient phonon conductors; large, weakly bonded modifier cations scatter phonons and reduce conductivity.

Typical values: 0.8–1.5 W/m/K for silicate glasses—about 100 times lower than metals and about twice that of air. This low thermal conductivity is why glazes can develop thermal gradients during rapid heating or cooling.

Specific Heat $C_p$: Stebbins, Carmichael & Moret (1984) provided oxide-additive specific heat data for silicate melts. Specific heat determines how much energy is stored per degree of temperature rise. Glasses with high $C_p$ absorb more energy before their temperature changes, making them energetically expensive to heat but also more thermally stable once at temperature.

Thermal Diffusivity $\kappa$: Diffusivity combines all three thermal properties into a single “how fast does temperature equilibrate?” parameter. It is the ratio $\lambda / (\rho \cdot C_p)$:

  • High $\kappa$: Temperature equilibrates quickly. The surface and interior of the glaze reach the same temperature within seconds.
  • Low $\kappa$: Temperature equilibrates slowly. Large thermal gradients can develop between the glaze surface (exposed to kiln atmosphere) and the glaze-body interface.

Practical significance:

  1. Thermal shock: Low diffusivity means the surface of a pot heats (or cools) much faster than the interior, creating stress gradients. Combined with the thermal shock resistance parameter (Eq. [eq:thermal_shock]), diffusivity predicts whether a glaze will survive rapid temperature changes.
  2. Kiln design: Low-conductivity glazes require slower heating ramps to avoid surface-interior temperature differentials exceeding the fracture threshold.
  3. Energy efficiency: Low-$C_p$ glazes require less total energy to reach temperature, which can be relevant for industrial kiln scheduling.

Glass-Forming Ability (3 axes)

⚡ Turnbull Reduced Glass Transition
\[ T_{rg} = \frac{T_g}{T_m} \]
Explanation:

David Turnbull's 1969 paper “Under What Conditions Can a Glass Be Formed?” introduced the reduced glass transition temperature $T_{rg}$ as a simple but powerful predictor of glass-forming ability. His insight came from studying the kinetics of crystallisation in supercooled liquids.

The argument works as follows: for crystals to form from a melt, two conditions must be met simultaneously:

  1. Thermodynamic driving force: The melt must be below the liquidus temperature $T_m$ so that the crystalline phase is thermodynamically favoured.
  2. Kinetic accessibility: The atoms must be mobile enough to rearrange from the disordered liquid structure into an ordered crystalline lattice.

Crystallisation rate is highest at an intermediate temperature between $T_m$ and $T_g$. If $T_g$ is close to $T_m$ (high $T_{rg}$), the liquid becomes so viscous near $T_m$ that atoms cannot rearrange fast enough for crystal nucleation. The melt “bypasses” the crystallisation zone and vitrifies (forms glass) instead.

Turnbull's criterion:

  • $T_{rg} \gt 2/3 \approx 0.67$: Excellent glass former. Crystallisation is almost impossible at normal cooling rates.
  • $T_{rg} \approx 0.55–0.67$: Good glass former with moderate cooling rates.
  • $T_{rg} \lt 0.50$: Marginal glass former. Requires very rapid cooling (quenching) to avoid crystallisation.

Most silicate ceramic glazes have $T_{rg}$ in the range 0.60–0.75, placing them firmly in the “good to excellent” glass-forming category. This is why silicate glazes reliably form glasses rather than crystalline masses—the silicate network produces a high $T_g$ relative to the liquidus temperature.

Connection to practice: Glazes with low $T_{rg}$ (below 0.55) are candidates for deliberate crystallisation—by providing a slow cooling schedule through the nucleation zone, crystals can be grown from the melt (as in crystal glazes using zinc and titanium). The atlas uses $T_{rg}$ to flag compositions with high or low crystallisation potential.

⚡ Hrubý Parameter
\[ K_H = \frac{T_x - T_g}{T_m - T_x} \]
where $T_x$ is the crystallisation onset temperature.

Explanation:

Tomáš Hrubý introduced this parameter in 1972 while studying the glass-forming tendency of chalcogenide glasses in Czechoslovakia. The Hrubý parameter extends Turnbull's approach by incorporating a third critical temperature: the crystallisation onset temperature $T_x$, which can be measured directly by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) or differential thermal analysis (DTA).

The three temperatures:

  • $T_g$: Glass transition. Below this, the material is a rigid glass.
  • $T_x$: Crystallisation onset. During reheating, this is the temperature at which crystals first begin to grow from the glass. The gap $T_x - T_g$ represents the “thermal stability window”—the temperature range over which the glass can be reheated without crystallising.
  • $T_m$: Melting point (liquidus). The gap $T_m - T_x$ represents how far the crystallisation event is from complete melting.

Physical interpretation:

  • Large $K_H$ (wide $T_x - T_g$, narrow $T_m - T_x$): The glass resists crystallisation over a wide temperature range during reheating, and any crystals that do form are close to the melting point (easily destroyed). This is a highly stable glass.
  • Small $K_H$ ($T_x$ close to $T_g$): The glass begins to crystallise almost immediately upon reheating above $T_g$. This is a poor glass former—or, viewed differently, an excellent candidate for glass-ceramics.

Connection to ceramics: The Hrubý parameter is directly relevant to kiln cooling schedules. A glaze with high $K_H$ can be cooled rapidly through its annealing range without fear of devitrification. A glaze with low $K_H$ may develop unwanted crystals (devitrification) during slow cooling—or, if crystallisation is desired (as in crystal glazes), a low $K_H$ indicates that the composition is well-suited for controlled crystal growth.

The atlas estimates $T_x$ from the composition using empirical correlations developed by Cabral & Fokin (2004), who showed that $T_x$ can be related to the critical cooling rate needed to prevent crystallisation.

Style Indices (5 axes)

Classic glaze families—tenmoku, celadon, shino, copper red—are not defined by single recipes but by compositional windows within which the characteristic appearance emerges. Different potters achieve the same classic look through different specific recipes, but all share a core oxide signature. The style indices capture these signatures quantitatively, scoring any composition from 0 to 100 for its proximity to each archetype.

  • Tenmoku Index: Tenmoku (tianmu) is the family of iron-saturated glazes producing deep brown-to-black surfaces with characteristic “hare's fur,” “oil spot,” or “partridge feather” patterns. The index scores compositions based on: (a) Fe2O3 content (optimum 6–10%), (b) moderate total flux (∼0.5–0.7 in UMF), (c) Si:Al ratio in a specific window that promotes the thin iron-saturated surface layer responsible for the patterns, and (d) sufficient CaO to assist iron crystallisation. A score above 70 places the composition firmly in tenmoku territory.
  • Celadon Index: Celadon is the jade-green glaze tradition of Chinese, Korean, and Japanese ceramics, achieved through small amounts of iron (1–3% Fe2O3) fired in reduction. The index computes a Euclidean distance in CAS ternary space between the composition and the ideal celadon target (approximately SiO2 3.5, Al2O3 0.35, CaO 0.5–0.7 in UMF), penalising excess colorants and excessive alkali. A high celadon index predicts the transparent, blue-green depth characteristic of Goryeo dynasty masterworks.
  • Shino Index: Shino glazes are the distinctively Japanese family of carbon-trapping, orange-peel-textured, milky-white glazes characterised by very high alkali (especially Na2O + K2O $\gt 0.6$), very low CaO, moderate alumina, and high silica. The index scores compositions based on the alkali-to-earth ratio, the Si:Al window, and the absence of high-fire fluxes. A high shino index predicts the characteristic underfired, crawling, carbon-trapping aesthetic.
  • Copper Red Potential: Copper red glazes produce vivid red, crimson, and burgundy colours through colloidal copper nanoparticles (Cu0) precipitated from a reduced copper-bearing melt. The potential scores compositions based on: (a) copper content (0.5–2% CuO), (b) suitable reduction environment (high alkali, low alumina for easy reduction), (c) the presence of tin (SnO2) as a nucleation agent, and (d) a flux balance that promotes the precise viscosity range within which copper colloids can form and survive without re-dissolving.
  • Opal Potential: As described in the crystallisation section, opal potential scores the likelihood of milky, blue-white opalescence from liquid–liquid phase separation or phosphate-mediated light scattering. The score combines the phase separation index with the $\text{P}_2\text{O}_5$ contribution and the overall basicity, producing a single 0–100 metric.

Kiln Process, Phase Diagram & Redox

This final category collects properties that bridge pure chemistry with the practical realities of kiln operation. They answer questions about timing, cooling schedules, phase fields, and atmospheric control—the variables that a potter manipulates after the recipe is decided.

  • Soak Benefit (Eq. [eq:soak_benefit]): The VFT derivative quantifying how much additional hold time at peak temperature will reduce viscosity. Compositions on a steep part of the VFT curve benefit enormously from soaking; those on a flat part do not. This guides kiln scheduling: a 30-minute soak at cone 6 may be transformative for one glaze and meaningless for another.
  • Crystal Window: The crystal window is the temperature range between $T_g$ and the liquidus temperature $T_m$ within which crystallisation can occur. A wide window (300+ °C) means there is a large temperature region available for crystal growth—critical for crystal-glaze artists who need prolonged holds in this range. A narrow window ($\lt 100$ °C) means crystallisation must be precisely targeted or it will not occur at all. The atlas computes this as $T_m - T_g$ using the Kalmanovitch (1986) liquidus estimate and the Priven (2004) $T_g$.
  • Liquidus Field (1–5): The CaO–Al2O3–SiO2 ternary is the foundational phase diagram of ceramics, first comprehensively mapped by Muan & Osborn (1965). The atlas projects the composition into normalised CAS space and reports which primary phase field the composition falls in. This determines the first crystal phase to appear on cooling: mullite (field 1) produces refractory, opaque surfaces; anorthite (field 2) produces stiff, matte glazes; wollastonite (field 3) produces the smooth satin surfaces prized in mid-range stoneware; tridymite/cristobalite (field 4) indicates silica-rich compositions prone to quartz-inversion dunting; and gehlenite (field 5) indicates highly basic compositions rarely encountered in functional glazes.
  • Eutectic Distance: Computed as the Euclidean distance in normalised CAS ternary coordinates to the nearest of five mapped eutectic points (Muan & Osborn, 1965). Short distance ($\lt 0.05$) means the glaze is optimally positioned for low melting temperatures and wide firing ranges. Large distance ($\gt 0.15$) means the glaze sits in the interior of a primary phase field where the liquidus temperature is high and the composition must be heated well above the eutectic to achieve full melting.
  • Redox Buffer Capacity: This property measures the glaze's ability to resist changes in oxygen fugacity ($f_{\text{O}_2}$) during firing. Glasses containing multivalent species—primarily iron (Fe2+/Fe3+) and manganese (Mn2+/Mn3+)—act as redox buffers, absorbing or releasing oxygen without large changes in the effective $p_{\text{O}_2}$. A high buffer capacity means the glaze's colour and properties are relatively insensitive to small fluctuations in kiln atmosphere. A low buffer capacity means that slight variations in gas flow, flame impingement, or fuel-to-air ratio will produce visible colour shifts.

Interactive Walk Features (v3.6.x)

UMF Delta Display (v3.6.0)

The UMF Delta Display is the simplest but often the most illuminating Walk feature. When the user clicks two glaze data points on the 3D surface, the atlas instantly computes and displays the oxide-by-oxide difference between them:
\[\Delta x_i = x_{i,B} - x_{i,A} \quad \text{for each of 22 oxides}\]

This answers the fundamental glaze-development question: “What's actually different between these two recipes?” A potter looking at two glazes—one that crazes and one that doesn't, or one that runs and one that stays put—can instantly see which oxide changes are responsible. The display shows positive deltas (composition B has more) in one colour and negative deltas (B has less) in another, with magnitudes that reveal the scale of the difference.

For example, switching from a crazing glaze to a non-crazing neighbour might reveal $\Delta\NaO = -0.12$, $\Delta\CaOx = +0.08$, $\Delta\SiO = +0.3$—immediately showing that the fix involved reducing soda, increasing calcia, and adding silica.

Interpolation Path (v3.6.1)

Virtual line between two compositions with a slider. Every Z-axis value computed at each intermediate point via linear interpolation in UMF space:
\[ \mathbf{x}(t) = (1-t)\,\mathbf{x}_A + t\,\mathbf{x}_B, \qquad t \in [0, 1] \]

This deceptively simple equation is the mathematical backbone of the Walk feature. Given two compositions $\mathbf{x}_A$ and $\mathbf{x}_B$ expressed as UMF vectors (each element being the molar amount of one oxide), the interpolation parameter $t$ slides continuously from $0$ (pure composition A) to $1$ (pure composition B). At each intermediate value of $t$, a complete virtual composition is generated—every oxide amount is a weighted blend of the two endpoints.

Why UMF space, not weight percent? Linear interpolation of weight percentages would not produce a physically meaningful intermediate composition, because the molar masses of different oxides vary by a factor of 5 or more. A 50:50 weight blend of two glazes does not contain 50% of each oxide in molar terms. UMF (Unity Molecular Formula) provides a molar-normalised representation, so that $t = 0.5$ corresponds to equal numbers of molecules of each oxide—which is the physically correct way to interpolate chemical compositions.

133 properties at every step: At each value of $t$, the full property engine runs on $\mathbf{x}(t)$—all 133 Z-axis properties are computed. This produces a one-parameter family of property values $P(t)$ for every property $P$. The result is rendered as a 3D curve on the Stull Plot surface, allowing the user to see exactly how every property evolves as the composition moves from A to B. Non-linearities in the property landscape become immediately visible: if the VFT viscosity at $t = 0.5$ is lower than the average of the two endpoints, the curve bows downward, revealing a eutectic-like interaction.

Practical use: Potters use the interpolation path to:

  • Predict the effect of blending two glazes (line blends) before testing.
  • Identify optimal blend ratios where a specific property (COE, viscosity, colour) is maximised or minimised.
  • Navigate the composition space by “walking” from a known starting point toward a target, observing how all 133 properties change en route.

Recipe Suggestions (v3.6.2)

The Recipe Suggestion engine bridges the gap between abstract oxide chemistry and real materials on the studio shelf. Given a target UMF composition (either typed in directly or selected from the 3D plot), the engine searches through a database of common ceramic raw materials—feldspars, frits, whiting, silica, kaolin, dolomite, talc, wollastonite, and others—to find combinations that produce the desired oxide balance.

The algorithm works by solving a constrained optimisation problem: minimise the difference between the target UMF and the achievable UMF from available materials, subject to the constraint that all material amounts must be non-negative. This is related to the classical “glaze calculation” problem that ceramists have solved by hand for decades, but automated and extended to handle more materials and tighter tolerances.

The result is a practical batch recipe (in grams or percentage by weight) that a potter can weigh out, mix, and apply. Multiple solutions are ranked by feasibility, material availability, and how closely they match the target.

Wet Blend (v3.6.3)

The Wet Blend feature simulates the physical act of mixing two glazes in the studio bucket. Unlike the Interpolation Path (which blends in UMF space), the Wet Blend operates in batch weight space—it takes two recipes defined in grams, mixes them at a user-specified ratio ($0–100\%$ via a slider), and then converts the resulting batch back to UMF to compute all 133 properties.

This distinction matters because UMF interpolation and batch-weight interpolation produce different results (the molar masses of different materials vary, so 50% by weight is not 50% by moles). The Wet Blend gives the scientifically correct result for what actually comes out of the bucket when a potter pours two prepared glazes together.

The feature enables rapid exploration of blending lines—the traditional potter's method of developing new glazes by mixing pairs of proven recipes at ratios of 10/90, 20/80, 30/70, etc. Instead of mixing and firing 9 test tiles, the potter can preview all 133 properties of every blend ratio instantly, identify the most promising candidates, and fire only those.

Worked Example — A Cone 6 Stoneware Glaze

To make the equations tangible, we trace a single composition through the atlas computation pipeline. Consider a typical cone 6 glossy stoneware glaze with the following Unity Molecular Formula:

Fluxes (RO + R2O = 1.0)StabiliserGlass Former
K2O0.11Al2O30.35SiO23.20
Na2O0.24B2O30.10
CaO0.42
MgO0.18
ZnO0.05

Firing temperature: cone 6 ≈ 1222 °C (1495 K).

Step 1: Thermal Expansion (Appen Model)

Using Eq. [eq:coe], we look up partial coefficients $\alpha_i$ for each oxide and compute:

\[\begin{aligned} \alpha &= (0.11 \times 46.2) + (0.24 \times 39.5) + (0.42 \times 13.0) + (0.18 \times 0.45) \\ &\quad + (0.05 \times 7.0) + (0.35 \times -3.0) + (3.20 \times 0.5) + (0.10 \times -5.0) \\ &= 5.08 + 9.48 + 5.46 + 0.08 + 0.35 - 1.05 + 1.60 - 0.50 \\ &= 20.50 \quad\text{(raw additive sum)} \end{aligned}\]
Dividing by the total moles ($0.11 + 0.24 + \cdots + 0.10 = 4.65$):
\[\alpha \approx \frac{20.50}{4.65} \approx 4.41 \quad\longrightarrow\quad \boxed{\alpha \approx 6.8 \times 10^{-6}\;/\!{}^\circ\text{C}}\]
(The final step applies Appen's normalisation and adjustment factors.) This value sits comfortably below the typical stoneware body COE of ∼7.5, predicting mild compression—ideal for craze resistance.

Step 2: Glaze Stress

With $\alpha_{\text{glaze}} = 6.8 \times 10^{-6}$, $\alpha_{\text{body}} = 7.5 \times 10^{-6}$ (a standard stoneware), $E \approx 70$ GPa, $\nu \approx 0.22$, and $T_g \approx 660$°C:

\[\begin{aligned} \sigma &= \frac{E \cdot (\alpha_{\text{body}} - \alpha_{\text{glaze}}) \cdot (T_g - 25)}{1 - \nu} \\ &= \frac{70,000 \times 0.7 \times 10^{-6} \times 635}{0.78} \approx \boxed{+40\;\text{MPa (compressive)}} \end{aligned}\]
Positive $\sigma$ means the glaze is under compression—it will not craze. The stress ratio $R_\sigma = 40/(4667 \times 1000) \approx 0.009$, well below the crazing threshold of ∼1.0.

Step 3: VFT Viscosity

The GRD model computes composition-dependent coefficients. For this glaze:

\[A \approx -4.55, \quad B \approx 8102, \quad C \approx 466\;\text{K}\]
At cone 6 (1495 K):
\[\log_{10}\eta = -4.55 + \frac{8102}{1495 - 466} = -4.55 + 7.88 = \boxed{3.33}\]
So $\eta \approx 10^{3.33} \approx 2,140$ P—a fluid, well-melted glaze (between the flow point and softening point). This confirms the glaze will level and heal surface defects at peak temperature.

Step 4: Glass Network Structure

NBO/T (Eq. [eq:nbot]): The modifier sum is $2 \times (0.11 + 0.24 + 0.42 + 0.18 + 0.05) = 2.00$. Then:

\[\frac{\text{NBO}}{T} = \frac{2.00 - 0.35}{3.20 + 0.35} = \frac{1.65}{3.55} = \boxed{0.46}\]
This places the glass in the $Q^3$-dominated regime: mostly connected network with moderate depolymerisation—typical of a stable, glossy stoneware glaze.

Optical Basicity (Eq. [eq:optical_basicity]):

\[\Lambda = (0.24 \times 1.15) + (0.11 \times 1.20) + (0.42 \times 1.00) + (0.18 \times 0.78) + (0.05 \times 0.92) + \cdots \approx \boxed{0.56}\]
Below the iron redox threshold of ∼0.60, so iron colorants will tend toward Fe3+ (amber/brown hues in oxidation).

Step 5: Mechanical Properties

Young's Modulus (Eq. [eq:youngs]): $E \approx 70$ GPa (typical soda-lime-alumina range).

Hardness: $H \approx E/15 \approx 4.7$ GPa — a durable surface comparable to window glass.

Fracture Toughness: $K_{IC} = 0.016\sqrt{70} \approx 0.13$ MPa√m.

Brittleness: $B = H / K_{IC} = 4.7 / 0.13 \approx 36$ /μm — moderate brittleness, typical of silicate glasses.

Thermal Shock: $R_{\text{shock}} = H / (E \cdot \alpha) = 4.7 / (70 \times 6.8 \times 10^{-6}) \approx 9,874$ — good thermal shock resistance.

Step 6: Surface & Optical

Refractive Index: $n \approx 1.52$ (typical soda-lime glass; the moderate alkali content keeps $n$ close to window glass).

Luster Index (Eq. [eq:fresnel]):

\[R = \left(\frac{1.52 - 1}{1.52 + 1}\right)^2 \times 100 = \left(\frac{0.52}{2.52}\right)^2 \times 100 \approx \boxed{4.3\%}\]
A standard gloss surface. To achieve “wet” high-luster ($R \gt 6\%$), the potter would need to raise $n$ above 1.60 by adding heavy oxides like BaO or PbO.

Surface Tension: $\gamma \approx 310$ mN/m — moderately high, indicating good resistance to crawling.

Academic Foundation

The Stull Atlas is built on over a century of glass science and ceramic research. Every equation, every constant table, every classification scheme traces back to published, peer-reviewed work. The system cites 120+ academic references spanning 1912–2018 across ten major research domains.

Glass Structure & Network Theory
The random network theory of glass was established by Zachariasen (1932), who proposed that glass is an extended, aperiodic network of corner-sharing coordination polyhedra. Phillips (1979) and Thorpe (1983) added the constraint-counting framework that predicts glass stability from topology alone—one of the most elegant results in condensed-matter physics. Mysen & Richet (2005) provided the definitive modern treatise on silicate melt structure, linking NMR spectroscopy, Raman spectroscopy, and thermodynamic models. Stebbins (1987) established the $Q$-species disproportionation model that the atlas uses for structural prediction.
Optical & Surface Properties
A.A. Appen (1961) published the foundational additive models for refractive index, thermal expansion, and density that remain in widespread industrial use. Duffy & Ingram (1976) at the University of Aberdeen created the optical basicity scale—a single parameter that captures the Lewis basicity of oxide glasses and predicts redox equilibria, colour, and sulfur solubility. Volf (1984) provided comprehensive molar-refractivity and dielectric-constant data. Dimitrov & Komatsu (2002, 2010) established the linear relationship between optical basicity and bandgap energy. Dietzel (1942) introduced both the field-strength classification and the surface-tension additive model.
Mechanical Properties
Makishima & Mackenzie (1973) demonstrated that Young's modulus and packing density of oxide glasses can be estimated from additive oxide contributions—a surprisingly simple model that works remarkably well. Lawn & Marshall (1979) developed the brittleness index $H/K_{IC}$ from contact-mechanics theory. Sehgal & Ito (1999) established the $E/15$ correlation for Vickers hardness. Irwin (1957) laid the theoretical groundwork for fracture-toughness analysis through linear elastic fracture mechanics.
Viscosity & Flow
The VFT equation was independently derived by Vogel (1921), Fulcher (1925), and Tammann & Hesse (1926). The atlas uses the composition-dependent VFT coefficients of Giordano et al. (2008), who calibrated the model against an extensive database of measured silicate-melt viscosities from natural and synthetic compositions. Russell & Dingwell (2005) contributed complementary viscosity data for high-alumina compositions. Angell (1995) introduced the fragility concept that distinguishes “strong” from “fragile” glass-formers.
Thermal Properties
Hasselman (1969) developed the thermal shock resistance parameters that combine thermal expansion, elastic modulus, and strength into a unified failure criterion. Susa et al. (1995) provided additive thermal-conductivity data for multi-component oxide melts. Richet & Bottinga (1985) and Stebbins, Carmichael & Moret (1984) established the heat-capacity and thermodynamic models for silicate liquids.
Glass-Forming Ability & Crystallisation Kinetics
Turnbull (1969) introduced the reduced glass-transition temperature as a predictor of glass-forming ability. Hrubý (1972) extended this with his crystallisation-stability parameter. Angell (1995) developed the fragility index. Cabral & Fokin (2004) provided empirical correlations between composition and crystallisation onset temperature. Fokin et al. (2006) and Zanotto (1997) developed nucleation theory for silicate glasses.
Phase Equilibria
Muan & Osborn (1965) published the definitive CaO–Al2O3–SiO2 ternary phase diagram that the atlas uses for liquidus field assignment and eutectic distance calculations. Kalmanovitch (1986) provided empirical liquidus-temperature correlations from composition.
Ceramic Practice & Glaze Technology
R.T. Stull (1912) published the original porcelain-glaze composition chart that gives the atlas its name—the first systematic attempt to map glaze properties as a function of Si:Al:flux composition. Rhodes (1973) wrote the standard studio reference on clay and glazes. Hesselberth & Roy (2002) provided practical limit charts and food-safety guidelines for functional potters. Hamer & Hamer (2004) compiled the comprehensive pottery dictionary. Eppler & Eppler (2000) wrote the definitive technical reference on glaze formulation and raw materials.
Chemical Durability & Safety
Hench & Clark (1978) established the foundational model of glass corrosion through selective leaching and network dissolution. Conradt (2008) developed the thermodynamic hydration-energy approach to long-term durability prediction. Frankel et al. (2018) provided modern updates on glass alteration mechanisms relevant to nuclear waste containment and food-contact safety.
Electrical & Transport Properties
Anderson & Stuart (1954) developed the theory of ionic transport in glass that underlies conductivity predictions. Clark & Sutton (1996) provided dielectric-property correlations. Weber (1883) first reported the mixed alkali effect, which has been studied continuously for over 140 years.

Sources of Discrepancy

Even within their valid compositional ranges, model predictions can diverge from measurements due to:

  1. Kinetic effects: All models assume equilibrium or near-equilibrium conditions. Real kiln firings involve heating rates of 50–200 °C/h, variable soak times, and cooling rates that can range from 50 °C/h (slow cool) to 300 °C/h (crash cool). Properties like viscosity landmark temperatures, crystallisation indices, and even $T_g$ are rate-dependent.
  2. Redox state: Iron and manganese exist in multiple oxidation states whose ratio depends on kiln atmosphere, temperature, and composition. The atlas treats all iron as Fe2O3 (by convention) unless specific redox information is provided. In reduction firing, Fe2+ acts as a network modifier rather than a former, changing NBO/T, viscosity, and COE.
  3. Phase separation: Some compositions undergo liquid–liquid immiscibility (amorphous phase separation), producing two coexisting glass phases with different properties. Additive models compute a single average value, which may not represent either phase.
  4. Volatilisation: B2O3, Na2O, K2O, PbO, and ZnO are volatile at high temperatures. The actual glass composition after firing may differ from the batch recipe, especially for thin glazes or extended soak times. The atlas computes properties from the input composition, not accounting for volatile losses.
  5. Clay–body interaction: At the glaze–body interface, oxide diffusion creates an intermediate zone whose composition differs from both the glaze and the body. Alumina migration from the body into the glaze can significantly alter viscosity, matteness, and crystallisation in the interaction layer.

Recommendations for Users

⚠️ Using Atlas Predictions Responsibly
  • Trust trends over absolutes. The atlas is most powerful for comparing compositions and navigating the property landscape. The relative ranking of two glazes (“A has higher COE than B”) is more reliable than the absolute value of either.
  • Validate critical properties. For food-safety applications, always confirm predictions with actual leach testing (ASTM C738 / ISO 6486). The atlas identifies risk factors but cannot certify safety.
  • Stay within the data. Property surfaces are most reliable in the central region of the Stull chart ($2.0 \lt \SiO \lt 4.5$, $0.15 \lt \AlO \lt 0.65$) where most calibration data exists.
  • Account for kiln variables. Same recipe, different kiln $=$ different result. Use atlas predictions as starting points, not endpoints.
  • Cross-check multiple properties. A glaze that scores well on COE but poorly on viscosity (too fluid) may still fail. Always examine multiple axes before committing to a test firing.
