Defect Physics

Introduction to Glaze Defects

Every glaze defect is a message from the physics. Crazing says the thermal expansion is too high. Crawling says the surface tension is too high relative to the melt's ability to wet the body. Pinholing says gases were trapped. Understanding defects through the Atlas equations transforms troubleshooting from guesswork into engineering.

This chapter covers the six major glaze defects: crazing, shivering, crawling, pinholing/blistering, crawling, and dunting. For each defect, we describe the physics, identify the controlling Atlas equations, and provide diagnostic flowcharts.

Crazing

What It Is

Crazing is a network of fine cracks in the glaze surface. The cracks do not penetrate the clay body ‚Äî they exist entirely within the glaze layer. Crazing may appear immediately after cooling, hours later, or even months/years later (‚Äúdelayed crazing‚Äù).

The Physics

Crazing occurs when the glaze is in tension ‚Äî when it contracts more during cooling than the underlying body. The glaze ‚Äúwants‚Äù to be smaller than the body, and when the tensile stress exceeds the glaze's fracture strength, it cracks.

‚ö° Glaze Stress from Thermal Mismatch
\[ \sigma_{\text{glaze}} = \frac{E_g}{1 - \nu_g}\,(\alpha_g - \alpha_b)\,(T_{\text{set}} - T_{\text{room}}) \]
where:
  • $E_g$: Elastic modulus of the glaze (Atlas Eq. 16, typically 60‚Äì80 GPa)
  • $\nu_g$: Poisson's ratio of the glaze (Atlas Eq. 17, typically 0.20‚Äì0.25)
  • $\alpha_g$: Coefficient of thermal expansion of the glaze (Atlas Eq. 6)
  • $\alpha_b$: Coefficient of thermal expansion of the body
  • $T_{\text{set}}$: Temperature at which glaze becomes rigid ($\approx T_g + 50$¬∞C)
  • $\sigma \gt 0$: Tension (crazing risk). $\sigma \lt 0$: Compression (shivering risk).

Crazing occurs when $\alpha_g \gt \alpha_b$. The glaze contracts more than the body.

The Role of COE (Atlas Eq. 6)

The Atlas calculates COE using an additive model:

\[\alpha_g = \sum_i x_i \cdot \alpha_i\]
where $x_i$ is the mole fraction of oxide $i$ and $\alpha_i$ is its expansion factor.

Oxide contributions to expansion:

Crazing Diagnostic Flowchart

‚ÑπÔ∏è Crazing Troubleshooting
  1. Calculate $\alpha_g$ from Atlas Eq. 6. Is $\alpha_g \gt \alpha_b$?
    • Yes ‚Üí Confirmed: thermal mismatch. Go to step 2.
    • No ‚Üí Crazing may be from moisture expansion of the body (see step 5).
  2. Reduce $\alpha_g$: Replace Na2O with K2O (slightly lower $\alpha$), or better, increase SiO2 or Al2O3.
  3. Or increase $\alpha_b$: Add flux to the body (quartz, feldspar).
  4. Target $\alpha_g - \alpha_b \approx -0.5$ to $-1.0 \times 10^{-6}$ /K: The glaze should be in slight compression, not exactly matched.
  5. Delayed crazing? The body may be absorbing moisture from the environment, causing it to expand over time. If $\alpha_g \approx \alpha_b$, even small moisture expansion breaks the balance. Increase the compression margin or fire the body harder (reduce porosity).

Shivering (Spalling)

What It Is

Shivering is the opposite of crazing: the glaze is in excessive compression and flakes or chips off the body. It typically manifests as curved flakes popping off rims and edges.

The Physics

$\alpha_g \ll \alpha_b$: The glaze contracts less than the body during cooling, putting the glaze under compression. A small amount of compression (<30 MPa) is desirable (it strengthens the ware). But if the compressive stress exceeds the glaze-body adhesion strength (‚àº50‚Äì100 MPa), the glaze delaminates.

Fixes

  1. Increase $\alpha_g$: Add more alkali flux (Na2O, K2O, Li2O)
  2. Decrease $\alpha_b$: Use a body with more silica or less feldspar
  3. Check: Was the body over-fired? Over-fired bodies contract more (higher $\alpha_b$), pushing the glaze into compression.

Crawling

What It Is

Crawling is the withdrawal of the molten glaze from areas of the surface, exposing bare clay. The glaze ‚Äúbeads up‚Äù like water on a waxed surface.

The Physics

Crawling occurs when the surface tension of the molten glaze ($\gamma$, Atlas Eq. 26) is too high relative to its wetting ability. The balance is:

‚ö° Crawling Condition
\[ \gamma_{LV}\cos\theta \gt \gamma_{SL} \]
where $\gamma_{LV}$ is the liquid-vapor surface tension, $\theta$ is the contact angle, and $\gamma_{SL}$ is the solid-liquid interfacial tension. When the glaze-body contact angle exceeds ‚àº90¬∞, the glaze de-wets.

Common causes:

  1. Dusty or contaminated bisque: Dust, oils, or wax prevent wetting. Always clean bisqueware.
  2. High surface tension: MgO and Al2O3 increase $\gamma$. Compositions with $\gt 0.3$ MgO or $\gt 0.5$ Al2O3 in UMF are crawling-prone.
  3. Raw material issues: Raw calcined materials (especially zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) applied dry can shrink dramatically during firing, cracking the unfired glaze layer and creating stress points that promote crawling.
  4. Thick application: Very thick glaze layers develop drying cracks that become crawling initiation sites.
  5. Under-sinter of the bisque: Low bisque temperatures (<900 ¬∞C) leave the body too porous, absorbing too much glaze water and destabilising the raw glaze layer.

üî¨ Atlas Equation for Surface Tension
The Stull Atlas Eq. 26 calculates surface tension using an additive model:
\[\gamma = \sum_i x_i \cdot \gamma_i\]

Oxides that increase surface tension (crawling risk): Al2O3, MgO, CaO, SiO2.

Oxides that decrease surface tension (crawling resistance): PbO, B2O3, Na2O, K2O, Li2O.

A practical threshold: $\gamma \gt 350$ mN/m at peak temperature signals crawling risk.

Pinholing & Blistering

What It Is

Pinholing: small, rounded holes in the glaze surface where gas bubbles reached the surface but the glaze failed to heal (flow together and smooth over). Blistering: larger, raised bubbles that never broke or broke but left a crater.

The Physics

During firing, multiple gas sources produce bubbles in the glaze:

  • CO2 from carbonate decomposition (CaCO3 ‚Üí CaO + CO2)
  • SO2 from sulfate decomposition
  • H2O from dehydroxylation of clay minerals
  • CO from organic burnout or reduction atmosphere
  • O2 from iron and manganese redox reactions

These gases must escape before the glaze becomes too viscous to heal. The critical competition:

‚ö° Pinholing Condition
The time for a bubble to heal after breaking at the surface:
\[ t_{\text{heal}} \propto \frac{\eta}{\gamma} \]
Pinholing occurs when $t_{\text{heal}} \gt t_{\text{available}}$ ‚Äî the glaze becomes too viscous to flow before all bubbles have escaped and healed.

Atlas Connection

The key Atlas equations:

  • VFT viscosity (Eq. 11): Lower viscosity at peak ‚Üí faster healing. Target $\log\eta \lt 4.5$ at peak temperature.
  • Surface tension (Eq. 26): Higher $\gamma$ ‚Üí faster healing (surface tension pulls the glaze closed). But too-high $\gamma$ causes crawling.
  • Gas solubility: Not directly modelled, but correlated with NBO/T (Eq. 14): more depolymerised melts dissolve more gas.

Pinholing Diagnostic

‚ö†Ô∏è Pinholing Troubleshooting
  1. Are the pinholes concentrated on thick areas?
    • Yes ‚Üí Body gas source. The thicker the glaze, the longer the gas path and the later gas arrives at the surface.
    • Fix: Higher bisque temperature to burn out organics/carbonates before glazing.
  2. Are the pinholes uniform across the surface?
    • Yes ‚Üí Glaze viscosity too high at peak. Check VFT viscosity.
    • Fix: Add more flux, reduce Al2O3, extend soak at peak temperature.
  3. Are there large blisters rather than pinholes?
    • Yes ‚Üí Gas generation at or above peak temperature (late sulfate decomposition or reduction bubbles).
    • Fix: Oxidation soak before reduction. Use materials with lower sulfur content.

Dunting

What It Is

Dunting is the cracking of the entire pot (body + glaze) during cooling, typically producing clean, sharp-edged breaks. It is not a glaze defect per se ‚Äî it is a body failure, but glaze stress contributes.

The Physics

Dunting occurs at specific temperatures where rapid volume changes occur:

  • 573 ¬∞C: Quartz inversion ($\alpha$-quartz ‚Üí $\beta$-quartz). Volume change: ‚àº0.45%. This is the most dangerous temperature during cooling. If the kiln cools too fast through this zone, free quartz particles in the body undergo a sudden contraction (exothermic $\beta \to \alpha$), creating internal stress fractures.
  • 226 ¬∞C: Cristobalite inversion ($\alpha \to \beta$). Volume change: ‚àº3%. Only relevant if the body contains significant cristobalite (over-fired, or high free silica).

Prevention

  1. Cool slowly through $600‚Äì500$¬∞C: Maximum 50¬∞C/h for thick-walled ware, 100¬∞C/h for thin ware.
  2. Distribute the quartz: Use finer-ground silica (smaller particles undergo the inversion with less stress concentration). Or convert free quartz to mullite by firing higher.
  3. The glaze contribution: If $\alpha_g \gg \alpha_b$, the glaze adds additional tensile stress during cooling, increasing dunting risk. Atlas COE (Eq. 6) helps here.

Property Sensitivity & Optimisation

The Sensitivity Question

The potter's fundamental question: ‚ÄúIf I change the amount of oxide $X$ by some small amount, how much does property $Y$ change?‚Äù This is a question about partial derivatives ‚Äî the calculus of glaze optimisation.

The Jacobian Matrix

For a glaze with $n$ oxides and $m$ properties, the complete sensitivity information is captured in the Jacobian matrix:

‚ö° Jacobian Matrix of Glaze Properties
\[ \mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial P_1}{\partial x_1} & \frac{\partial P_1}{\partial x_2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial P_1}{\partial x_n} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial P_2}{\partial x_1} & \frac{\partial P_2}{\partial x_2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial P_2}{\partial x_n} \\[6pt] \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\[6pt] \frac{\partial P_m}{\partial x_1} & \frac{\partial P_m}{\partial x_2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial P_m}{\partial x_n} \end{pmatrix} \]
where $P_j$ is property $j$ (COE, viscosity, durability, etc.) and $x_i$ is the mole fraction of oxide $i$.

For the Stull Atlas with 12+ oxides and 40 equations, this is a $40 \times 12$ matrix with 480 entries. The Atlas can compute every entry analytically (since most equations are additive models, the partial derivatives are simply the coefficients).

Sensitivity Heatmap

üî¨ Reading the Sensitivity Heatmap
Imagine a colour-coded table: rows are properties (COE, $T_g$, viscosity, durability, $\Lambda$, etc.); columns are oxides (SiO2, Al2O3, Na2O, K2O, CaO, MgO, etc.). Each cell is coloured by the magnitude and sign of $\partial P / \partial x$:
  • Bright red (large positive): Increasing this oxide strongly increases this property
  • Bright blue (large negative): Increasing this oxide strongly decreases this property
  • White/gray (near zero): This oxide has little effect on this property

Key patterns that emerge:

  1. Na2O has large positive sensitivity for COE, large negative for viscosity, moderate positive for optical basicity ‚Äî it is a ‚Äústrong lever‚Äù that moves many properties simultaneously. Adjusting Na2O is powerful but dangerous: it changes everything at once.
  2. SiO2 has moderate negative sensitivity for most properties (COE, $\Lambda$, NBO/T) and positive for hardness, durability, $T_g$. It is the ‚Äústabiliser‚Äù ‚Äî more silica makes nearly everything more conservative.
  3. Al2O3 has small sensitivities in most additive models but enormous structural effects (2:1 NBO/T lever, viscosity increase). It is the ‚Äústructural enforcer.‚Äù
  4. MgO has surprisingly small effects on most bulk properties but large effects on surface tension and crystallisation tendency. It is a ‚Äúhidden influencer.‚Äù

Gradient-Descent Recipe Design

The Optimisation Problem

Given:

  • A starting composition $\mathbf{x}_0 = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$
  • A set of target properties $\mathbf{P}^* = (P_1^*, P_2^*, \ldots, P_m^*)$

Find the composition $\mathbf{x}$ that minimises:

‚ö° Optimisation Objective
\[ L(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^{m} w_j \left(\frac{P_j(\mathbf{x}) - P_j^*}{P_j^*}\right)^2 \]
subject to constraints:
\[\begin{aligned} \sum_i x_i &= 1 \quad \text{(composition sums to 1)} \\ x_i &\geq 0 \quad \text{(no negative oxides)} \\ x_i^{\min} \leq x_i &\leq x_i^{\max} \quad \text{(practical oxide limits)} \end{aligned}\]
where $w_j$ are user-assigned importance weights.

The Gradient

The gradient of $L$ with respect to composition:

\[\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} L = \mathbf{J}^T \cdot \mathbf{r}\]
where $\mathbf{J}$ is the Jacobian and $\mathbf{r}$ is the residual vector.

The composition update step:

\[\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k - \alpha_k \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} L\]

This is the foundation of automated recipe optimisation: the Atlas equations provide $\mathbf{J}$, the user provides the targets, and gradient descent (or a more sophisticated algorithm like L-BFGS or Nelder‚ÄìMead) finds the optimal composition.

Pareto Frontiers

Many property pairs are fundamentally in conflict:

  • Low COE (no crazing) vs. low viscosity (good healing)
  • High durability vs. low firing temperature
  • High hardness vs. low surface tension (no crawling)

No single composition can optimise both simultaneously. The Pareto frontier is the set of compositions where no property can be improved without degrading another:

‚ÑπÔ∏è Pareto Frontier
A composition is Pareto-optimal if there exists no other feasible composition that is better in all objectives. The collection of all Pareto-optimal points forms the Pareto frontier ‚Äî a curve (in 2D) or surface (in higher dimensions) that represents the fundamental trade-off boundary.

Example: For COE vs. viscosity at cone 6, the Pareto frontier might run from (COE = $5.5 \times 10^{-6}$, $\log\eta = 5.0$) to (COE = $8.5 \times 10^{-6}$, $\log\eta = 2.5$). Every point on this curve is optimal in the sense that you cannot lower COE without raising viscosity, and vice versa. The potter chooses their preferred trade-off along this curve based on priorities.

Firing Schedule Science

The VFT-Derived Firing Profile

The VFT equation (Atlas Eq. 11) gives viscosity as a function of temperature:

\[\log_{10}\eta(T) = A + \frac{B}{T - T_0}\]

This single equation encodes the entire temperature‚Äìfluidity relationship of the glaze. By inverting it, we can calculate the temperatures at which specific viscosity milestones occur:

Softening (Littleton)$6.65$Glass deforms under own weight

The Optimal Soak Temperature

For a glaze to mature properly, the peak viscosity should be in the range $\log\eta = 3.0‚Äì5.0$. Below 3.0, the glaze runs off. Above 5.0, it may not heal pinholes.

The Atlas reports the VFT parameters ($A$, $B$, $T_0$), allowing the potter to calculate the exact temperature at which the glaze reaches any target viscosity:

‚ö° Inverting VFT for Target Viscosity
\[ T(\log\eta^*) = T_0 + \frac{B}{\log\eta^* - A} \]

Soak Optimisation

Why Soak?

A soak (hold at peak temperature) serves three purposes:

  1. Equalisation: Kiln temperature is never perfectly uniform. A soak allows cooler spots to catch up. With typical kiln gradients of 20 ¬∞C‚Äì50 ¬∞C between top and bottom, a 15‚Äì30 minute soak brings all zones to within 5 ¬∞C.
  2. Bubble healing: Gas bubbles must reach the surface, break, and the glaze must flow to heal the crater. The healing time (Eq. [eq:healing]) depends on $\eta/\gamma$. A longer soak at peak gives more healing time.
  3. Dissolution: Refractory materials (quartz grains, alumina lumps, zircon) need time to dissolve into the melt. Undissolved particles cause surface defects and optical scattering.

Drop-and-Soak vs. Traditional Soak

Modern kiln controllers enable ‚Äúdrop-and-soak‚Äù schedules:

  1. Fire to peak temperature and hold briefly (5‚Äì10 minutes)
  2. Drop 50 ¬∞C‚Äì100 ¬∞C rapidly
  3. Hold at the lower temperature for 30‚Äì60 minutes

The advantage: at the lower temperature, viscosity is higher, so the glaze does not run. But it is still fluid enough ($\log\eta \lt 6$) to heal pinholes. This is particularly effective for glazes that tend to run at peak but have pinholing issues.

The Atlas VFT equation calculates the exact viscosity at the drop temperature, confirming that it is in the healing range ($\log\eta = 4‚Äì6$).

The atmosphere in a fuel-burning kiln varies dramatically with time and location:

Effect on Atlas Properties

  • Optical basicity ($\Lambda$): Not directly affected by atmosphere (it is a compositional property), but the effective $\Lambda$ experienced by a redox-sensitive ion depends on the local electron availability. Reduction effectively increases the electron donation to the cation.
  • Viscosity: Fe2+ is a weaker network modifier than Fe3+ (it tends to sit in network-modifying sites in either case, but with subtle structural effects). Heavy reduction can slightly lower viscosity in iron-bearing glazes.
  • Color: As described extensively in the companion colorant report, atmosphere controls the Fe2+/Fe3+ ratio, Cu2+/Cu+/Cu0 balance, and Mn2+/Mn3+ equilibrium.
  • Surface quality: Reduction atmosphere generates CO/CO2 gas at the glaze surface. If reduction begins too early (before the glaze has melted and sealed), carbon deposits trap beneath the glaze surface, causing blistering and gray/black discoloration (‚Äúcarbon coring‚Äù or ‚Äúbloating‚Äù).

Consequences

Volatile loss changes the surface composition relative to the bulk. The surface becomes enriched in refractory, non-volatile components (SiO2, Al2O3, CaO) and depleted in volatile flux. This creates a ‚Äúsurface skin‚Äù with:

  • Higher viscosity than the bulk
  • Higher $T_g$ than the bulk
  • Different COE than the bulk (possible surface crazing)
  • Matte or dry appearance even though the bulk glass is fully melted

In extreme cases, the volatile-depleted surface layer devitrifies (cristobalite, wollastonite) while the interior remains glassy.

Energy Cost Modelling

The Energy‚ÄìTemperature Relationship

The total energy required to fire a glaze is approximately:

\[ E_{\text{total}} = m \cdot c_p \cdot (T_{\text{peak}} - T_{\text{room}}) + m \cdot \sum_i \Delta H_i \]
where the first term is sensible heat and the second includes endothermic reactions (combustion of organics, dehydroxylation, carbonate decomposition, melting).

For a typical stoneware kiln load (500 ), the energy cost of a cone 6 firing (1222 ¬∞C) is approximately 200‚Äì400 kWh (electric) or 30‚Äì60 ¬≥m natural gas. Increasing the firing temperature by 50 ¬∞C increases energy consumption by approximately 5‚Äì8%.

Low-Fire Design via Atlas

The Atlas can identify compositions that achieve target properties at lower temperatures by:

  1. Using VFT inversion (Eq. [eq:vft_invert]) to find compositions where $\log\eta = 4$ occurs at lower $T$
  2. Increasing flux content (especially Li2O, B2O3, Na2O) to depress the melting point
  3. Checking that lower-temperature compositions still meet durability and COE targets

  • Optical basicity ($\Lambda$): $\approx 0.54‚Äì0.56$. Moderate basicity. High enough to support Fe2+ in heavy reduction but not so high as to promote excessive iron reduction.
  • NBO/T: $\approx 0.6‚Äì0.8$. Moderately polymerised ‚Äî contributes to the viscous, ‚Äúthick‚Äù quality of the melt.
  • VFT viscosity at 1280 ¬∞C: $\log\eta \approx 3.5‚Äì4.5$. Appropriately fluid but not runny. The glaze self-levels but does not run off.
  • COE: $\approx 6.5‚Äì7.5 \times 10^{-6}$/¬∞C. Well-matched to the porcelain body ($\alpha_b \approx 7.0$).
  • Fe2+/Fe3+ ratio: At $\Lambda = 0.55$ and heavy reduction ($\log p_{\text{O}_2} \approx -12$), the Fe2+ fraction is ‚àº0.6‚Äì0.7. The Fe2+ $d‚Äìd$ band absorbs red, transmitting blue-green celadon.
  • P2O5 role: The 0.5‚Äì2% P2O5 from wood ash induces nanoscale phase separation, producing a subtle opalescence that gives Longquan celadons their distinctive ‚Äúdepth‚Äù and ‚Äúglow.‚Äù

üî¨ Why Longquan Has ‚ÄúJade‚Äù Quality
The jade metaphor is not merely poetic. Jade (nephrite, Ca2(Mg,Fe)5Si8O22(OH)2) is a fibrous silicate with a semi-translucent, waxy quality. Longquan celadon achieves a similar visual effect through:
  1. Thick glaze layers (1‚Äì3 mm) that create an optical path for Fe2+ absorption
  2. Phosphate-induced opalescence that scatters light within the glaze bulk, creating a luminous interior glow
  3. Surface smoothness from optimal viscosity at peak temperature
  4. Slight opacity that prevents clear-through transparency, giving the appearance of ‚Äúdepth‚Äù
No single Atlas equation captures this quality, but the combination of VFT viscosity (controls thickness and smoothness), refractive index (light interaction), and phase separation (opalescence) together predict it.

Islamic Lustre Ware (9th‚Äì14th Century CE)

Lustre ware represents one of the most sophisticated applications of chemistry in the ancient world. The metallic sheen on the glaze surface is produced by metallic nanoparticles of copper and silver embedded in the glaze surface layer.

The Lustre Process

  1. A lead-tin opaque white glaze is fired on the pot (first firing, oxidation, ‚àº1050 ¬∞C).
  2. A ‚Äúlustre paint‚Äù (a mixture of copper and silver salts/oxides, plus ochre clay, vinegar, and sulfur) is applied to the fired surface.
  3. Second firing at ‚àº600 ¬∞C in heavy reduction:
    • The organic binder burns, generating CO and H2
    • Cu2+ and Ag+ ions from the paint diffuse into the glaze surface
    • The reducing atmosphere converts them to Cu0 and Ag0 nanoparticles
    • The nanoparticles (5‚Äì50 nm) embed in the top 50‚Äì500 nm of the glaze surface
  4. The unfired ochre/residue is wiped off, revealing the metallic lustre.

Atlas Connections

  • The host glaze must have a low $T_g$ (<550 ¬∞C) so that the ions can diffuse into the surface at ‚àº600 ¬∞C. Lead glazes satisfy this perfectly ($T_g \approx$ 400 ¬∞C‚Äì500 ¬∞C).
  • The optical basicity must be high enough ($\Lambda \gt 0.55$) to promote reduction of Cu2+ and Ag+ to their metallic states.
  • The refractive index of the host glass (Eq. 24) determines the SPR wavelength of the nanoparticles and hence the lustre color (gold, ruby, amber, etc.).

Italian Majolica (15th‚Äì17th Century CE)

Majolica (or maiolica) is the Italian tradition of tin-opacified lead glazes decorated with metallic oxide pigments. The technique came to Italy from Islamic Spain (via Majorca, hence the name).

The Tin-Opacified Glaze

The white ground is achieved by adding 5‚Äì10% SnO2 (cassiterite) to a lead-alkali base glaze. The SnO2 remains undissolved as fine particles (<1 Œºm) that scatter light, producing an opaque white. This is Mie scattering, not Rayleigh ‚Äî the particles are comparable in size to the wavelength of light.

Atlas Eq. 24 (refractive index) is relevant: the opacity depends on $\Delta n = n_{\text{SnO}_2} - n_{\text{glass}}$. Cassiterite has $n \approx 2.05$; the lead glass has $n \approx 1.55‚Äì1.65$. The large $\Delta n \approx 0.4‚Äì0.5$ produces strong scattering ‚Äî far more effective than, say, zircon ($n \approx 1.96$) in a similar matrix.

The Pigment Palette

Every majolica color is a case study in crystal field theory (see companion colorant report):

  • Blue: Cobalt oxide (Co2+ tetrahedral)
  • Green: Copper oxide (Cu2+ octahedral in the lead glass)
  • Yellow: Antimony yellow (Pb2Sb2O7, Naples yellow pigment)
  • Orange: Iron oxide (Fe3+ LMCT + iron oxide pigment)
  • Purple/brown: Manganese oxide (Mn3+ in oxidation)
  • Black: Mixed Fe + Co + Mn (broadband absorption)

Raw Materials & Batch Chemistry

From Rocks to Oxides

The Stull Atlas works in oxide notation (UMF ‚Äî Unity Molecular Formula). But potters work with raw materials ‚Äî feldspars, silica, whiting, kaolin, ball clay, dolomite, talc, bone ash, etc. Converting between the two is the central problem of batch chemistry.

The UMF Convention

In UMF notation:

  • Flux oxides (R2O + RO) are normalised to sum to 1.000
  • Al2O3 and SiO2 are expressed as ratios to the flux unity
  • Additional oxides (colorants, etc.) are listed separately

Example reference composition: $\text{K}_2\text{O}~0.11$, $\text{Na}_2\text{O}~0.24$, $\text{CaO}~0.42$, $\text{MgO}~0.18$, $\text{ZnO}~0.05$. Flux sum: $0.11 + 0.24 + 0.42 + 0.18 + 0.05 = 1.00$. $\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 = 0.35$. $\text{SiO}_2 = 3.20$.

Every raw material undergoes thermal decomposition before contributing its oxides to the melt. Understanding these reactions is essential for designing firing schedules.

Every raw material that releases gas (CO2, H2O, SO2) during firing has a non-zero LOI. The batch weight of raw materials is always greater than the weight of oxides in the fired glaze. Typical LOI values:
  • Calcite: 44% (CaCO3 is 56% CaO, 44% CO2)
  • Dolomite: 47%
  • Kaolin: 14%
  • Ball clay: 8‚Äì12%
  • Feldspar: <1%
  • Silica: 0%
Failure to account for LOI is the single most common error in glaze batch calculation.

Batch-to-UMF Conversion

The Forward Problem: UMF ‚Üí Batch

Given a target UMF, the potter must calculate the weight of each raw material needed. This is a linear system:

‚ö° Batch Calculation
\[ \mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{u} \]
where:
  • $\mathbf{A}$: Matrix of oxide contributions per gram of each raw material (from material analyses)
  • $\mathbf{b}$: Vector of batch weights (unknowns)
  • $\mathbf{u}$: Vector of target oxide moles (from UMF)
The system is typically under-determined (more raw materials available than oxides needed), so additional constraints (cost, availability, plasticity) are used to select among feasible solutions.

The Inverse Problem: Batch ‚Üí UMF

Given a batch recipe (in weight percentages), calculate the fired UMF:

  1. Multiply each material weight by its oxide analysis (wt% of each oxide per material)
  2. Sum all oxide contributions
  3. Subtract LOI
  4. Convert wt% to moles (divide by molecular weight of each oxide)
  5. Normalise flux moles to 1.000

Material Substitution

Substitution Equivalence

When a raw material is unavailable, the potter must substitute another that provides the same oxides. The critical question: ‚ÄúHow many grams of material B provide the same oxides as 100 g of material A?‚Äù

üìù Substituting Wollastonite for Whiting
Both provide CaO. Which provides more, gram for gram?
  • Whiting (CaCO3): MW = 100 g/mol ‚Üí 56 g CaO per 100 g (with 44 g CO2 lost)
  • Wollastonite (CaSiO3): MW = 116 g/mol ‚Üí 48.3 g CaO + 51.7 g SiO2 per 100 g (no gas!)

So substituting wollastonite for whiting:

  1. Requires $100 \times (56/48.3) = 115.9$ g wollastonite to provide the same CaO
  2. But adds $115.9 \times 0.517 = 59.9$ g extra SiO2 ‚Äî must reduce silica elsewhere
  3. Eliminates CO2 gas evolution ‚Äî reduces pinholing risk

Stull Chart Topology

The Chart as a Property Landscape

The Stull Chart plots glazes in a two-dimensional space: Al2O3 (x-axis) vs. SiO2 (y-axis), with all flux oxides normalised to unity. Every point on the chart represents a unique Al:Si ratio at a given flux level. Moving across the chart changes the balance between network formers (SiO2), intermediates (Al2O3), and modifiers (fluxes).

Iso-Property Contours

The Atlas calculates 40 properties at every point on the chart. When plotted as contour maps, these reveal the topological structure of property space:

üî¨ Contour Map Observations
  • COE contours run nearly vertically (parallel to the Si axis): COE is almost entirely determined by flux identity and amount, with little dependence on Si:Al ratio. Moving horizontally (changing Al) barely changes COE; moving vertically (changing Si) does.
  • Viscosity contours run diagonally from lower-left to upper-right: Both Al2O3 and SiO2 increase viscosity, but Al2O3 has a stronger per-mole effect. The viscosity gradient is steepest toward low Si, low Al (high flux fraction).
  • Durability contours run roughly horizontally: Durability increases strongly with SiO2 and moderately with Al2O3. The most durable glazes are in the upper-right quadrant.
  • NBO/T contours run diagonally: NBO/T is maximal at low Si, low Al (fully depolymerised melt) and minimal at high Si, high Al (fully polymerised network).
  • Optical basicity ($\Lambda$) contours are nearly vertical: $\Lambda$ depends primarily on the identity and amount of the flux oxides, not on the Si:Al ratio.

The ‚ÄúSafe Zone‚Äù and Boundary Regions

‚ÑπÔ∏è The Five Regions of the Stull Chart
  1. Upper-right quadrant (high Si, high Al): Very refractory. Hard, durable, chemically resistant glazes but require very high temperatures. Risk of underfiring. These are porcelain/stoneware glazes.
  2. Upper-left quadrant (high Si, low Al): Clear, glossy, hard glazes. Excellent durability. Risk of cristobalite devitrification at extreme silica levels. These are the Stull Chart ‚Äúsweet spot‚Äù for functional ware.
  3. Lower-right quadrant (low Si, high Al): Matte, rough, viscous. These glazes may not fully melt. Risk of crawling (high surface tension from Al2O3). Rarely useful.
  4. Lower-left quadrant (low Si, low Al): Highly fluxed, very fluid. Low durability, high COE, poor chemical resistance. These are crystal glazes, low-fire glazes, and lead glazes. Functional use limited.
  5. Central region (‚àº0.3‚Äì0.5 Al, ‚àº2.5‚Äì4.0 Si): The convergence zone. Multiple eutectic boundaries intersect here. These compositions have:
    • Moderate viscosity (good healing, acceptable running)
    • Moderate durability
    • Moderate COE (adjustable by flux choice)
    • Best glass-forming ability (highest $T_{rg}$)
    • Most predictable behavior
    This is where the majority of functional stoneware glazes live.

The Si:Al Ratio as a Master Variable

The Si:Al ratio is arguably the single most important structural parameter in a silicate glass. It controls:

  • The balance between 4-coordinated (tetrahedral) Al (Al[4], acts as a network former) and 5/6-coordinated Al (Al$^{[5]}/\text{Al}^{[6]}$, acts as a modifier)
  • The degree of network polymerisation (NBO/T)
  • The tendency toward crystallisation (which crystal phase nucleates)
  • The optical properties (refractive index, transparency)

The Atlas reports Si:Al ratio directly and uses it in several equations.

Atlas Equations for Durability

Chemical Durability (Eq. 28)

The Atlas chemical durability equation estimates the resistance of the glass to acid attack:

‚ö° Chemical Durability Index
\[ D = \sum_i x_i \cdot d_i \]
where $d_i$ is the durability contribution of each oxide. Higher $D$ = more durable.

Oxides that improve durability: SiO2 ($+$), Al2O3 ($+$), ZrO2 ($+$large), SnO2 ($+$).

Oxides that reduce durability: Na2O ($-$), K2O ($-$), PbO ($-$), B2O3 ($-$).

Hydration Free Energy (Eq. 29)

The Atlas hydration free energy estimates the thermodynamic tendency of the glass to react with water:

\[\Delta G_{\text{hyd}} = \sum_i x_i \cdot \Delta G_{\text{hyd},i}\]

More negative $\Delta G_{\text{hyd}}$ = stronger tendency to hydrate = lower durability.

Safe Colorant Practices

‚ö†Ô∏è Colorant Safety Guidelines
  1. Lead-free glazes: For any ware intended for food contact, use zero PbO. Modern frits and flux combinations can achieve all the same colors and surface qualities without lead.
  2. Cadmium-free: Avoid CdS/CdSe inclusion pigments unless the encapsulating zircon matrix is proven to prevent leaching at the specific firing temperature.
  3. Barium: BaO is relatively safe in a well-formulated, durable glass (Ba2+ is tightly bound in the network). However, underfired barium glazes or those applied over porous bodies can leach toxic Ba2+. Use only in fully matured glazes with $D \gt 0.6$.
  4. Cobalt: Safe at typical glaze concentrations (<3% CoO). The Co2+ is strongly bound in the tetrahedral glass network.
  5. Copper: Safe in most oxidation glazes. Reduced copper (Cu0 nanoparticles) in copper red glazes has not been shown to leach.
  6. Chromium: Safe as Cr3+ (essentially non-toxic, strongly bound in octahedral sites). Dangerous as Cr6+ (chromate), but Cr6+ forms only in extremely basic compositions ($\Lambda \gt 0.65$). Avoid chrome in high-alkali, high-barium, or lead-bearing glazes.
  7. Manganese: Safe in glass if fully dissolved. Underfired or crystalline manganese glazes may contain MnO2 particles that can dissolve in acid.

Microwave Safety

Microwave-safe ceramics must not:

  1. Contain metals that absorb microwave radiation (heating the pot, not the food)
  2. Absorb water into the body (steam generation, cracking)

‚ÑπÔ∏è Microwave Absorption by Glazes
Most silicate glazes are transparent to microwaves (2.45 ). However:
  • Iron-bearing glazes: Fe2+‚ÄìFe3+ IVCT absorption extends into the microwave region. High-iron glazes (>5% Fe2O3) can absorb significant microwave energy, causing the pot to heat unevenly.
  • Metallic glazes: Any glaze containing metallic particles (copper red Cu0, iron metallic, gold lustre) will absorb microwaves and produce sparking/arcing.
  • Carbon-cored bodies: If the body contains trapped carbon from incomplete burnout, the carbon absorbs microwaves intensely.

Most functional stoneware and porcelain glazes are microwave-safe unless they contain metallic inclusions or are heavily iron-bearing.

Validation & Model Accuracy

The Nature of Atlas Predictions

The Stull Atlas is built on additive oxide-contribution models: for most properties, the prediction is a weighted linear sum of oxide mole fractions. This is the simplest possible model, and it is important to understand both its strengths and its limitations honestly.

The Additive Assumption

The core assumption:

\[ P = \sum_i x_i \cdot p_i + P_0 \]
where $P$ is the predicted property, $x_i$ is the mole fraction of oxide $i$, $p_i$ is the oxide's contribution coefficient, and $P_0$ is a baseline (often zero).

This assumes:

  1. Each oxide contributes independently to the property
  2. There are no interaction terms (cross-products $x_i \cdot x_j$)
  3. The relationship is linear in composition (no curvature, no thresholds)

These assumptions are approximately correct for many properties over limited composition ranges, but they are fundamentally wrong in important ways.

Where the Additive Model Works Well

Thermal Expansion (COE)

The additive model works best for COE (Eq. 6). Thermal expansion is primarily determined by the mean bond strength and coordination number, both of which are additive to first order. Literature comparisons show:

üìù COE Model Accuracy
  • Composition range: Soda-lime-silica glasses (‚àº60‚Äì75% SiO2, 10‚Äì20% Na2O, 5‚Äì15% CaO)
  • Accuracy: $\pm 0.3‚Äì0.5 \times 10^{-6}$/¬∞C (relative error ‚àº4‚Äì7%)
  • Systematic bias: Slight underprediction at very high alkali content (the expansion increase is super-linear beyond ‚àº20% Na2O)
  • Known failure: The ‚Äúboron anomaly‚Äù ‚Äî B2O3 changes from a network former ($[3]$-coordinated) to a modifier ($[4]$-coordinated) at ‚àº15 mol% B2O3 in sodium borosilicate glasses, causing a non-monotonic COE response that the linear model cannot capture

Density and Molar Volume

Density (Eq. 7/8) is one of the most accurately predicted properties by additive models. The molar volume is simply the sum of partial molar volumes:

\[V = \sum_i x_i \cdot V_i\]

Typical accuracy: $\pm 1‚Äì2\%$ for compositions within the training range.

Refractive Index

Refractive index (Eq. 24) is closely related to density through the Lorentz‚ÄìLorenz relation:

\[\frac{n^2 - 1}{n^2 + 2} = \frac{4\pi}{3}\,N_A\,\frac{\alpha_e}{V}\]

The additive model for $n$ inherits the good accuracy of the density model. Typical accuracy: $\pm 0.005‚Äì0.01$ (relative error ‚àº0.3‚Äì0.7%).

Where the Additive Model Breaks Down

Viscosity

VFT viscosity (Eq. 11) is the property where the additive model is most severely strained. The reason: viscosity depends on the connectivity of the silicate network, which is a highly nonlinear function of composition.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Mixed-Alkali Effect
The most dramatic failure of any additive oxide model is the mixed-alkali effect (MAE). When two alkali oxides (e.g., Na2O and K2O) are present simultaneously, viscosity is higher than predicted by a linear interpolation between the two end-member compositions. The deviation can be enormous ‚Äî up to 2 orders of magnitude in log viscosity.

The MAE arises because dissimilar alkali ions block each other's diffusion paths through the glass network (a cooperative structural effect). The additive model has no terms for $x_{\text{Na}} \cdot x_{\text{K}}$ interactions and completely misses this effect.

Practical impact: A glaze with $0.5~\text{Na}_2\text{O} + 0.5~\text{K}_2\text{O}$ will be more viscous (and fire higher) than one with $1.0~\text{Na}_2\text{O}$ or $1.0~\text{K}_2\text{O}$ alone, even though the additive model predicts the same viscosity.

Glass Transition Temperature

$T_g$ (Eq. 10) inherits the viscosity model's limitations because $T_g$ is defined as the temperature where $\log\eta \approx 12$. Any error in the viscosity model translates directly to error in $T_g$. Typical accuracy: $\pm 20‚Äì40$ ¬∞C for standard compositions, potentially $\pm 50‚Äì80$ ¬∞C for compositions with strong mixed-alkali effects.

Surface Properties

Surface tension (Eq. 26), surface energy, and contact angle are poorly predicted by additive models because surface properties depend on preferential surface segregation: certain oxides (especially alkalis and lead) preferentially concentrate at the glass surface, creating a surface composition different from the bulk. The additive model uses bulk composition, not surface composition, and therefore systematically overpredicts surface tension.

Improving the Models

Beyond Additive: Interaction Terms

The next level of model sophistication adds pairwise interaction terms:

\[ P = \sum_i x_i \cdot p_i + \sum_{i\lt j} x_i x_j \cdot p_{ij} \]

This captures effects like:

  • Mixed-alkali effect ($x_{\text{Na}} \cdot x_{\text{K}}$ term)
  • Borosilicate anomaly ($x_{\text{B}} \cdot x_{\text{Na}}$ term)
  • Lead-alkali synergy ($x_{\text{Pb}} \cdot x_{\text{Na}}$ term)

The cost: many more parameters to fit, requiring many more experimental data points.

Machine Learning Approaches

Neural networks, random forests, and Gaussian process models can capture arbitrary nonlinear composition-property relationships. Recent work (2018‚Äì2024) has shown ML models achieving:

  • COE prediction within $\pm 0.2 \times 10^{-6}$/¬∞C
  • $T_g$ prediction within $\pm 15$ ¬∞C
  • Viscosity prediction within $\pm 0.3$ log units

However, ML models require large training datasets (>1000 compositions) and offer less physical insight than parametric models. They also risk overfitting to specific composition families.

The Atlas Approach: Honest Simplicity

The Stull Atlas deliberately uses additive models because:

  1. They are transparent: every coefficient has a physical meaning.
  2. They are robust: they never produce wildly wrong predictions (no extrapolation catastrophes from overfitting).
  3. They are fast: instant computation for any composition.
  4. They are sufficient for the target audience: potters need reliable rank-ordering (‚Äúwill composition A have higher COE than composition B?‚Äù) more than exact numerical values. Additive models excel at rank-ordering.

‚ÑπÔ∏è The Honest Disclaimer
The Stull Atlas predictions should be used as guides, not guarantees. They are most reliable for:
  1. Comparing two compositions (‚Äúwhich direction should I move?‚Äù)
  2. Identifying compositions that are clearly outside safe/functional ranges
  3. Rank-ordering candidate recipes for testing

They are least reliable for:

  1. Predicting exact numerical property values for novel compositions
  2. Compositions far from the soda‚Äìlime‚Äìalumina‚Äìsilica family
  3. Properties that depend on processing (atmosphere, cooling rate, raw material particle size) as much as composition

Always test. The kiln is the final arbiter, and no model replaces a well-designed test tile programme.
