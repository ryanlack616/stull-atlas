Nucleation Theory, Devitrification, Crystal Glaze Design, and Phase Separation in Silicate Ceramic Glazes

Companion Report to the Stull Atlas Application

Introduction: Glass vs. Crystal

A ceramic glaze is, thermodynamically, a metastable material. The equilibrium state at room temperature for any silicate melt composition is a mixture of crystalline phases ‚Äî it ‚Äúwants‚Äù to be a rock, not a glass. That it remains glassy at all is a kinetic accident: the melt was cooled fast enough that the atoms could not find their equilibrium crystal lattice positions before thermal motion was quenched.

This report explores what happens when that kinetic barrier is overcome ‚Äî when crystals do form in the glaze, either deliberately (crystal glazes, matte surfaces, devitrified textures) or accidentally (devitrification defects, unwanted haze).

The Thermodynamic Driving Force

The driving force for crystallisation is the free energy difference between the supercooled liquid (the glass) and the crystal:

‚ö° Gibbs Free Energy of Crystallisation
\[ \Delta G_{\text{cryst}} = G_{\text{crystal}} - G_{\text{liquid}} = \Delta H_{\text{fus}}\,\frac{T_m - T}{T_m} \approx -\Delta H_{\text{fus}}\,\frac{\Delta T}{T_m} \]
where $\Delta T = T_m - T$ is the undercooling below the liquidus temperature $T_m$, and $\Delta H_{\text{fus}}$ is the enthalpy of fusion. This linearisation (Turnbull approximation) is valid for moderate undercooling.

Key points:

  • At $T = T_m$: $\Delta G_{\text{cryst}} = 0$. No driving force; crystal and liquid coexist.
  • At $T \lt T_m$: $\Delta G_{\text{cryst}} \lt 0$. Crystallisation is thermodynamically favoured.
  • The deeper the undercooling, the larger the driving force.
  • But deep undercooling also means high viscosity (from VFT, Atlas Eq. 11), which slows atomic diffusion and hence crystal growth.

This competition between increasing driving force and decreasing mobility is the central tension of crystallisation kinetics.

Classical Nucleation Theory

Before a crystal can grow, it must first nucleate ‚Äî a small cluster of atoms must spontaneously arrange themselves in the crystal lattice. This is the nucleation step, and it is the critical bottleneck for crystallisation.

Homogeneous Nucleation

In a perfectly uniform liquid, nucleation must occur spontaneously (‚Äúhomogeneously‚Äù). The free energy of forming a spherical nucleus of radius $r$ has two competing terms:

‚ö° Classical Nucleation Free Energy
\[ \Delta G(r) = -\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \,\frac{|\Delta G_v|}{V_m} + 4\pi r^2 \,\sigma \]
where:
  • First term: Volume free energy gain ($\Delta G_v \lt 0$, favouring crystallisation)
  • Second term: Surface energy cost ($\sigma \gt 0$, opposing nucleation)
  • $V_m$: Molar volume of the crystal

The volume term scales as $r^3$ (grows faster) while the surface term scales as $r^2$. At small $r$, the surface penalty dominates ‚Äî tiny nuclei are unstable and dissolve. At large $r$, the volume gain dominates ‚Äî large crystals grow spontaneously.

The critical radius $r^*$ is where $d(\Delta G)/dr = 0$:

‚ö° Critical Nucleus Radius
\[ r^* = \frac{2\sigma V_m}{|\Delta G_v|} = \frac{2\sigma T_m}{\Delta H_{\text{fus}} \, \Delta T} \]
The critical nucleus contains ‚àº100‚Äì1000 atoms for typical silicate systems.

The activation barrier for nucleation (the height of the $\Delta G(r)$ curve at $r = r^*$):

‚ö° Nucleation Barrier
\[ \Delta G^* = \frac{16\pi\sigma^3 V_m^2}{3(\Delta G_v)^2} = \frac{16\pi\sigma^3 T_m^2}{3(\Delta H_{\text{fus}})^2(\Delta T)^2} \]
üî¨ The Nucleation Paradox
As undercooling $\Delta T$ increases:
  1. The critical radius $r^*$ decreases (Eq. [eq:critical_radius]) ‚Äî smaller nuclei become stable.
  2. The nucleation barrier $\Delta G^*$ decreases (Eq. [eq:nucleation_barrier]) ‚Äî nucleation becomes thermodynamically easier.
  3. But the melt viscosity $\eta$ increases exponentially (VFT, Atlas Eq. 11) ‚Äî atomic diffusion slows down.

Result: The nucleation rate passes through a maximum at an intermediate undercooling. Too little undercooling: low driving force. Too much undercooling: atoms can't move. This maximum defines the ‚Äúnose‚Äù of the TTT diagram (Section [sec:ttt]).

Nucleation Rate

The steady-state nucleation rate (number of critical nuclei formed per unit volume per unit time):

‚ö° Steady-State Nucleation Rate
\[ I = I_0 \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta G^*}{k_B T}\right) \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta G_D}{k_B T}\right) \]
where:
  • $I_0$: Pre-exponential factor (‚àº10^{30}‚Äì$10^{42}$ /¬≥m/s)
  • $\Delta G^*$: Nucleation barrier (Eq. [eq:nucleation_barrier])
  • $\Delta G_D$: Activation energy for diffusion across the nucleus-liquid interface
  • $k_B$: Boltzmann constant
The diffusion term can be related to viscosity via the Stokes‚ÄìEinstein relation: $\Delta G_D \propto \ln \eta$.

Heterogeneous Nucleation

In practice, homogeneous nucleation in silicate melts is extremely rare. The nucleation barrier $\Delta G^*$ is too high for most compositions. Instead, nucleation occurs heterogeneously ‚Äî on pre-existing surfaces, impurities, or compositional fluctuations that reduce the barrier:

‚ö° Heterogeneous Nucleation
\[ \Delta G^*_{\text{het}} = \Delta G^*_{\text{hom}} \cdot f(\theta) \]
where $f(\theta) = \frac{(2 + \cos\theta)(1 - \cos\theta)^2}{4}$ is the wetting angle factor, always $\leq 1$.

Heterogeneous nucleation agents in ceramic glazes:

  • TiO2 (rutile/anatase): The most powerful nucleating agent in silicate glasses. TiO2 promotes phase separation (immiscibility), creating nanoscale compositional heterogeneities that serve as nucleation sites.
  • ZrO2 (zirconia): Acts as an insoluble seed crystal for wollastonite and other calcium silicate phases.
  • P2O5 (phosphorus pentoxide): Promotes liquid-liquid immiscibility in silicate melts, creating phosphate-rich droplets that nucleate apatite or whitlockite crystals.
  • Cr2O3: Forms spinel nuclei that seed crystal growth.
  • Metallic particles (Cu0, Fe0): Surface sites for crystallisation.
  • Bubble surfaces: The gas-glass interface provides nucleation sites. This is why crystals often form on bubble trails.

Crystal Growth

Once a stable nucleus has formed (passed $r^*$), the crystal grows by adding atoms from the surrounding supercooled liquid to the crystal surface.

Growth Rate

The crystal growth rate $u$ depends on the same two competing factors as nucleation:

‚ö° Crystal Growth Rate
\[ u = u_0 \left[1 - \exp\!\left(-\frac{|\Delta G_v|}{k_B T}\right)\right] \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta G_D}{k_B T}\right) \]
  • First factor: Thermodynamic driving force (zero at $T_m$, increases with undercooling)
  • Second factor: Kinetic mobility (maximum at $T_m$, decreases with undercooling)
Result: Like nucleation rate, growth rate passes through a maximum at intermediate undercooling.

Growth Morphology

The shape of the growing crystal depends on the growth rate relative to diffusion:

RegimeConditionCrystal Morphology
Interface-controlled$u \ll D/r$Faceted, euhedral crystals
Diffusion-controlled$u \gg D/r$Dendritic, skeletal, needle-like
Mixed$u \approx D/r$Rounded, botryoidal, spherulitic

In ceramic glazes, the most common morphologies are:

  • Needle/acicular: Wollastonite (CaSiO3), mullite (3Al2O3¬∑ 2SiO2)
  • Plate/tabular: Zinc silicate (Zn2SiO4, willemite), hematite (Fe2O3)
  • Dendritic: Zinc silicate crystal glazes, rutile ‚Äútiger skin‚Äù
  • Spherulitic: Devitrification crystals, calcium phosphate opalescence

TTT Diagrams and the Nose of Crystallisation

A TTT (Time‚ÄìTemperature‚ÄìTransformation) diagram plots the time required for a given volume fraction of crystallisation to occur at each temperature. It captures the competition between thermodynamic driving force and kinetic limitation.

The C-Curve

The TTT curve for silicate glasses has a characteristic ‚ÄúC‚Äù shape (or ‚Äúnose‚Äù shape):

Temperature RegionCrystallisation Time
Near $T_m$ (small $\Delta T$)Very long (low driving force)
$T_{\text{nose}}$ (optimal $\Delta T$)Shortest (maximum rate)
Near $T_g$ (large $\Delta T$)Very long (high viscosity, frozen kinetics)
Below $T_g$Effectively infinite (glass state)
üíé Practical Implications of the TTT Nose
  1. To avoid crystallisation (clear glaze): Cool fast enough to ‚Äúmiss the nose‚Äù ‚Äî pass through $T_{\text{nose}}$ before significant nucleation occurs. The critical cooling rate $q_c$ is:
    \[q_c \approx \frac{T_m - T_{\text{nose}}}{t_{\text{nose}}}\]
    where $t_{\text{nose}}$ is the time at the nose. For most stoneware glazes, $q_c \approx 1‚Äì10$ ¬∞C/min.
  2. To promote crystallisation (crystal glaze): Hold at or near $T_{\text{nose}}$ during cooling. The longer the hold, the more crystal growth occurs.
  3. To control crystal size: Nucleate at high undercooling (many small nuclei) then grow at $T_{\text{nose}}$ (controlled growth). Or nucleate at low undercooling (few nuclei) for large, spectacular crystals.

Atlas Connection: The Hrub√Ω Parameter

The Stull Atlas Equation 40 provides a quantitative measure of a glass's resistance to crystallisation:

‚ö° Hrub√Ω Glass-Forming Parameter
\[ K_H = \frac{T_c - T_g}{T_m - T_c} \]
where:
  • $T_c$: Onset temperature of crystallisation (from DSC)
  • $T_g$: Glass transition temperature (Atlas Eq. 10)
  • $T_m$: Melting/liquidus temperature
Higher $K_H$ = more stable glass = harder to crystallise. $K_H \gt 0.5$: excellent glass former. $K_H \lt 0.1$: poor glass former (crystallises easily).

Atlas Connection: The Turnbull Reduced Glass Transition

Equation 39 gives:

‚ö° Reduced Glass Transition Temperature
\[ T_{rg} = \frac{T_g}{T_m} \]
$T_{rg} \gt 2/3$: Strong glass former; crystallisation suppressed. $T_{rg} \lt 1/2$: Weak glass former; crystallisation likely.

For crystal glaze design, we want a low $T_{rg}$ ‚Äì the melt should be a poor glass former that readily crystallises. For clear, glassy glazes, we want high $T_{rg}$.

Devitrification: Unwanted Crystallisation

Diagnosing Devitrification

‚ÑπÔ∏è Devitrification Diagnostic Flowchart
  1. Is the matte/haze on thin edges or rims only?
    • Yes ‚Üí Likely wollastonite or anorthite devitrification, exacerbated by thinner glaze (less thermal mass, cools faster locally ‚Äî wait, actually thin glaze cools faster, which should suppress crystallisation. The real reason is that thin edges have reduced flux concentration due to body absorption and thermal gradient effects.)
    • No ‚Üí Continue
  2. Is the matte uniform across the entire surface?
    • Yes ‚Üí Likely cristobalite or fine-grained devitrification throughout; cool faster.
    • No ‚Üí Patchy devitrification, usually from composition variation or kiln cold spots.
  3. Can you see individual crystals?
    • Yes (needles, plates, stars) ‚Üí Wollastonite needles, zinc silicate plates, or surface crystal nucleation.
    • No (uniform matte/satin) ‚Üí Extremely fine-grained devitrification or phase separation.

Preventing Devitrification

Strategies derived from Atlas equations:

  1. Increase $T_{rg}$ (Eq. 39): Add network formers (SiO2, B2O3, Al2O3) to raise $T_g$ relative to $T_m$.
  2. Increase $K_H$ (Eq. 40): Increase the glass-forming ability by adding mixed alkalis (confusion effect), B2O3, or Al2O3.
  3. Cool faster past the nose: If the nose temperature is known, programme the kiln to drop through that range at $\gt q_c$ ¬∞C/min.
  4. Avoid nucleating impurities: Keep TiO2, ZrO2, P2O5 levels low if crystal-free surfaces are desired.
  5. Balance the composition: Use the atlas to check that Si:Al ratios and flux levels do not place the composition in a known devitrification field.

Crystal Glaze Design

Crystal glazes are the deliberate, artistic exploitation of crystallisation. Large, visible crystals (often 1‚Äì10 cm in diameter) are grown in the glaze by carefully controlling nucleation and growth.

Zinc Silicate (Willemite) Crystal Glazes

The most common and spectacular crystal glaze type. Zinc silicate (Zn2SiO4, willemite) crystals grow as hexagonal discs or six-pointed stars with vivid colors from added colorants.

Composition Requirements

Key features:
  • Very high ZnO (40‚Äì60% of flux): Provides the Zn for Zn2SiO4 crystal growth
  • Very low Al2O3 ($\lt 0.1$): Alumina inhibits crystallisation by increasing melt viscosity and stabilising the glass
  • High total flux (low $T_{rg}$): Makes a poor glass former that readily crystallises
  • High silica ($\gt 3.0$): Provides the Si for Zn2SiO4

The Firing Schedule

The firing schedule for crystal glazes is the most exacting in ceramics:

  1. Heat to peak temperature (‚àº1280 ¬∞C for cone 10, 1230 ¬∞C for cone 6) and soak 15‚Äì30 minutes to fully melt the glaze and dissolve all zinc silicate that may have formed prematurely.
  2. Crash-cool by ‚àº100 ¬∞C‚Äì150 ¬∞C (e.g., from 1280 ¬∞C to 1130 ¬∞C) in 30‚Äì60 minutes. This passes through the high-temperature nucleation zone without allowing large nuclei to form. The goal is to reach the growth zone with few nuclei (for large crystals).
  3. Hold at the growth temperature ($T_{\text{hold}} \approx$ 1050 ¬∞C‚Äì1130 ¬∞C) for 2‚Äì6 hours. During this hold, existing nuclei grow into large crystals. The radius of a crystal grows approximately as:
    \[ r(t) \approx r_0 + u \cdot t \]
    where $u$ is the growth rate (typically 0.5‚Äì5 Œºm/s) and $t$ is the hold time.
  4. Cool slowly to below $T_g$ to avoid thermal shock cracking.
üî¨ Why Crystal Glazes Need a Catch Tray
Crystal glazes have extremely high flux content (>70% flux oxides by weight) and very low alumina. The VFT viscosity (Atlas Eq. 11) at peak temperature gives $\log\eta \approx 2.0‚Äì3.0$ ‚Äî the glaze is almost as fluid as honey. It will run off vertical surfaces and pool at the bottom. Crystal glaze pots must be fired on a pedestal over a catch tray, and the glaze must be wiped or ground from the foot.

This runny nature is not a flaw but a requirement: the low viscosity allows Zn2+ and Si4+ ions to diffuse rapidly to the growing crystal front. If the glaze were more viscous (higher alumina), diffusion would be too slow and crystals would remain microscopic.

Wollastonite (CaSiO3) Crystals

Wollastonite crystals form in high-calcium, moderate-silica glazes. They produce a matte or satin surface with fine, needle-like crystals. Unlike zinc silicate crystals, wollastonite crystals are typically small (<1 mm) and produce a textured surface rather than large individual crystals.

Composition range:

  • CaO: 0.60‚Äì0.80 of flux unity
  • Al2O3: 0.15‚Äì0.25
  • SiO2: 2.50‚Äì3.50
  • MgO: 0.05‚Äì0.15 (diopside variant if Mg is high)

Wollastonite matte glazes are the workhorses of mid-fire functional ware ‚Äî durable, food-safe, attractive, and tolerant of slight kiln temperature variations.

Rutile Crystal Effects

Rutile (TiO2, as the mineral) produces spectacular ‚Äútiger skin,‚Äù ‚Äústardust,‚Äù and ‚Äúaventurine‚Äù effects through multiple mechanisms:

  1. Phase separation nucleation: TiO2 induces liquid-liquid immiscibility, creating Ti-rich and Ti-poor regions. The Ti-rich regions crystallise as rutile needles or anatase plates.
  2. Fe‚ÄìTi interaction: In iron-bearing glazes, rutile promotes Fe2TiO5 (pseudobrookite) or ilmenite (FeTiO3) crystal formation, producing golden-brown ‚Äútiger‚Äù streaks.
  3. Surface nucleation: Rutile crystals often nucleate at the glaze surface (where $p_{\text{O}_2}$ is highest), creating a surface crust of crystals with a glassy interior ‚Äî the ‚Äúfloating crystal‚Äù effect.
üíé Rutile Blue ‚Äî More Than Just TiO2
The famous ‚Äúrutile blue‚Äù glaze (sometimes called ‚Äúfloating blue‚Äù or ‚Äúchun blue‚Äù) is produced by adding 2‚Äì6% rutile to a glaze containing $\lt 1\%$ iron and moderate alkali flux. The blue color is not from titanium absorption (Ti4+ is $d^0$, colorless). Instead:
  1. The rutile induces nanoscale phase separation (50‚Äì200 nm droplets)
  2. These droplets have a different refractive index from the matrix ($\Delta n \approx 0.02$‚Äì0.05)
  3. Rayleigh scattering of short wavelengths (blue/violet) off these nanodroplets produces a structural blue color ‚Äî the same physics as a blue sky
  4. The underlying iron gives a yellow-brown body color; the blue scattering overlays this, producing a blue/gray/lavender appearance
This is not a colorant-based blue; it is an opalescence effect (see Section [sec:opalescence]).

Phase Separation (Liquid‚ÄìLiquid Immiscibility)

Not all ‚Äúcrystallisation‚Äù events involve true crystal formation. In many glass systems, the homogeneous supercooled liquid separates into two liquid phases of different composition before either crystallises. This is liquid-liquid immiscibility, or phase separation.

Thermodynamics of Immiscibility

Phase separation occurs when the free energy of mixing curve $\Delta G_{\text{mix}}(x)$ develops a region of negative curvature:

‚ö° Condition for Immiscibility
\[ \frac{\partial^2 \Delta G_{\text{mix}}}{\partial x^2} \lt 0 \]
In this composition range, the system can lower its total free energy by separating into two phases of different composition.

Spinodal vs. Binodal Decomposition

Two mechanisms of phase separation exist, depending on whether the composition falls inside or outside the spinodal:

MechanismContinuous, spontaneousRequires nucleation barrier

The Immiscibility Dome in Silicate Systems

The most important immiscibility systems in ceramic glazes:

  1. Na2O‚ÄìB2O3‚ÄìSiO2 (Pyrex-type): The borosilicate immiscibility dome. The glass separates into a Na2O‚ÄìB2O3-rich phase and a SiO2-rich phase. This is the basis of Vycor glass processing and is relevant to any borosilicate glaze formulation.
  2. CaO‚ÄìSiO2‚ÄìAl2O3: The lime‚Äìsilica immiscibility region. High-calcium, moderate-silica compositions can phase-separate, producing opalescent wollastonite-composition droplets.
  3. P2O5‚ÄìSiO2: Phosphorus is highly immiscible with silica. Even 0.5% P2O5 can induce phase separation in silicate glasses, producing the characteristic opalescent ‚Äúchun‚Äù or ‚Äújun‚Äù blue.
  4. TiO2‚ÄìSiO2: Titanium-rich compositions phase-separate, producing the rutile effects described above.
  5. ZnO‚ÄìSiO2‚ÄìAl2O3: The zinc system has complex immiscibility that affects zinc crystal glaze behavior at sub-crystallisation temperatures.

Opalescence & Structural Color

The Physics of Opalescence

Opalescence is the phenomenon where a glass appears bluish in transmitted light and orange-brownish in reflected light (or vice versa). It arises from Rayleigh scattering by nanoscale phase-separated droplets or crystallites:

‚ö° Rayleigh Scattering Intensity
\[ I \propto \frac{d^6}{\lambda^4}\,(\Delta n)^2\,N \]
where:
  • $d$: Droplet diameter ($d \ll \lambda$, typically 10‚Äì100 nm)
  • $\lambda$: Wavelength of light
  • $\Delta n$: Refractive index contrast between droplet and matrix
  • $N$: Number density of droplets

The $\lambda^{-4}$ dependence is critical: blue light ($\lambda \approx 450$ nm) is scattered ‚àº5.7√ó more strongly than red light ($\lambda \approx 700$ nm). When white light enters the glass:

  • Blue is scattered sideways and backward ‚Üí glass appears blue in reflected/scattered light
  • Red passes through ‚Üí glass appears orange/amber in transmitted light

Chun / Jun Blue (Èàû)

The most famous opalescent glaze in ceramic history. Song Dynasty Jun ware (960‚Äì1279 CE) exhibits a luminous, ethereal blue that cannot be reproduced by any colorant. The mechanism:

üíé The Chun Blue Mechanism
  1. The glaze contains 0.5‚Äì3% P2O5 (from bone ash or wood ash) in a high-calcium, moderate-alkali base.
  2. During cooling, phosphate-rich liquid droplets phase-separate from the silicate matrix (P2O5‚ÄìSiO2 immiscibility).
  3. The droplets are 30‚Äì100 nm in diameter ‚Äî below the wavelength of visible light.
  4. Rayleigh scattering produces structural blue color.
  5. A small amount of Fe2+ (0.5‚Äì1% Fe2O3 from impurities) adds a subtle green-blue tint that deepens the perceived color.

The blue is opalescent: viewed against a dark background, it appears blue; against light, it appears amber-brown.

‚ÑπÔ∏è Atlas Equations for Opalescence Design
To design an opalescent glaze:
  • Use Atlas NBO/T (Eq. 14) to ensure sufficient network depolymerisation for phase separation
  • Use VFT viscosity (Eq. 11) to ensure the melt is fluid enough for droplet formation but viscous enough to prevent coalescence (droplets growing too large ‚Üí Mie scattering ‚Üí white opacity instead of blue)
  • Target $T_g$ (Eq. 10) to freeze in the optimal droplet size
  • Use refractive index (Eq. 24) to estimate $\Delta n$ between phases

Phase Diagrams and Eutectic Navigation

Phase diagrams are the roadmaps of crystallisation. They show which crystal phases are stable at each composition and temperature, and where the liquid (glass) phase exists.

The Binary Eutectic

The simplest relevant case: a two-component system (e.g., CaO‚ÄìSiO2) with a single eutectic point.

‚ö° Eutectic Properties
At the eutectic composition and temperature:
\[ T_E = \min(T_{\text{liq}}); \quad x_E = \text{composition at } T_E \]
  • $T_E$: The lowest melting temperature in the system
  • $x_E$: The eutectic composition ‚Äî the only composition that melts completely at $T_E$
  • At $x_E$, the liquid coexists with two solid phases simultaneously

Why eutectics matter for potters:

  1. The eutectic represents the most fusible composition in a system. Formulating near the eutectic gives the lowest firing temperature for a given set of raw materials.
  2. Eutectic compositions produce the best glass formers: they have the least thermodynamic driving force for any single crystal phase, making it hardest for any one phase to nucleate.
  3. Moving away from the eutectic increases the liquidus temperature and the risk of primary-phase crystallisation (devitrification).

Ternary Phase Diagrams and the Stull Chart

The Stull Chart itself is fundamentally a projected ternary phase diagram. It maps compositions in the R2O/RO‚ÄìAl2O3‚ÄìSiO2 space onto a two-dimensional grid with Al2O3 on the $x$-axis and SiO2 on the $y$-axis (with total flux normalised to 1.0 in UMF notation).

üî¨ The Stull Chart as Phase Map
Each region of the Stull Chart corresponds to a different primary crystallisation field:
  • Low Si, low Al: Flux mineral fields (nepheline, leucite). Glazes here are very runny, high-expansion, poor chemical resistance.
  • Low Si, high Al: Corundum/mullite fields. Unrealistic for glazes (too refractory).
  • High Si, low Al: Quartz/cristobalite fields. Clear, glossy, hard glazes. Risk of cristobalite devitrification.
  • High Si, high Al: Mullite field. Very refractory; only reached in porcelain bodies, not glazes.
  • The ‚Äúsweet spot‚Äù (‚àº0.3‚Äì0.5 Al, ‚àº2.5‚Äì4.0 Si): Multiple eutectic boundaries converge here. The most stable glazes ‚Äî best glass formers, least devitrification, best balance of properties. This is where most functional stoneware glazes live.

The iso-property contours calculated by the Stull Atlas (COE, viscosity, durability, etc.) overlay this phase landscape.

Specific Crystal Systems in Glazes

Calcium Phosphate (Bone Ash) Opalescence

Adding bone ash (Ca3(PO4)2, tricalcium phosphate) to a glaze introduces both CaO and P2O5. The phosphorus is almost completely immiscible with the silicate network, and during cooling it precipitates as amorphous calcium phosphate droplets (later crystallising as whitlockite, $\beta$-Ca3(PO4)2, or hydroxyapatite).

Effects:

  • 2‚Äì4% bone ash: Subtle opalescence (blue haze). The ‚Äújun‚Äù effect.
  • 5‚Äì10% bone ash: Milky opacity. The phosphate droplets grow large enough for Mie scattering, producing a white or cream opacity.
  • 10‚Äì20% bone ash: Dense white opacity with possible crawling (high surface tension from phosphate).

Aventurine

Aventurine glazes contain large, flat, hexagonal platelets of hematite (Fe2O3) or fuchsite/mica crystals that produce a brilliant metallic sparkle. The platelets form during very slow cooling (2‚Äì5 ¬∞C/h) from a highly fluid, iron-rich melt ($\gt 8\%$ Fe2O3).

Requirements:

  • Very high iron (8‚Äì15%)
  • Very low viscosity ($\log\eta \lt 3$ at peak temperature)
  • Extremely slow cooling through the crystal growth zone
  • Oxidation atmosphere (hematite is the stable phase)

Matte Glazes: Controlled Microcrystallisation

Matte glazes achieve their non-glossy surface through controlled crystallisation of extremely fine crystals ($\lt 1\;\text{Œºm}$) that scatter light in all directions, breaking the specular reflection.

‚ö†Ô∏è Crystal Matte vs. Underfired Matte
These two types of matte glaze look similar but behave very differently:

Crystal matte (wollastonite, diopside): Fully melted, then crystallised during cooling. The surface is made of interlocking crystals with glassy matrix between them. It is durable, food-safe, and predictable. It resists acid attack because the crystalline phases are chemically stable.

Underfired matte: Never fully melted. The surface is rough because unmelted particles protrude. It is porous, weak, and potentially food-unsafe. The unmelted regions may contain soluble flux phases that leach into food.

Always aim for crystal matte over underfired matte. The Atlas can help: check that VFT viscosity at peak temperature gives $\log\eta \lt 5$ (fully melted), then choose a composition that crystallises during cooling (high CaO, slow cool).

Example 1: Will This Glaze Devitrify?

üìù Devitrification Risk Assessment
Given the reference composition (K2O 0.11, Na2O 0.24, CaO 0.42, MgO 0.18, ZnO 0.05, Al2O3 0.35, SiO2 3.20, B2O3 0.10):

Step 1: Check $T_{rg}$

  • From Atlas Eq. 10: $T_g \approx$ 623 ¬∞C (896 K)
  • Estimated $T_m \approx$ 1200 ¬∞C (1473 K) (from cone 6 target)
  • $T_{rg} = 896/1473 = 0.608$
This is above 0.5 but below 2/3. Moderate glass-forming ability ‚Äî some devitrification risk.

Step 2: Check crystallisation-prone phases

  • CaO = 0.42 (moderate; wollastonite risk if >0.5)
  • MgO = 0.18 (moderate; diopside risk if >0.25)
  • ZnO = 0.05 (low; zinc silicate unlikely)
  • Al2O3 = 0.35 (good; alumina stabilises glass)

Step 3: Assess cooling rate requirement

  • VFT viscosity at $T_{\text{nose}} \approx$ 1000 ¬∞C: $\log\eta \approx 8‚Äì9$ (very viscous; slow diffusion)
  • Critical cooling rate: $q_c \approx 2$ ¬∞C/min (easily achievable in most kilns)

Verdict: Low devitrification risk. This is a well-balanced composition that will produce a clear, glossy glaze if cooled at normal kiln rates (>2 ¬∞C/min through the danger zone).

üî¨ The Crystal Decision Tree
When formulating a new glaze, the crystallisation question reduces to:
  1. Do I want crystals?
    • No (clear/glossy) ‚Üí Maximise $T_{rg}$ and $K_H$. Use moderate flux, adequate Al2O3, mixed alkalis. Cool at $\gt q_c$ through $T_{\text{nose}}$.
    • Yes (crystal glaze) ‚Üí Minimise $T_{rg}$. High flux (esp. ZnO), minimal Al2O3, slow cool with holds.
    • Partially (matte) ‚Üí Target the wollastonite or diopside field. High CaO or MgO, moderate Al2O3, normal cooling gives fine microcrystals.
  2. Which crystal phase?
    • Check the composition against known crystallisation fields (binary/ternary phase diagrams).
    • High CaO ‚Üí wollastonite, anorthite
    • High ZnO ‚Üí willemite (zinc crystal glaze)
    • High MgO ‚Üí diopside, forsterite
    • High Fe2O3 ‚Üí hematite, magnetite (tenmoku)
    • High TiO2 ‚Üí rutile (tiger skin)
  3. What size crystals?
    • Microscopic (matte surface): Many nuclei, short growth time.
    • Macroscopic (visible crystals): Few nuclei, long growth hold.
    • Control via crash-cool rate and hold temperature/duration.
