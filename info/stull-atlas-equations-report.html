Introduction

Core Ratios & Composition Metrics

The first five equations are dimensionless ratios that describe the architecture of a glaze composition. They require no physical constants, no lookup tables, and no empirical calibration ‚Äî they are pure arithmetic applied to the UMF vector. Yet despite their simplicity, they encode some of the deepest insights in glaze chemistry.

Equation 1: Flux Ratio

‚ö° Flux Ratio
\[ R_{\text{flux}} = \frac{\sum \text{fluxes}}{\SiO + \AlO + \BO + \sum \text{fluxes}} \]
where ‚Äúfluxes‚Äù includes all RO and R2O species: Na2O, K2O, Li2O, CaO, MgO, ZnO, BaO, SrO, PbO.

Historical Context

The concept of a flux ratio predates formal glass science. Potters in Song Dynasty China (960‚Äì1279 CE) understood empirically that the ratio of ‚Äúmelting stones‚Äù (feldspars, limestone) to ‚Äústiffening stones‚Äù (silica, kaolin) controlled whether a glaze would flow or remain refractory. The modern oxide-ratio formulation crystallised in the work of Hermann Seger (1839‚Äì1893), whose Unity Molecular Formula system was explicitly designed to separate fluxes from glass formers and stabilisers.

The flux ratio as implemented in the Stull Atlas is a mole-fraction metric: it expresses the proportion of the total composition that consists of network-modifying (flux) species, versus the total of all species including network formers and intermediates.

Deep Physical Meaning

At the atomic level, a silicate glass consists of a three-dimensional network of $\text{SiO}_4$ tetrahedra linked by bridging oxygens (Si‚ÄìO‚ÄìSi bonds). When a flux oxide enters the melt, its cation (Na+, Ca2+, etc.) breaks one of these bridging oxygen bonds, creating two non-bridging oxygens (NBOs). This depolymerises the network, reducing viscosity, lowering the melting temperature, and increasing thermal expansion.

The flux ratio is therefore a first-order measure of how much of the glass network has been disrupted by modifier cations. A high flux ratio means the network is heavily depolymerised ‚Äî the glass is fluid, low-melting, and thermally expansive. A low flux ratio means the network is intact ‚Äî the glass is viscous, refractory, and dimensionally stable.

What You Can Understand

  • Melting behaviour: A glaze with $R_{\text{flux}} \lt 0.15$ will barely melt at cone 10; one with $R_{\text{flux}} \gt 0.35$ may run off the pot at cone 6.
  • Surface quality: Moderate flux ratios (0.18‚Äì0.25) typically produce glossy, well-healed surfaces. Too low gives dry, underfired surfaces; too high gives excessive flow and pooling.
  • Durability trade-off: Every flux that aids melting also weakens the glass network. The flux ratio quantifies this fundamental tension.
  • Crazing tendency: All fluxes increase thermal expansion. A high flux ratio is an early warning for crazing risk.

What You Can Calculate

The flux ratio itself is a leaf property ‚Äî it does not feed any downstream equation directly. However, it serves as a critical diagnostic:

  1. Firing-range classification: Low ($\lt 0.18$) = cone 9‚Äì12; medium (0.18‚Äì0.28) = cone 5‚Äì8; high ($\gt 0.28$) = cone 04‚Äì4.
  2. Glaze-type identification: Matte glazes cluster at low flux ratios; bright glossy glazes at medium; low-fire commercial glazes at high.
  3. Quick sanity check: Before examining viscosity, COE, or durability, the flux ratio tells you whether the composition is in a reasonable melting range for the intended cone.

Equation 2: Silica-to-Alumina Ratio

‚ö° Silica-to-Alumina Ratio
\[ r = \frac{[\SiO]}{[\AlO]} \]
where brackets denote molar amounts in the UMF.

Historical Context

The silica-to-alumina ratio is arguably the single most important compositional parameter in all of ceramics. R.T. Stull himself (1912) built his original porcelain-glaze chart around this ratio as the primary axis ‚Äî it literally is the Stull chart. His insight was that, for a given flux content, the balance between silica and alumina controls everything from surface texture to thermal expansion to durability.

The ratio's importance arises from the fundamentally different structural roles of $\SiO$ and $\AlO$ in the glass network. Both are network formers, but silicon builds the network (4-coordinated Si4+ in $\text{SiO}_4$ tetrahedra) while aluminium stiffens it by introducing charge-balanced 4-coordinated Al3+ sites that resist flow and increase viscosity.

Deep Physical Meaning

Silicon is the primary glass-former. Each $\text{SiO}_4$ tetrahedron shares oxygen corners with up to four neighbours, building the three-dimensional random network that defines glass structure. More silica = longer chains = higher polymerisation = higher viscosity = higher chemical durability = lower thermal expansion.

Aluminium enters the network in 4-coordination (replacing Si4+) when sufficient charge-balancing cations (Na+, K+, Ca2+) are available. Each Al3+ in 4-coordination introduces a charge deficit that is compensated by a nearby alkali or alkaline-earth cation, effectively locking that cation into a structural role rather than a mobile modifier role. This is why adding alumina to a glaze simultaneously:

  1. Increases viscosity (stiffer network)
  2. Improves chemical durability (cations locked in place, not leachable)
  3. Reduces thermal expansion (fewer mobile cations)
  4. Increases surface tension (more cohesive melt)
  5. Prevents crazing (via the expansion reduction)

The Si:Al ratio captures the balance between these two network-forming roles.

What You Can Understand

  • Surface texture: Low Si:Al ($\lt 5$) with adequate flux = matte or satin surfaces (alumina-rich melts are viscous and tend to crystallise). High Si:Al ($\gt 10$) = gloss (silica-rich melts are fluid and glassy).
  • Stull chart position: The Si:Al ratio determines horizontal position on the classic Stull chart. Low = left (matte zone). High = right (gloss zone). The vertical axis is flux content.
  • Crawling risk: Very high Si:Al (low alumina) reduces surface tension, increasing the risk of crawling ‚Äî the melt beads up instead of wetting the clay body.
  • Refractory character: Alumina is the most refractory common oxide. Increasing Al relative to Si makes the glaze harder to melt ‚Äî important at lower cones where heat energy is limited.

What You Can Calculate

Like the flux ratio, Si:Al is a leaf diagnostic. It does not feed downstream equations directly but is essential for:

  1. Stull chart navigation: Locating the composition on the classic 2D surface-texture map.
  2. Limit-chart compliance: Traditional limit formulas (Hesselberth & Roy, Rhodes) specify Si:Al windows for each cone range. Checking compliance is a standard quality step.
  3. Firing-range targeting: At cone 6, typical Si:Al is 7‚Äì12. At cone 10, it drops to 5‚Äì9 (more alumina tolerated because more heat is available).

This places the reference glaze firmly in the glossy stoneware region ‚Äî consistent with the intended cone 6 gloss surface.

Limitations

  • Does not account for boron (B2O3), which acts as a glass former like silica but with different thermal and structural properties. A boron-rich glaze may be glossy despite a low Si:Al ratio.
  • Does not reflect the charge-balance requirement: Al3+ can only enter 4-coordination if sufficient R+ or R2+ cations are available. If the Alumina Saturation Index (Eq. [eq:dd_asi]) exceeds ‚àº1.0, excess Al enters 5- or 6-coordination and behaves very differently.
  • The ratio is undefined if $\AlO = 0$ (division by zero). Some speciality glazes (lead bisilicate, borosilicate) contain no alumina.

Equation 3: Alumina Saturation Index (ASI)

‚ö° Alumina Saturation Index
\[ \text{ASI} = \frac{[\AlO]}{[\NaO] + [\KO] + [\LiO] + [\CaOx]} \]

Historical Context

The Alumina Saturation Index originates in igneous petrology, where it was introduced by Shand (1927) to classify igneous rocks based on the alumina content relative to the charge-balancing capacity of alkalis and calcium. The concept was later adopted by glass scientists to predict the structural role of aluminium in oxide glasses.

Shand's original classification divides compositions into three regimes:

  • Peraluminous (ASI $\gt 1$): More alumina than available charge-balancing cations. Excess Al3+ cannot achieve 4-coordination and must adopt 5- or 6-coordination, fundamentally changing its structural role.
  • Metaluminous ($0.5 \lt $ ASI $\lt 1$): Sufficient cations to charge-balance all alumina. All Al3+ is in 4-coordination (network-forming). Excess cations beyond those needed for charge balance act as network modifiers.
  • Peralkaline (ASI $\lt 0.5$): Large excess of cations over alumina. Network is heavily modified, viscosity is low, expansion is high.

Deep Physical Meaning

The ASI answers a fundamental structural question: does this glass have enough cations to keep all its aluminium in the network-forming role?

When Al3+ substitutes for Si4+ in a tetrahedral site, it introduces a local charge deficit of $-1$ (since Al is trivalent, not tetravalent). This deficit must be compensated by a nearby monovalent (Na+, K+, Li+) or divalent (Ca2+) cation. Each mole of R2O provides 2 moles of R+ cations, so one mole of $\NaO$ can charge-balance one mole of $\AlO$. Each mole of $\CaOx$ provides one Ca2+ cation, which can also charge-balance one mole of $\AlO$.

üî¨ The Structural Transition at ASI = 1
When ASI crosses 1.0, the glass undergoes a structural phase transition in the sense that a qualitatively new structural motif appears:

Below ASI = 1: Every Al3+ sits in a $\text{AlO}_4^{-}$ tetrahedron with a nearby charge-balancing cation. The glass is a conventional aluminosilicate network. Leftover cations (those not consumed by Al charge-balancing) act as network modifiers, creating non-bridging oxygens and reducing viscosity.

Above ASI = 1: Some Al3+ ions have no charge-balancing partner. These ‚Äúorphan‚Äù aluminiums adopt higher coordination (5-fold $\text{AlO}_5$ triclusters or 6-fold $\text{AlO}_6$ octahedra). This dramatically changes the glass properties:

  • Viscosity drops (counterintuitively, because the higher-coordinated Al sites act as network defects)
  • Chemical durability decreases (exposed Al sites are hydrolysis-susceptible)
  • The VFT model becomes less accurate (it was calibrated on metaluminous compositions)

What You Can Understand

  • Cation budgeting: The ASI tells you whether your alkali/earth cations are ‚Äúused up‚Äù charge-balancing alumina or are free to modify the network. A glaze with ASI $= 0.4$ has 60% of its cations free for network modification; one with ASI $= 0.9$ has only 10% free.
  • VFT reliability: The Giordano et al. (2008) VFT model was calibrated primarily on metaluminous compositions (ASI $\lt 1$). For ASI $\gt 1.2$, viscosity predictions become unreliable.
  • Durability prediction: Chemical durability models assume 4-coordinated Al. Peraluminous compositions have different leaching mechanisms that the standard models do not capture.

What You Can Calculate

  1. Structural regime classification: Peralkaline / metaluminous / peraluminous ‚Äî each with different property trends.
  2. Free-modifier fraction: $f_{\text{free}} = 1 - \text{ASI}$ (for ASI $\leq 1$) represents the fraction of charge-balancing cations available for network modification.
  3. VFT confidence flag: ASI $\gt 1.2$ should trigger a warning that viscosity predictions are outside the calibration domain.

Worked Example

\[\text{ASI} = \frac{0.35}{0.24 + 0.11 + 0 + 0.42} = \frac{0.35}{0.77} = \boxed{0.45}\]
This is solidly metaluminous (peralkaline, even). Only 45% of the charge-balancing capacity is consumed by alumina, leaving 55% of the cation budget free for network modification. This is consistent with a well-fluxed, moderately fluid glazed.

Limitations

  • MgO is excluded from the denominator in the strict geochemical definition (Mg2+ is too small to effectively charge-balance Al in 4-coordination in silicate glasses, though it can do so in some aluminosilicate compositions). Some formulations include it.
  • The binary at ASI $= 1$ is an idealisation; the transition between 4- and higher-coordinated Al is gradual, not sharp.
  • Does not account for boron, which can also compete for charge-balancing cations.

Equation 4: Combined Alkali

‚ö° Combined Alkali
\[ \text{CA} = [\LiO] + [\NaO] + [\KO] \]

Historical Context

The combined alkali metric has its roots in the glass industry of the 19th and early 20th centuries, where soda-lime-silica (window glass) compositions dominated production. Manufacturers quickly learned that total alkali content was the primary driver of melting cost, thermal expansion, and chemical durability. The metric was formalised in ceramic limit charts by Hesselberth & Roy (2002) and is a standard parameter in the Seger system.

Deep Physical Meaning

Alkali oxides (R2O = Li2O, Na2O, K2O) are the most powerful network modifiers in silicate glass chemistry. Each mole of R2O introduces two monovalent cations (R+) into the glass network. These cations are small, highly mobile, and each one breaks one bridging oxygen bond, creating a non-bridging oxygen (NBO).

The combined alkali metric sums the molar contributions of all three common alkali oxides, providing a single number that captures the total ‚Äúalkali assault‚Äù on the glass network. This matters because:

  1. Thermal expansion: Alkali cations occupy interstitial sites in the network and push apart the surrounding oxygen polyhedra. More alkali = more expansion. The three alkali species have the highest partial thermal-expansion coefficients of any common oxide ($\alpha_{\text{Na}_2\text{O}} \approx 39.5$, $\alpha_{\text{K}_2\text{O}} \approx 46.2$, $\alpha_{\text{Li}_2\text{O}} \approx 27$).
  2. Chemical durability: Alkali ions are the most leachable species in glass. Water attacks the glass surface by ion-exchanging H+ (or H3O+) for Na+/K+, depleting alkali from the surface layer. Higher combined alkali = more material available for leaching = lower durability.
  3. Ionic conductivity: Alkali ions are the primary charge carriers in glass. High combined alkali = high ionic conductivity = microwave absorption = poorer dielectric performance.
  4. Viscosity reduction: Each NBO created by an alkali ion weakens the network locally, reducing the activation energy for viscous flow.

What You Can Understand

  • Crazing risk: CA $\gt 0.5$ in UMF is a strong indicator of crazing risk, because the combined thermal expansion contribution from all three alkalis will likely push $\alpha_{\text{glaze}}$ above most stoneware body COEs.
  • Food safety: High-alkali glazes ($\text{CA} \gt 0.6$) are more susceptible to leaching of any co-present heavy metals (lead, cadmium, barium) because the alkali-depleted surface layer is less protective.
  • Li substitution effect: Li2O has a lower partial expansion coefficient than Na2O or K2O, so substituting lithia for soda reduces expansion while maintaining flux power. This is the principle behind lithia-based craze-resistant formulas.

Equation 5: R2O:RO Ratio

‚ö° R2O:RO Ratio
\[ \text{R}_2\text{O:RO} = \frac{[\LiO] + [\NaO] + [\KO]}{[\CaOx] + [\MgOx] + [\ZnOx] + [\BaOx] + [\SrOx]} \]

Historical Context

The distinction between R2O (alkali) and RO (alkaline-earth) fluxes is one of the oldest organising principles in ceramic chemistry. The Seger formula system (1880s) explicitly groups fluxes into these two families and normalises their sum to unity. The ratio between them captures a fundamental trade-off: alkalis are powerful, low-temperature fluxes with high expansion; earths are gentler, higher-temperature fluxes with low expansion.

Deep Physical Meaning

The R2O:RO ratio captures the character of the flux package:

Alkali-dominated (ratio $\gt 1$): Monovalent cations (Na+, K+, Li+) are small, mobile, and each creates one NBO. The glass is fluid, low-melting, highly expansive, and electrically conductive. These characteristics arise because monovalent cations need only one NBO to satisfy their charge ‚Äî they are ‚Äúcheap‚Äù network breakers.

Earth-dominated (ratio $\lt 1$): Divalent cations (Ca2+, Mg2+, Zn2+) are larger (Ca, Ba, Sr) or smaller but more tightly bonded (Mg), and each requires two NBOs to satisfy its charge. The result is a melt that is stiffer, more refractory, less expansive, and more chemically durable. Divalent cations also promote crystallisation more than monovalent ones, because their stronger field strength encourages local ordering.

What You Can Understand

  • Expansion management: Shifting the ratio toward RO is the primary tool for reducing thermal expansion without reducing total flux content (and thus without reducing melt fluidity).
  • Crystal potential: High RO content (especially CaO) promotes wollastonite and anorthite crystallisation ‚Äî the basis for matte and satin surfaces.
  • Durability optimisation: Replacing R2O with RO at constant total flux dramatically improves chemical durability because divalent cations are much harder to leach than monovalent ones.

  • Treats all earths equivalently, despite vast differences: CaO is a powerful high-temperature flux; MgO is relatively refractory and promotes different crystal phases; ZnO is a strong flux that promotes crystal growth.
  • Does not include PbO or B2O3, both of which are technically RO/R2O3 species with unique fluxing behaviours.
  • The ratio is undefined if the denominator is zero (pure alkali glaze with no earths ‚Äî rare but possible in speciality raku/soda formulations).

Thermal Expansion & Glaze Fit

The next five equations form a tightly coupled chain that begins with thermal expansion and ends with thermal shock resistance, passing through the most practically important topic in all of glaze science: glaze-body fit. A glaze that does not fit its clay body will craze (tensile failure) or shiver (compressive failure), regardless of how beautiful its color, how perfect its surface, or how brilliant its chemistry.

Equation 6: Coefficient of Thermal Expansion (Appen Model)

‚ö° COE ‚Äî Appen Additive Model
\[ \alpha = \sum_i x_i \, \alpha_i \]
where $x_i$ is the mole fraction of oxide $i$ and $\alpha_i$ is the partial thermal-expansion coefficient of oxide $i$ (units: $10^{-6}\;/\text{¬∞C}$).

Historical Context

A.A. Appen (1901‚Äì1971) was a Soviet glass scientist whose 1961 book Chemistry of Glass (–•–∏–º–∏—è —Å—Ç–µ–∫–ª–∞) established additive models as the standard industrial tool for predicting glass properties from composition. Working at the Grebenshchikov Institute of Silicate Chemistry in Leningrad, Appen measured thermal expansion, refractive index, density, and other properties of hundreds of binary and ternary oxide glasses, then showed that each property could be approximated as a weighted sum of per-oxide contributions.

Appen's genius was recognising that, despite the enormous complexity of glass structure at the atomic level, the macroscopic thermal expansion of a multicomponent glass can be decomposed into independent additive contributions from each oxide species. This works because thermal expansion is a volume-averaged property: the bulk expansion is the composition-weighted average of the local expansions around each type of cation site.

Deep Physical Meaning

Thermal expansion arises from the anharmonicity of interatomic potential energy curves. At the atomic level, the potential energy of a pair of bonded atoms is not a symmetric parabola but has a steeper repulsive wall and a shallower attractive tail. As temperature increases (atoms vibrate with larger amplitude), the asymmetry causes the average interatomic distance to increase ‚Äî the material expands.

The magnitude of this expansion depends on the bond strength and coordination geometry around each cation:

  • Strong, directional bonds (Si‚ÄìO, Al‚ÄìO, B‚ÄìO): Small $\alpha_i$ (0.5, $-3$, $-5$ respectively). These network-forming bonds are stiff; vibrations don't shift the mean distance much.
  • Weak, non-directional bonds (Na‚ÄìO, K‚ÄìO): Large $\alpha_i$ (39.5, 46.2). The alkali cations sit in large, loosely-defined coordination polyhedra. Thermal vibration causes large shifts in mean position.
  • Intermediate bonds (Ca‚ÄìO, Mg‚ÄìO): Moderate $\alpha_i$ (13.0, 0.45). Divalent cations are more tightly bonded than alkalis but less so than network formers.
üî¨ Why Al2O3 Has a Negative Expansion Coefficient
One of the most counterintuitive features of the Appen model is that $\alpha_{\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3} \approx -3.0$. How can an oxide reduce thermal expansion?

The answer is that alumina, when it enters the glass network in 4-coordination, consumes a modifier cation for charge balance. That modifier cation would otherwise have contributed its own (large, positive) $\alpha_i$ as a free network modifier. By trapping it in a charge-balancing role, alumina effectively removes a high-expansion contribution. The net effect is negative: adding alumina replaces a high-$\alpha$ modifier contribution with a low-$\alpha$ network site.

This is why alumina is the potter's primary tool for reducing thermal expansion and preventing crazing. Every 0.1 moles of $\AlO$ added to the UMF reduces the COE by roughly $0.3 \times 10^{-6}\;/\text{¬∞C}$.

What You Can Calculate

The COE is the most important upstream property in the thermal chain. It feeds:

  1. Craze Risk (Eq. [eq:dd_craze]): $\Delta\alpha = \alpha_{\text{glaze}} - \alpha_{\text{body}}$.
  2. Glaze Stress (Eq. [eq:dd_stress]): The thermomechanical stress in the glaze layer.
  3. Stress Ratio (Eq. [eq:dd_stress_ratio]): Normalised stress relative to glaze strength.
  4. Thermal Shock Resistance (Eq. [eq:dd_thermal_shock]): Resistance to rapid temperature change.

Using Appen coefficients ($\alpha_i$ values in $10^{-7}\;/\text{¬∞C}$ per mole, normalised after summation):

Limitations

  • Assumes linearity: actual glass expansion is mildly non-linear in composition, especially near phase-separation or crystallisation boundaries.
  • The Appen coefficients were calibrated on soda-lime and borosilicate glasses. For high-lead, high-barium, or high-phosphate compositions, the coefficients may be inaccurate.
  • Does not account for the boron anomaly: at low B2O3 content ($\lt 15$ mol%), boron enters 4-coordination and reduces expansion; at higher content, it enters 3-coordination and the expansion-reduction effect saturates or reverses.
  • Temperature-dependent: the Appen model gives an average COE between room temperature and $T_g$. The actual COE varies with temperature, especially near $T_g$ where it increases sharply.

Equation 7: Craze Risk

‚ö° Craze Risk ‚Äî Expansion Mismatch
\[ \Delta\alpha = \alpha_{\text{glaze}} - \alpha_{\text{body}} \]

Deep Physical Meaning

When a glazed ceramic cools from the glass transition temperature ($T_g$) to room temperature, both the glaze and the clay body contract. If they contract at different rates, a stress develops at the interface. The sign and magnitude of $\Delta\alpha$ determine the nature of this stress:

  • $\Delta\alpha \gt 0$ (glaze contracts more): The glaze wants to be smaller than the body. The body restrains it, putting the glaze under tension. If the tensile stress exceeds the glaze's fracture strength, the glaze cracks ‚Äî this is crazing. Crazing appears as a network of fine cracks (craquelure) on the surface.
  • $\Delta\alpha \lt 0$ (body contracts more): The body wants to be smaller than the glaze. The glaze is put under compression. Moderate compression ($-2 \lt \Delta\alpha \lt 0$ in units of $10^{-6}$) is desirable ‚Äî it prestresses the glaze like tempered glass, increasing strength and preventing crazing. Excessive compression ($\Delta\alpha \lt -3$) can cause shivering ‚Äî the glaze flakes off the body.
  • $\Delta\alpha = 0$ (perfect match): Zero interface stress. Theoretically ideal but practically rare, and actually slightly less desirable than mild compression because it provides no safety margin against crazing.

What You Can Understand

  • The craze/shiver window: The optimal $\Delta\alpha$ is not zero but slightly negative: $-1.5$ to $-0.5$ ($\times 10^{-6}\;/\text{¬∞C}$). This puts the glaze in mild compression, providing a safety margin against crazing from thermal cycling and aging.
  • Delayed crazing: Even if $\Delta\alpha$ is only slightly positive, the glaze may not craze immediately. Stress relaxation in the body (moisture expansion of fired clay over months/years) gradually shifts the effective $\Delta\alpha$ more positive, causing delayed crazing weeks, months, or even years after firing. This is why apparently craze-free glazes can develop crazing in service.
  • The body matters: $\Delta\alpha$ depends on both the glaze and the body. A glaze that fits one body may craze on another. The atlas uses a default body COE (adjustable) to compute craze risk, but the user must know their actual body COE for accurate results.

Equation 8: Glaze Stress

‚ö° Glaze Stress ‚Äî Thermoelastic Model
\[ \sigma = \frac{E \cdot \Delta\alpha \cdot (T_g - 25)}{1 - \nu} \]
where $E$ is Young's modulus (MPa), $\Delta\alpha = \alpha_{\text{body}} - \alpha_{\text{glaze}}$ (note sign convention), $T_g$ is the glass transition temperature (¬∞C), and $\nu$ is Poisson's ratio.

Historical Context

The thermoelastic stress equation for thin coatings on substrates was developed in the 1950s‚Äì1960s by researchers studying metal oxide films and semiconductor devices. The formulation parallels Stoney's formula (1909) for stress in thin films, adapted for the case where the coating (glaze) and substrate (clay body) have different thermal expansion coefficients.

The equation is a direct application of Hooke's Law: the stress is proportional to the strain ($\Delta\alpha \cdot \Delta T$) times the elastic modulus, modified by the biaxial geometry factor $1/(1-\nu)$. The biaxial factor arises because the glaze is constrained in two dimensions (along the surface) but free in the third (perpendicular to the surface).

Deep Physical Meaning

As the glazed pot cools from $T_g$ to room temperature, the glaze tries to contract by a total thermal strain of $\alpha_{\text{glaze}} \cdot (T_g - 25)$, while the body tries to contract by $\alpha_{\text{body}} \cdot (T_g - 25)$. Since they are bonded together, neither can contract freely. The mismatch strain is:

\[\varepsilon = \Delta\alpha \cdot (T_g - 25)\]

This strain generates a biaxial stress in the glaze. The ‚Äúbiaxial‚Äù part is crucial: the glaze is a thin layer bonded over a large area. It cannot contract independently in the plane of the surface ‚Äî it is constrained by the body in all surface directions. The factor $1/(1-\nu)$ accounts for this two-dimensional constraint (it converts uniaxial Hooke's Law to the biaxial case).

Sign convention: In the atlas, positive $\sigma$ indicates compression (safe); negative $\sigma$ indicates tension (crazing risk). This follows the convention $\Delta\alpha = \alpha_{\text{body}} - \alpha_{\text{glaze}}$: if the body contracts more than the glaze, the body pulls the glaze into compression.

What You Can Calculate

  1. Absolute stress magnitude: Typical values range from $-100$ to $+100$ MPa. For comparison, the tensile strength of glass is typically 30‚Äì70 MPa (depending on surface flaws).
  2. Safety factor: $|\sigma| / \sigma_{\text{fracture}}$ gives the fraction of the glaze's strength consumed by the thermal mismatch. Below 0.5 is safe; 0.5‚Äì0.8 is marginal; above 0.8 risks failure.
  3. Stress Ratio (Eq. [eq:dd_stress_ratio]): A normalised version using hardness as a proxy for strength.

Worked Example

With $E = 70,000$ MPa, $\Delta\alpha = 0.7 \times 10^{-6}$, $T_g = 660$¬∞C, $\nu = 0.22$:

\[\begin{aligned} \sigma &= \frac{70,000 \times 0.7 \times 10^{-6} \times (660 - 25)}{1 - 0.22} \\ &= \frac{70,000 \times 0.7 \times 10^{-6} \times 635}{0.78} \\ &= \frac{31.115}{0.78} = \boxed{+39.9 \;\text{MPa} \text{ (compressive)}} \end{aligned}\]
The glaze is under moderate compression ‚Äî below the typical glass fracture strength of 30‚Äì50 MPa in tension. No crazing or shivering risk.

Equation 9: Stress Ratio

‚ö° Stress Ratio
\[ R_\sigma = \frac{|\sigma|}{H/3 \times 1000} \]
where $\sigma$ is the glaze stress (MPa) and $H$ is Vickers hardness (GPa).

Deep Physical Meaning

The stress ratio normalises the absolute thermomechanical stress against the glaze's intrinsic strength, estimated from its hardness. The denominator $H/3 \times 1000$ is an empirical approximation of the tensile strength of a brittle material from its indentation hardness (Tabor, 1951). For most silicate glasses, the tensile strength is roughly $H/3$ to $H/2.5$ (in consistent units).

A stress ratio below 1.0 means the thermal stress is less than the material's strength ‚Äî no failure expected. Above 1.0 means the thermal stress exceeds the strength ‚Äî failure (crazing or shivering) is predicted.

What You Can Understand

  • $R_\sigma$ integrates three independent property chains: thermal expansion (COE chain), elasticity (mechanical chain), and glass transition temperature ‚Äî making it one of the most information-rich single numbers in the atlas.
  • It is a convergence node in the property dependency graph ‚Äî the point where the thermal and mechanical branches meet.
  • Values near 0 indicate a stress-free or very low-stress glaze; values near 1 indicate marginal fit; values well above 1 indicate certain failure.

Worked Example

With $\sigma = 40$ MPa and $H = 4.7$ GPa:

\[R_\sigma = \frac{40}{4700/3 \times 1000} = \frac{40}{4700/3} = \frac{40}{1567} = \boxed{0.026}\]
An extremely low stress ratio ‚Äî this glaze is in no danger of failure. The thermal mismatch stress is only 2.6% of the material's strength.

Equation 10: Thermal Shock Resistance

‚ö° Thermal Shock Resistance
\[ R_{\text{shock}} = \frac{H}{E \cdot \alpha} \]
where $H$ is hardness (GPa), $E$ is Young's modulus (GPa), and $\alpha$ is COE ($\times 10^{-6}\;/\text{¬∞C}$).

Historical Context

D.P.H. Hasselman (1969) developed a comprehensive theory of thermal shock resistance for brittle ceramics, defining several resistance parameters for different failure regimes. The formulation used in the atlas is a simplified version of Hasselman's first thermal-shock parameter, which represents the maximum temperature difference a material can withstand before crack initiation.

Deep Physical Meaning

Thermal shock occurs when a rapid temperature change creates a temperature gradient within the material. The surface heats (or cools) faster than the interior, creating differential thermal strain. This strain generates stress. If the stress exceeds the material's strength, cracks initiate.

The resistance parameter $R_{\text{shock}} = H / (E \cdot \alpha)$ captures the competition between:

  • Strength ($H$): Higher strength = more stress the material can absorb before cracking. Hardness serves as a proxy for tensile strength.
  • Stiffness ($E$): Higher modulus = more stress generated per unit strain. A compliant material deforms rather than stressing; a stiff material resists deformation and builds up high stress.
  • Expansion ($\alpha$): Higher expansion = more dimensional change per degree = more strain from a given temperature difference.

Desirable: High $H$, low $E$, low $\alpha$ $\longrightarrow$ high $R_{\text{shock}}$.

Viscosity & Flow

Viscosity is the central physical property of a ceramic glaze. It governs whether the glaze melts, whether it flows, whether it heals surface defects, whether bubbles can escape, and whether it stays on the pot or runs off the shelf. The VFT equation is the computational heart of the Stull Atlas ‚Äî a single parameterised model that feeds nine downstream properties.

Equation 11: VFT Viscosity Model

‚ö° Vogel‚ÄìFulcher‚ÄìTammann (VFT) Viscosity
\[ \log_{10}\eta = A + \frac{B}{T - C} \]
where $\eta$ is viscosity (Pa¬∑s or Poise), $T$ is absolute temperature (K), and $A$, $B$, $C$ are composition-dependent parameters computed from the oxide analysis via the GRD (Giordano‚ÄìRussell‚ÄìDingwell) model.

Historical Context

The VFT equation is one of the most important empirical equations in all of materials science. It was independently discovered three times:

  1. Hans Vogel (1921): A German chemist who derived the equation while studying the temperature dependence of lubricant oils.
  2. Gordon Fulcher (1925): An American glass scientist at Corning Glass Works who applied it to silicate melts.
  3. Gustav Tammann & Walter Hesse (1926): German physicists who derived it from theoretical considerations of liquid state dynamics.

The equation looks deceptively simple, but it encodes a profound physical truth: the viscosity of glass-forming liquids does not follow a simple Arrhenius law ($\log\eta = A + E_a/RT$). Instead, the apparent activation energy for viscous flow increases as the temperature approaches a critical value $C$ (the Kauzmann temperature, roughly 50‚Äì100 K below $T_g$). At $T = C$, the equation predicts infinite viscosity ‚Äî the liquid has effectively become an infinitely rigid solid.

The Giordano, Russell & Dingwell (2008) parameterisation (GRD model) expresses $A$, $B$, and $C$ as functions of molar oxide composition through a multivariate regression calibrated against a database of 1,770 measured viscosity points spanning natural silicate melts, synthetic oxide glasses, and industrial compositions. This is the most comprehensive composition-dependent VFT calibration available.

Deep Physical Meaning

Parameter $A$ (typically $-4$ to $-6$): The infinite-temperature limit of $\log_{10}\eta$. Since $B/(T-C) \to 0$ as $T \to \infty$, $A$ represents the viscosity the melt would have at impossibly high temperatures ‚Äî essentially the ‚Äúphonon gas‚Äù limit where all structural barriers to flow have vanished. It is weakly composition-dependent.

Parameter $B$ (typically $4,000$ to $12,000$ K): The activation energy scale for viscous flow. Large $B$ means the viscosity changes dramatically with temperature ‚Äî the viscosity-temperature curve is steep. This is characteristic of ‚Äúfragile‚Äù glass-formers in Angell's classification (1995). Small $B$ means the viscosity changes gently ‚Äî a ‚Äústrong‚Äù glass former. Silica glass is the archetypal ‚Äústrong‚Äù glass ($B \approx 30,000$ K but nearly Arrhenius); multi-component silicates are ‚Äúfragile‚Äù ($B \approx 5,000‚Äì10,000$ K with strongly non-Arrhenius behaviour).

Parameter $C$ (typically $200$ to $600$ K): The ‚ÄúVFT temperature‚Äù or ‚Äúideal glass transition.‚Äù It represents the temperature at which configurational entropy vanishes (Adam‚ÄìGibbs theory) and the liquid would reach infinite viscosity. $C$ is always below $T_g$ (typically 50‚Äì200 K below). A high $C$ indicates a fragile glass; a low $C$ (approaching 0) indicates a strong glass. For most ceramic glazes, $C$ falls in the range 400‚Äì550 K.

The VFT prediction is a smooth, S-shaped curve when plotted as $\log_{10}\eta$ vs. $1/T$. At high temperatures (the melt regime), viscosity is low and changes slowly. At intermediate temperatures (the working range), viscosity increases steeply. Near $T_g$, the curve flattens again as the glass approaches the solid state.

The VFT model is the most productive single equation in the atlas, feeding 9 downstream properties:

  2. Softening Point: The temperature where $\log_{10}\eta = 7.6$ ‚Äî used as a firing-range indicator.
  3. Annealing Point: Where $\log_{10}\eta = 13$ ‚Äî stress relaxation in 15 minutes; critical for cooling schedules.
  4. Strain Point: Where $\log_{10}\eta = 14.5$ ‚Äî below this, the glass accumulates permanent stress.
  5. Flow Point: Where $\log_{10}\eta = 4$ ‚Äî the glaze begins to flow under gravity.
  6. Working Range: The temperature interval between the flow point and softening point ‚Äî wider range = more forgiving firing.
  7. Soak Benefit Index (Eq. [eq:dd_soak]): How much additional hold time helps.
  8. Pinhole Risk: High viscosity traps surface defects.
  9. Bubble Risk: High viscosity traps interior bubbles.
  10. Healing Index: Low viscosity + high surface tension = self-repair.

Worked Example

For the reference composition, the GRD model yields: $A = -4.55$, $B = 8102$ K, $C = 466$ K.

Limitations

  • The GRD model was calibrated primarily on natural silicate melts (basalt-rhyolite series) and may be less accurate for:
    • Very high B2O3 compositions ($\gt 0.5$ UMF)
    • Lead-based glazes (PbO $\gt 0.3$)
    • Peraluminous compositions (ASI $\gt 1.2$)
  • The VFT equation itself breaks down near $T_g$ (where cooperative molecular motions become important) and at very high temperatures (where the viscosity approaches the phonon gas limit).
  • Does not model crystallisation: if the glaze devitrifies during cooling, the effective viscosity increases dramatically as crystals form.
  • Volatile losses during firing change the actual composition of the melt, making the VFT prediction for the original recipe inaccurate for the actual fired glass.

  • Firing-range assessment: If $T_{\text{flow}}$ is above your cone temperature, the glaze won't flow ‚Äî it's underfired. If $T_{\text{soft}}$ is far below your cone temperature, the glaze is wildly overfired and will run.

Equation 13: Soak Benefit Index

‚ö° Soak Benefit Index
\[ S = \left|\frac{B}{(T - C)^2}\right| \]
evaluated at $T = T_{\text{cone}}$ (the firing temperature in Kelvin).

Deep Physical Meaning

The soak benefit index is the derivative of the VFT equation with respect to temperature:

\[\frac{d(\log_{10}\eta)}{dT} = -\frac{B}{(T - C)^2}\]

This derivative tells you how fast the viscosity changes with temperature at the current firing temperature. It answers a profoundly practical question: if I hold the kiln at peak temperature for another 30 minutes instead of shutting off immediately, how much more will the glaze mature?

Large $S$ (steep curve): The glaze is on a steep part of the VFT curve, meaning its viscosity is changing rapidly with temperature. An extra soak at peak temperature will produce a large additional decrease in viscosity ‚Äî the glaze will flow more, heal more defects, smooth out more pinholes. The soak is highly beneficial.

Small $S$ (flat curve): The glaze is on a flat part of the VFT curve. Its viscosity is nearly constant around the firing temperature. Soaking longer won't significantly change anything. The soak is wasteful of time, energy, and kiln wear.

üìù When Soaking Matters vs. When It Doesn't
Scenario A ‚Äî High soak benefit: A stoneware glaze at cone 6 with $B = 10,000$ K and $T - C = 900$ K: $S = 10,000/810,000 = 0.012$. The VFT curve is moderately steep. A 30-minute soak reduces $\log_{10}\eta$ by approximately $0.3$ units ‚Äî equivalent to raising the temperature by ‚àº20¬∞C. This is a significant improvement for pinholes and surface smoothness.

Scenario B ‚Äî Low soak benefit: A low-fire glaze at cone 04 with $B = 5,000$ K and $T - C = 800$ K: $S = 5,000/640,000 = 0.008$. The curve is flatter. A 30-minute soak changes virtually nothing. The glaze is already fully mature.

What You Can Calculate

  1. Optimal soak duration: Compositions with high $S$ should receive extended soaks (30‚Äì60 minutes) at peak temperature. Those with low $S$ can be fired on a faster schedule.
  2. Firing strategy: A high $S$ value also suggests that the glaze would benefit from a ‚Äúdrop and soak‚Äù strategy: firing 1‚Äì2 cones above target, then dropping rapidly and soaking at target temperature.
  3. Energy cost-benefit: For industrial production, $S$ quantifies the return on investment for additional kiln time ‚Äî critical for scheduling and cost optimisation.

Worked Example

\[S = \frac{8102}{(1495 - 466)^2} = \frac{8102}{1029^2} = \frac{8102}{1,058,841} = \boxed{0.00765 \;/\text{K}}\]
Moderate soak benefit ‚Äî a 20‚Äì30 minute soak at cone 6 will help heal surface defects but is not critical for overall melting.

Glass Network Structure

The next four equations peer inside the glass at the atomic scale, describing the topology, connectivity, and electronic character of the oxide network. They answer the question: what does this glass look like at the scale of individual atoms and bonds?

Equation 14: Non-Bridging Oxygens per Tetrahedron (NBO/T)

‚ö° NBO/T ‚Äî Network Connectivity
\[ \frac{\text{NBO}}{T} = \frac{2\sum[\text{modifiers}] - [\AlO]}{[\SiO] + [\AlO]} \]
where ‚Äúmodifiers‚Äù includes all RO and R2O species, each counting by the number of cations delivered: $[\NaO]$ contributes $2 \times$ (two Na+ per formula unit), $[\CaOx]$ contributes $1 \times$, etc. The ‚Äú$2\sum$‚Äù in the numerator accounts for this: each mole of R2O delivers 2 modifier cations; each mole of RO delivers 1.

Historical Context

The NBO/T concept was formalised by Mysen, Virgo & Scarfe (1980) in their seminal studies of silicate melt structure using Raman spectroscopy. They showed that the Raman spectra of silicate glasses and melts could be decomposed into contributions from tetrahedral units with different numbers of non-bridging oxygens, and that the average NBO/T calculated from bulk composition closely matched the spectroscopic measurement.

The concept builds on Zachariasen's (1932) random network theory: glass consists of corner-sharing $\text{SiO}_4$ and $\text{AlO}_4$ tetrahedra. Each oxygen that bridges two tetrahedra (Si‚ÄìO‚ÄìSi or Si‚ÄìO‚ÄìAl) is a ‚Äúbridging oxygen‚Äù (BO). Each oxygen bonded to only one tetrahedral cation plus one or more modifier cations is a ‚Äúnon-bridging oxygen‚Äù (NBO). The NBO/T ratio counts the average number of NBOs per tetrahedral unit.

Deep Physical Meaning

NBO/T is the most fundamental structural parameter of a silicate glass. It directly measures the degree of polymerisation of the network:

0Fully polymerisedQ4 (vitreous silica)Diamond-like 3D network
1Sheet structureQ3Mica-like layers
2Chain structureQ2Polymer chains
3Dimer structureQ1Paired tetrahedra
4Isolated tetrahedraQ0Olivine (discrete SiO44-)

Most ceramic glazes have NBO/T between 0.2 and 1.2, corresponding to a network dominated by Q3 and Q2 species with some Q4 ‚Äî a partially depolymerised three-dimensional network.

üî¨ The Alumina Subtraction in the Numerator
The $-[\AlO]$ term in the numerator is critical. Each mole of $\AlO$ in 4-coordination consumes one modifier cation for charge balance (as discussed in the ASI section). That consumed modifier can no longer create NBOs. So the effective number of NBO-creating modifiers is the total modifier cation count minus the alumina moles.

If $[\AlO]$ exceeds the available modifier cations (ASI $\gt 1$), the numerator goes negative, which is physically interpreted as an indication that there are insufficient modifiers for even the charge-balancing function ‚Äî some Al3+ must adopt higher coordination, and the NBO/T framework loses validity.

What You Can Understand

  • Viscosity trend: NBO/T is the strongest single predictor of melt viscosity. More NBOs = more broken bonds = lower viscosity = more fluid melt. A 1-unit increase in NBO/T corresponds to roughly a 2‚Äì3 order-of-magnitude decrease in viscosity at a given temperature.
  • Chemical durability: Each NBO is a potential attack site for water. Higher NBO/T = more sites = lower durability. The most durable glasses (Pyrex, vitreous silica) have NBO/T $\approx 0$.
  • Surface texture prediction: NBO/T $\lt 0.3$: likely matte or dry (very polymerised, very viscous). NBO/T $0.3‚Äì0.8$: stoneware gloss range. NBO/T $\gt 1.0$: very fluid, may run.
  • Colorant behaviour: The coordination environment of transition-metal colorants depends on NBO/T. In highly polymerised glasses (low NBO/T), there are few available non-bridging sites, and colorant cations tend to be forced into tetrahedral coordination. In depolymerised glasses (high NBO/T), octahedral coordination is more accessible. This shifts colours.

Equation 15: Optical Basicity

‚ö° Optical Basicity ‚Äî Duffy & Ingram
\[ \Lambda = \sum_i x_i \, \Lambda_i \]
where $x_i$ is the equivalent fraction of oxide $i$ (normalised by the number of oxygen atoms each oxide contributes) and $\Lambda_i$ is the basicity moderating parameter of species $i$.

Historical Context

John A. Duffy and Michael D. Ingram, working at the University of Aberdeen, Scotland, introduced optical basicity in 1971 and published the definitive formulation in 1976. Their insight was revolutionary: they showed that a single parameter, derived from the UV absorption edge of probe ions in glass, could quantify the electron-donating power of the oxide ion in any given glass composition.

The concept of ‚Äúbasicity‚Äù in oxide chemistry is analogous to Br\o nsted‚ÄìLowry basicity in aqueous chemistry. Just as a base in water donates electron pairs to protons, the oxide ion O2- in glass donates electron density to cations. The more electron density the oxide ion can donate, the more ‚Äúbasic‚Äù the glass. This basicity is controlled by the surrounding cations: strong, small, highly-charged cations (Si4+, Al3+) withdraw electron density from O2- (making the glass more ‚Äúacidic‚Äù); weak, large, low-charged cations (Na+, K+, Ba2+) leave more electron density on O2- (making the glass more ‚Äúbasic‚Äù).

Deep Physical Meaning

Optical basicity captures the average electron density on oxygen atoms in the glass. This single number correlates with an extraordinary range of properties:

  1. Redox equilibria: The Fe2+/Fe3+ ratio, Cu+/Cu2+ ratio, and other multivalent-cation equilibria in glass are strongly controlled by $\Lambda$. High $\Lambda$ promotes reduction (more electron density available to reduce cations).
  2. Sulfur solubility and speciation: High-$\Lambda$ glasses dissolve sulfur as sulfide (S2-); low-$\Lambda$ glasses as sulfate (SO42-). This is critical in float glass manufacturing.
  3. Color: The color of transition-metal ions in glass depends on their coordination environment, which is controlled by $\Lambda$. Cobalt blue is most intense at intermediate $\Lambda$; copper red (colloidal Cu0) requires high $\Lambda$ for reduction.
  4. UV absorption edge: High $\Lambda$ shifts the absorption edge to longer wavelengths, making the glass more yellow/amber.
  5. Optical bandgap: Dimitrov & Komatsu (2002, 2010) showed a linear relationship $E_g \approx 4.0 - 1.5\Lambda$ (Eq. [eq:dd_bandgap]).

Equation 16: Phillips‚ÄìThorpe Constraint Counting

‚ö° Phillips‚ÄìThorpe Rigidity Theory
\[ n_c = \sum_i f_i \left[\frac{r_i}{2} + (2r_i - 3)\right] \]
where $f_i$ is the fraction of atoms of species $i$, $r_i$ is the coordination number of species $i$, and $n_c$ is the average number of constraints per atom.

Historical Context

James C. Phillips (1979) at AT&T Bell Laboratories proposed that the glass-forming ability of covalent networks could be predicted by counting the mechanical constraints on each atom. Michael F. Thorpe (1983) independently developed the rigidity percolation theory that provided the mathematical backbone. Together, they established one of the most elegant results in condensed-matter physics: the rigidity threshold at $n_c = 3$.

Deep Physical Meaning

Every atom in a glass network is subject to two types of mechanical constraint:

  1. Bond-stretching constraints: $r/2$ per atom (each bond is shared between two atoms). A 4-coordinated Si has $4/2 = 2$ stretching constraints.
  2. Bond-bending constraints: $(2r - 3)$ per atom. The angular relationships between bonds around an atom. A 4-coordinated Si has $2(4) - 3 = 5$ bending constraints.
  3. Total per atom: $r/2 + (2r - 3) = 5r/2 - 3$.

The average over all atoms gives $n_c$. The critical insight is:

‚ÑπÔ∏è The Rigidity Threshold
  • $n_c \lt 3$: The network is floppy (underconstrained). There are more degrees of freedom than constraints. The network can deform without breaking bonds ‚Äî it is a good glass former because the lack of rigidity prevents the ordered atomic rearrangements needed for crystallisation.
  • $n_c = 3$: The isostatic (Maxwell) condition. Constraints exactly equal degrees of freedom. The network is rigid but unstressed ‚Äî the ideal glass-forming composition. These glasses have the widest stability windows and the best optical properties.
  • $n_c \gt 3$: The network is stressed-rigid (overconstrained). Some constraints must be violated, creating internal stress. The glass is less stable and more prone to crystallisation.

What You Can Understand

  • Glass stability: Compositions near $n_c = 3$ form the most stable glasses ‚Äî they resist devitrification during cooling and have wide working ranges.
  • Mechanical optimum: The Phillips rigidity window ($n_c \approx 2.4‚Äì3.0$) often coincides with optimal mechanical properties ‚Äî the glass is stiff without being internally stressed.
  • Compositional design: Adding modifiers reduces $n_c$ (they have low coordination). Adding network formers increases $n_c$. The potter can use $n_c$ to steer toward the stability optimum.

Worked Example

For the reference composition, computing atom fractions and coordination numbers:

  • Si ($r = 4$): $n_{\text{Si}} = 5(4)/2 - 3 = 7$ constraints per Si atom
  • Al ($r = 4$): $n_{\text{Al}} = 7$ constraints per Al atom
  • O ($r = 2$): $n_{\text{O}} = 5(2)/2 - 3 = 2$ constraints per O atom
  • Na, K, Ca, Mg, Zn ($r = $ varies, typically 1‚Äì2 effective constraints via ionic bonding)
After weighted averaging over all atom types: $n_c \approx \boxed{2.7}$

This is below the rigidity threshold of 3 ‚Äî the network is slightly floppy. This is typical of well-fluxed stoneware glazes and indicates good glass-forming ability.

Equation 17: Q-Species Distribution

‚ö° Q-Species ‚Äî Network Speciation
\[ Q^n: \quad n = 4 - \frac{\text{NBO}}{T} \quad \text{(integer approximation of dominant species)} \]
where $Q^n$ denotes a tetrahedral unit with $n$ bridging oxygens (and $4 - n$ non-bridging oxygens).

Historical Context

The Q-species notation was introduced by Engelhardt & Michel (1987) for describing silicon environments in NMR spectroscopy. The superscript $n$ indicates the number of bridging oxygens on a given $\text{SiO}_4$ (or $\text{AlO}_4$) tetrahedron: Q4 = fully bridged (4 BO, 0 NBO), Q3 = one NBO, Q2 = two NBOs, down to Q0 = isolated tetrahedron (0 BO, 4 NBO).

Stebbins (1987) showed that the Q-species distribution in silicate glasses is not simply the statistical average implied by the bulk NBO/T: instead, a disproportionation equilibrium exists:

\[2\,Q^n \rightleftharpoons Q^{n-1} + Q^{n+1}\]
This means the actual distribution is narrower than a random model would predict ‚Äî Q-species tend to cluster near the dominant species rather than distributing uniformly.

Deep Physical Meaning

The Q-species distribution provides a more detailed structural picture than the single-number NBO/T. Instead of knowing ‚Äúthe average tetrahedron has 0.46 NBOs,‚Äù you know the population of each type of tetrahedron:

  • Q4 tetrahedra: The fully connected backbone. These regions are vitreous silica-like: hard, chemically resistant, low-expansion.
  • Q3 tetrahedra: Sheet-like regions with one dangling bond per tetrahedron. These are the most common species in stoneware glazes and contribute to the characteristic balance of fluidity and durability.
  • Q2 tetrahedra: Chain-like regions. Present in significant numbers in high-flux glazes. These regions are relatively weak and easily leached.
  • Q1/Q0 tetrahedra: Isolated or paired units. Rare in most ceramic glazes except extremely low-silica compositions.

What You Can Understand

  • Raman spectroscopy prediction: Each Q-species produces a characteristic Raman band (Q0: ‚àº850$~\text{cm}^{-1}$; Q1: ‚àº900$~\text{cm}^{-1}$; Q2: ‚àº950$~\text{cm}^{-1}$; Q3: ‚àº1050$~\text{cm}^{-1}$; Q4: ‚àº1100$~\text{cm}^{-1}$). The predicted Q-species distribution tells you what a Raman spectrum of the glaze should look like.
  • Heterogeneity indicator: A broad Q-species distribution (significant populations of 3+ species) indicates a structurally heterogeneous glass. A narrow distribution (one dominant species) indicates structural homogeneity.
  • Leaching susceptibility: Q1 and Q0 species are the most susceptible to leaching. Their abundance predicts the rate of network dissolution in acidic or alkaline environments.

Mechanical Properties

The mechanical equations form a cascade: Young's modulus ($E$) is the upstream value from which hardness, Poisson's ratio, bulk modulus, shear modulus, fracture toughness, and brittleness all derive.

Equation 18: Young's Modulus (Makishima‚ÄìMackenzie)

‚ö° Young's Modulus ‚Äî Additive Model
\[ E = \sum_i x_i \, E_i \]
where $x_i$ is the mole fraction of oxide $i$ and $E_i$ is the partial Young's modulus contribution of oxide $i$ (GPa).

Historical Context

Akio Makishima and John D. Mackenzie (1973), working at UCLA, demonstrated that the Young's modulus of multicomponent oxide glasses could be predicted from composition using an additive model. Their key insight was that the elastic modulus of a glass is determined by two factors: (1) the bond-stretching force constants of each oxide bond type, and (2) the packing density ‚Äî how tightly the atoms are packed.

The Makishima‚ÄìMackenzie model provides surprisingly accurate predictions ($\pm 5‚Äì10\%$) for a wide range of oxide glass compositions, from soda-lime to aluminoborosilicate to heavy-metal glasses.

Deep Physical Meaning

Young's modulus measures the stiffness of a material ‚Äî its resistance to elastic deformation under stress. In a glass, stiffness arises from the force constants of the interatomic bonds:

  • Strong, short bonds (Si‚ÄìO: ‚àº640$~\text{kJ/mol}$, Al‚ÄìO: ‚àº500$~\text{kJ/mol}$): High $E_i$. Network formers contribute high stiffness.
  • Weak, long bonds (Na‚ÄìO: ‚àº80$~\text{kJ/mol}$, K‚ÄìO: ‚àº50$~\text{kJ/mol}$): Low $E_i$. Modifier cations contribute low stiffness (and their presence reduces packing density, further reducing stiffness).
  • Intermediate bonds (Ca‚ÄìO: ‚àº130$~\text{kJ/mol}$, Mg‚ÄìO: ‚àº155$~\text{kJ/mol}$): $\MgOx$ is notable for contributing high stiffness despite being a modifier, because Mg2+ is small and tightly bonded.

What You Can Calculate

Young's modulus is the root of the mechanical cascade:

  1. Hardness $H \approx E/15$ (Eq. [eq:dd_hardness])
  2. Poisson's ratio $\nu$ (Eq. [eq:dd_poisson])
  3. Bulk modulus $K = E / [3(1-2\nu)]$ (Eq. [eq:dd_elastic]a)
  4. Shear modulus $G = E / [2(1+\nu)]$ (Eq. [eq:dd_elastic]b)
  5. Fracture toughness $K_{IC} = 0.016\sqrt{E}$ (Eq. [eq:dd_elastic]c)
  6. Brittleness $B = H / K_{IC}$ (Eq. [eq:dd_elastic]d)
  7. Thermal shock resistance $R = H / (E\alpha)$ (Eq. [eq:dd_thermal_shock])
  8. Glaze stress $\sigma = E \Delta\alpha (T_g - 25)/(1-\nu)$ (Eq. [eq:dd_stress])

Changing $E$ simultaneously affects all eight downstream properties.

Equation 19: Vickers Hardness

‚ö° Vickers Hardness ‚Äî Sehgal & Ito Correlation
\[ H \approx \frac{E}{15} \]
where $E$ is in GPa and $H$ is in GPa.

Historical Context

Jagjit Sehgal and Satoshi Ito (1999) at Tokyo Institute of Technology established this empirical correlation by measuring Vickers hardness and Young's modulus for a wide range of oxide glasses. The $E/15$ relationship arises from the fact that, in brittle materials, the indentation process involves a mix of elastic and plastic deformation, with the ratio between them being roughly constant across oxide glass compositions.

Deep Physical Meaning

Hardness measures resistance to localised permanent deformation ‚Äî specifically, the resistance to a diamond pyramid (Vickers geometry) being pressed into the surface. In a glass:

  • The indenter creates a small zone of permanently densified and shear-deformed material beneath the indent.
  • The force required is proportional to both the elastic modulus (resistance to elastic compression) and the yield strength (resistance to irreversible densification).
  • For oxide glasses, the ratio $H/E$ is approximately constant at ‚àº1/15, because the densification and shear mechanisms scale similarly with bond strength.

Practical significance:

  1. Scratch resistance: A harder glaze resists surface scratching from cutlery, cleaning abrasives, and everyday use. $H \gt 5$ GPa is comparable to window glass.
  2. Wear resistance: For floor tiles and industrial applications, hardness determines service life.
  3. Food-contact suitability: Harder glazes resist the progressive surface damage that can eventually expose leachable species beneath the intact surface layer.

Worked Example

\[H = \frac{70}{15} = \boxed{4.67\;\text{GPa}}\]
Comparable to standard window glass ‚Äî adequate for functional dinnerware.

Equation 20: Poisson's Ratio

‚ö° Poisson's Ratio ‚Äî Additive Model
\[ \nu = \sum_i x_i \, \nu_i \]
where $\nu_i$ are partial Poisson's ratio contributions for each oxide.

Deep Physical Meaning

Poisson's ratio $\nu$ describes how much a material contracts laterally when stretched axially (or expands laterally when compressed axially). It is the ratio of transverse strain to axial strain:

\[\nu = -\frac{\varepsilon_{\text{transverse}}}{\varepsilon_{\text{axial}}}\]

For oxide glasses:

  • $\nu \approx 0.17$: Vitreous silica (a very open, low-density network that can densify under stress rather than deforming laterally).
  • $\nu \approx 0.22‚Äì0.25$: Typical soda-lime and stoneware glazes.
  • $\nu \approx 0.27‚Äì0.30$: Heavy-modifier glasses (dense, close-packed).

The physical origin lies in the atomic packing: loosely packed networks (low-modifier, high-$\SiO$) have low $\nu$ because the open network accommodates compression by collapsing internal voids. Densely packed networks (high-modifier) have higher $\nu$ because there are no voids to collapse ‚Äî deformation must cause lateral expansion.

What You Can Calculate

Poisson's ratio serves as the second input (alongside $E$) for computing $K$, $G$, and the glaze stress:

  • Bulk modulus: $K = E / [3(1-2\nu)]$
  • Shear modulus: $G = E / [2(1+\nu)]$
  • Biaxial stress factor: $1 / (1 - \nu)$

Worked Example

\[\nu \approx \boxed{0.22}\]
Standard for modest-alkali stoneware ‚Äî between the open silica network ($\nu = 0.17$) and dense soda-lime ($\nu = 0.25$).

‚ö° Elastic Moduli ‚Äî Derived from $E$ and $\nu$
\[\begin{aligned} K &= \frac{E}{3(1 - 2\nu)} & &[\text{Bulk modulus, \text{GPa}}] \\[4pt] G &= \frac{E}{2(1 + \nu)} & &[\text{Shear modulus, \text{GPa}}] \\[4pt] K_{IC} &= 0.016\sqrt{E} & &[\text{Fracture toughness, \text{MPa‚àöm}}] \\[4pt] B &= \frac{H}{K_{IC}} & &[\text{Brittleness, \text{/Œºm}}] \end{aligned}\]

Deep Physical Meaning

These four equations complete the mechanical characterisation of the glaze:

Bulk Modulus $K$: Resistance to uniform compression (hydrostatic pressure). A high $K$ means the glass resists volume change. $K$ connects to the glass's response to firing pressure (kiln atmosphere, gas evolution) and to the Clausius‚ÄìMossotti dielectric model (Eq. [eq:dd_cm]).

Shear Modulus $G$: Resistance to shape change at constant volume. $G$ determines the glass's response to tangential stresses (stirring, wiping, abrasion). For most oxide glasses, $G \approx 0.4E$.

Fracture Toughness $K_{IC}$: The critical stress intensity factor ‚Äî the maximum stress that can be applied to a crack tip before the crack propagates. The $0.016\sqrt{E}$ correlation (derived by Sehgal & Ito from indentation fracture data) is specific to oxide glasses and reflects the constancy of the $K_{IC}/\sqrt{E}$ ratio across a wide range of glass compositions.

Fracture toughness is arguably the most practically important mechanical property: it determines whether a pot survives an impact. $K_{IC} \approx 0.13$ MPa‚àöm for typical stoneware glazes, compared to ‚àº1.0 for engineering ceramics (alumina, zirconia) and ‚àº50 for steel.

Brittleness $B = H/K_{IC}$: Lawn & Marshall (1979) defined the brittleness index as the ratio of hardness (resistance to deformation) to fracture toughness (resistance to cracking). A high $B$ means the material is hard but cracks easily ‚Äî it shatters rather than denting. A low $B$ means the material can absorb some impact before failing.

Temperature & Volatility

Equation 22: Glass Transition Temperature

‚ö° Glass Transition Temperature ‚Äî Priven Model
\[ T_g = \sum_i x_i \, T_{g,i} \]
where $T_{g,i}$ are partial $T_g$ contributions for each oxide (in ¬∞C or K).

Historical Context

Alexander Priven (2004) at Saint-Petersburg State Technological University developed a comprehensive additive model for the glass transition temperature of multicomponent oxide glasses. Building on earlier work by Mazurin et al. (SciGlass database), Priven calibrated partial $T_g$ coefficients against thousands of measured $T_g$ values for binary, ternary, and multicomponent systems.

Deep Physical Meaning

The glass transition temperature $T_g$ marks the boundary between two fundamentally different states of matter:

  • Above $T_g$: The material is a supercooled liquid. Atoms are mobile; the structure can rearrange in response to changes in temperature, pressure, or stress. Viscosity is below $\sim 10^{12}$ Pa¬∑s. The material flows (slowly) and relaxes stress.
  • Below $T_g$: The material is a glass ‚Äî a frozen liquid. The structure is ‚Äúlocked in‚Äù at whatever configuration existed when the material cooled through $T_g$. Atoms can vibrate but cannot rearrange. Stress is permanent (no relaxation). The thermal expansion coefficient drops abruptly.

$T_g$ is not a thermodynamic phase transition (like melting) but a kinetic phenomenon: it depends on the cooling rate. Faster cooling = higher apparent $T_g$ (the glass freezes at a higher temperature because there is less time for structural relaxation). For practical purposes, $T_g$ is reproducible within $\pm 5$¬∞C for a given cooling rate.

What You Can Calculate

$T_g$ feeds into:

  1. Glaze stress (Eq. [eq:dd_stress]): The thermal strain accumulates from $T_g$ to room temperature, so a higher $T_g$ means more total strain and more stress.
  2. Turnbull ratio (Eq. [eq:dd_turnbull]): $T_{rg} = T_g / T_m$ ‚Äî the glass-forming ability predictor.
  3. Hrub√Ω parameter (Eq. [eq:dd_hruby]): Uses $T_g$ as the lower bound of the stability window.
  4. Crystal window: $T_m - T_g$ defines the temperature range for crystal growth.
  5. Annealing/strain points: While formally derived from VFT, $T_g$ serves as an independent cross-check.

Equation 23: Volatilisation Index

‚ö° Volatilisation Index
\[ V = w_{\text{Na}} [\NaO] + w_{\text{K}} [\KO] + w_{\text{B}} [\BO] + w_{\text{Pb}} [\PbOx] + w_{\text{Zn}} [\ZnOx] + w_{\text{Li}} [\LiO] \]
where $w_i$ are volatility weighting factors (higher = more volatile).

Deep Physical Meaning

Certain oxides evaporate significantly during the high-temperature firing of ceramic glazes. This volatilisation means the composition of the fired glaze differs from the batch recipe. The volatilisation index quantifies how vulnerable a composition is to this effect.

The most volatile common glaze oxides:

  1. B2O3: Extremely volatile above 1100 ¬∞C. Can lose 10‚Äì30% of its boron content during a cone 6 firing, and up to 50% at cone 10. This is why boron-containing glazes often behave differently in thinner applications (more loss proportionally).
  2. Na2O: Volatilises as Na metal or NaOH at high temperatures. Loss of 5‚Äì15% is common at stoneware temperatures.
  3. K2O: Similar to Na2O but slightly less volatile due to higher molar mass.
  4. PbO: Highly volatile above 1000 ¬∞C ‚Äî one of the major safety and environmental concerns with lead glazes.
  5. ZnO: Moderately volatile; zinc fuming can create distinctive surface effects.
  6. Li2O: Volatile at high temperatures; lithium loss can shift the expansion and melting point.

What You Can Understand

  • Property drift: A high volatilisation index warns that the computed properties (COE, viscosity, NBO/T, etc.) may not match the fired result because the volatile loss changes the effective composition. Specifically, volatile loss increases the relative proportion of $\SiO$ and $\AlO$ (which don't volatilise), making the fired glass more refractory, more viscous, and lower in expansion than predicted.
  • Thickness sensitivity: Thin glaze applications lose a larger fraction of their volatile content than thick ones (surface-to-volume ratio effect). This means thin areas on a pot may behave differently from thick pooled areas.
  • Kiln atmosphere: Volatile loss is accelerated in reduction atmospheres (which lower the partial pressure of oxide vapours) and in well-ventilated kilns (which sweep away volatiles).

Using estimated weighting factors ($w_{\text{Na}} = 3$, $w_{\text{K}} = 2$, $w_{\text{B}} = 5$, $w_{\text{Zn}} = 1$):

Historical Context

The additive approach works because the refractive index of a glass is related to its molar refractivity (a measure of the polarisability of the constituent ions), and molar refractivity is well approximated as an additive property.

Deep Physical Meaning

The refractive index $n$ measures how much the glass slows light relative to vacuum. The slowdown arises from the interaction between the electromagnetic wave and the electron clouds of the atoms:

  • Highly polarisable ions (Pb2+, Ba2+, Ti4+, Bi3+): Large electron clouds that interact strongly with light. High $n_i$. Lead crystal has $n \approx 1.65‚Äì1.80$ because Pb2+ is enormously polarisable.
  • Low-polarisability ions (Si4+, B3+): Small, tightly held electron clouds. Low $n_i$. Vitreous silica has $n = 1.458$.
  • Intermediate (Ca2+, Na+, Al3+): Moderate contributions.

The connection to packing density is also important: a denser glass has more atoms per unit volume interacting with the light, increasing $n$ even if the ions themselves are not highly polarisable.

What You Can Calculate

  1. Luster / surface reflectance: Via Fresnel's equation (Eq. [eq:dd_fresnel]). Higher $n$ = more reflective = more ‚Äúglossy.‚Äù
  2. Brilliance: In glass art and crystal design, higher $n$ produces more light dispersion and sparkle.
  3. Layered-glaze effects: If two glaze layers have different $n$ values, light partially reflects at the interface, creating optical depth and visual complexity.
  4. Colour saturation: Higher $n$ increases the path length of light through the glaze layer (via increased internal reflection), enhancing colour saturation.

Equation 25: Fresnel Luster

‚ö° Fresnel Surface Reflectance (Luster)
\[ R = \left(\frac{n - 1}{n + 1}\right)^2 \times 100\% \]

Historical Context

Augustin-Jean Fresnel (1788‚Äì1827), the French physicist who established the wave theory of light, derived the reflection and transmission coefficients for light at an interface between two media. For normal incidence (light hitting the surface straight on) at an air-glass boundary, the reflectance reduces to the beautifully simple expression above.

Deep Physical Meaning

This equation connects the atomic-level property (refractive index, determined by ion polarisability and packing) to the macroscopic visual property that the potter observes: how shiny is the surface?

The key insight is that luster is not a surface coating phenomenon ‚Äî it is an intrinsic bulk property of the glass. A perfectly smooth, uncontaminated surface of any glass will have a specific, predictable reflectance determined entirely by its composition through $n$. The potter cannot make a glaze ‚Äúshinier‚Äù by polishing harder ‚Äî only by changing the chemistry.

üî¨ Why Matte Glazes Look Matte
If Fresnel reflectance is determined by composition rather than surface roughness, why do matte glazes exist?

The answer reveals that matte surfaces are not low-reflectance surfaces ‚Äî they are high-roughness surfaces. Each tiny facet on a rough matte surface reflects light with the same Fresnel reflectance $R$ as a polished surface. But those facets point in random directions, so the reflected light scatters diffusely rather than producing a specular (mirror-like) highlight. The total reflected energy is the same; it is merely distributed across all angles rather than concentrated in one direction.

A truly low-$n$ glass (like a borosilicate with $n \approx 1.47$) would appear subtly less reflective even with a perfectly smooth surface. But the effect is small: the difference between $n = 1.47$ ($R = 3.6\%$) and $n = 1.58$ ($R = 5.1\%$) is only 1.5 percentage points ‚Äî barely perceptible to the eye.

Equation 26: Surface Tension

‚ö° Surface Tension ‚Äî Dietzel / Lyon Additive Model
\[ \gamma = \sum_i x_i \, \gamma_i \]
where $\gamma$ is in mN/m (= dyn/cm) and $\gamma_i$ are partial surface tension contributions per oxide.

Historical Context

Adolf Dietzel (1942) and K. C. Lyon (1944) independently developed additive models for the surface tension of oxide glass melts. Surface tension in glass melts was of critical industrial importance for the development of the Pilkington float glass process (1950s), where molten glass floats on a bath of molten tin ‚Äî the surface tension of the glass controls the equilibrium thickness of the float ribbon.

Deep Physical Meaning

Surface tension $\gamma$ measures the energy cost of creating new surface area. It arises from the fundamental asymmetry at a glass surface: atoms at the surface have fewer neighbours than atoms in the bulk, so their average binding energy is higher (less negative). Creating more surface requires breaking more bonds, which costs energy.

Oxide-specific contributions:

  • $\SiO$ ($\gamma_i \approx 300$ mN/m): Very high surface tension because the Si‚ÄìO bonds are strong and the surface energy cost of breaking them is large.
  • $\AlO$ ($\gamma_i \approx 600$ mN/m): Even higher ‚Äî alumina dramatically raises surface tension.
  • $\NaO$ ($\gamma_i \approx 80$ mN/m): Low, because Na+ ions are weakly bound and readily segregate to the surface (lowering the surface energy).
  • $\KO$ ($\gamma_i \approx 50$ mN/m): Even lower than soda; potassium is an effective surface-tension reducer.
  • $\BO$ ($\gamma_i \approx 75$ mN/m): Low ‚Äî boron oxide reduces surface tension, which is why borosilicate glazes wet surfaces easily.
  • $\PbOx$ ($\gamma_i \approx 130$ mN/m): Moderate. Lead glazes have relatively low surface tension, contributing to their excellent flow and self-healing.

What You Can Calculate

  1. Self-healing ability: Surface tension is the driving force that pulls a disturbed glaze surface back to smoothness. Higher $\gamma$ = stronger restorative force. The ‚Äúhealing index‚Äù combines low viscosity with high surface tension: $\text{Healing} \sim \gamma / \eta$.
  2. Bubble escape: High surface tension resists the formation of small bubbles (Laplace pressure $\Delta P = 2\gamma/r$). Once a bubble forms, high $\gamma$ makes the free surface reluctant to open a hole for the bubble to escape. This creates a complex trade-off: high $\gamma$ prevents bubble formation but makes existing bubbles harder to release.
  3. Crawling and beading: If the surface tension of the glaze is too high relative to the surface energy of the body, the glaze will bead up and crawl (dewet). The Young‚ÄìDupr√© equation governs this:
    \[\cos\theta_c = \frac{\gamma_{\text{body}} - \gamma_{\text{interface}}}{\gamma_{\text{glaze}}}\]
  4. Wetting of raw body: During early melting, good wetting (low contact angle) requires moderate $\gamma$.
  5. Float glass thickness: In the Pilkington process, the equilibrium thickness of the glass ribbon on the tin bath is proportional to $\sqrt{\gamma/\rho g}$.

Equation 27: Optical Bandgap

‚ö° Optical Bandgap ‚Äî Dimitrov & Komatsu
\[ E_g = 4.0 - 1.5\,\Lambda \]
where $E_g$ is the optical bandgap in eV and $\Lambda$ is the optical basicity (Eq. [eq:dd_ob]).

Historical Context

Vesselin Dimitrov and Takayuki Komatsu (Nagaoka University of Technology, Japan) published this remarkable linear correlation in a series of papers from 2002 to 2010. By analysing UV-visible absorption spectra of hundreds of oxide glass compositions across many glass families, they showed that the optical bandgap ‚Äî the minimum photon energy needed to excite an electron from the valence band to the conduction band ‚Äî is linearly related to optical basicity.

Deep Physical Meaning

The optical bandgap determines the transparency window of the glass:

  • Photons with energy $\lt E_g$ pass through the glass (it is transparent at those wavelengths).
  • Photons with energy $\geq E_g$ are absorbed (exciting electrons across the gap).

The relationship to optical basicity is physically intuitive: in a more basic glass ($\Lambda$ high), the electron density on oxygen is higher, the valence band is pushed upward in energy, and the gap to the conduction band shrinks. The glass begins absorbing at longer wavelengths (lower energy), shifting the UV absorption edge into the visible spectrum and giving the glass a yellow or amber tint.

Conversely, in an acidic glass ($\Lambda$ low, high silica), the electron density on oxygen is low, the valence band is deep, the bandgap is wide, and the glass transmits deep into the UV ‚Äî it appears colourless.

What You Can Understand

  • UV absorption edge: $\lambda_{\text{edge}} = hc/E_g = 1240/E_g$ (nm). A glass with $E_g = 4.0$ eV absorbs below 310 nm (deep UV, invisible). A glass with $E_g = 3.0$ eV absorbs below 413 nm (violet), making it appear slightly yellow.
  • Colour prediction: Before adding any colorant, the base glass may already have an intrinsic colour from its UV absorption tail extending into the visible spectrum. This is particularly relevant for high-alkali or high-barium glasses.
  • UV protection: Glasses with low $E_g$ block UV light and can protect UV-sensitive contents (amber glass bottles).
  • Solarisation: Glasses with $E_g$ near the solar UV boundary (‚àº3.5$~eV$) can undergo solarisation (colour change from prolonged UV exposure) as photon-excited electrons form colour centres.

Worked Example

With $\Lambda \approx 0.56$:

\[E_g = 4.0 - 1.5(0.56) = 4.0 - 0.84 = \boxed{3.16\;\text{eV}}\]
\[\lambda_{\text{edge}} = \frac{1240}{3.16} = 392\;\text{nm}\]
The absorption edge is at 392 nm ‚Äî just at the boundary between UV-A and visible violet light. The base glass will appear essentially colourless but may develop a very faint yellow tint if the edge tail extends slightly into the visible range.

Chemical Durability & Thermodynamics

Equation 28: Chemical Durability Index

‚ö° Chemical Durability ‚Äî Silica/Modifier Ratio
\[ D = \frac{[\SiO]}{[\text{R}_2\text{O}]} \]
where $[\text{R}_2\text{O}]$ is the total alkali oxide content (in moles).

Deep Physical Meaning

Chemical durability measures a glass's resistance to attack by water, acids, and bases. The fundamental mechanism of glass corrosion proceeds in two stages:

Stage 1 ‚Äî Ion exchange (acid attack):

\[\equiv\text{Si--O--Na} + \text{H}^+ \to \;\equiv\text{Si--OH} + \text{Na}^+_{\text{(aq)}}\]
Hydrogen ions from water replace alkali cations bonded to non-bridging oxygens. This ‚Äúleaches‚Äù alkali from the glass surface, creating a silica-rich depleted layer. The rate is controlled by the number of alkali-bonded NBOs ‚Äî hence the inverse relationship with $[\text{R}_2\text{O}]$.

Stage 2 ‚Äî Network dissolution (base attack):

\[\equiv\text{Si--O--Si}\equiv \;+\; \text{OH}^- \to \;\equiv\text{Si--OH} + \;\equiv\text{Si--O}^-\]
Hydroxyl ions attack bridging oxygens, breaking the Si‚ÄìO‚ÄìSi backbone. The rate is controlled by the silica content ‚Äî high $[\SiO]$ means more bridging oxygens to break, requiring more dissolution before the network fails.

The ratio $D = [\SiO]/[\text{R}_2\text{O}]$ captures both factors: high $\SiO$ strengthens the network; high $\text{R}_2\text{O}$ provides easy-to-leach sites.

Equation 29: Hydration Energy

‚ö° Hydration Free Energy ‚Äî Conradt Model
\[ \Delta H_{\text{hyd}} = \sum_i x_i \, \Delta H_i \]
where $\Delta H_i$ are the partial molar hydration enthalpies of each oxide component (kJ/mol).

Historical Context

Reinhard Conradt (RWTH Aachen, 2008) developed a thermodynamic framework for predicting glass durability from first-principles thermochemical data. Rather than the empirical ratio $D$, Conradt proposed computing the actual Gibbs free energy of hydration ‚Äî the thermodynamic driving force for the dissolution reaction:

\[\text{Glass} + n\,\text{H}_2\text{O} \to \text{hydrated products}\]

If $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$ is strongly negative, the hydration reaction is thermodynamically favourable and the glass is susceptible to dissolution. If $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$ is near zero or positive, the glass is thermodynamically stable against water attack.

Deep Physical Meaning

The hydration energy provides a thermodynamic complement to the kinetic durability index $D$:

  • $D$ tells you how fast the glass will corrode (controlled by the number of attack sites).
  • $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$ tells you whether the glass wants to corrode at all (thermodynamic driving force).

A glass can have high $D$ (few attack sites) but negative $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$ (thermodynamically unstable) ‚Äî it corrodes slowly but inevitably. Or it can have low $D$ but near-zero $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}}$ ‚Äî many potential attack sites but no thermodynamic incentive to use them.

Oxide-specific contributions:

  • $\NaO$ ($\Delta H_i \approx -340$ kJ/mol): Strongly negative ‚Äî sodium oxide wants to dissolve. Each mole of Na2O that leaches releases substantial energy.
  • $\CaOx$ ($\Delta H_i \approx -65$ kJ/mol): Weakly negative. Calcium mildly promotes hydration but far less than alkalis.
  • $\SiO$ ($\Delta H_i \approx +5$ kJ/mol): Slightly positive ‚Äî silica resists hydration. This is why vitreous silica is essentially insoluble in water.
  • $\AlO$ ($\Delta H_i \approx +20$ kJ/mol): Moderately positive. Alumina in the network actively resists dissolution ‚Äî this is why small additions of alumina dramatically improve glass durability.

What You Can Calculate

  1. Thermodynamic stability: $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}} \gt 0$: glass is thermodynamically stable against water. $\lt 0$: susceptible to long-term corrosion.
  2. Archaeological prediction: Ancient glasses with $\Delta H_{\text{hyd}} \ll 0$ develop distinctive weathering crusts over centuries (iridescent ‚Äúpatina‚Äù on Roman glass). Conradt's model can predict which historical glass compositions will survive.
  3. Design optimisation: To maximise durability, the potter should maximise $\SiO$ and $\AlO$ (positive contributions) while minimising $\NaO$ and $\KO$ (negative contributions).

Worked Example

\[\Delta H_{\text{hyd}} \approx (3.20)(+5) + (0.35)(+20) + (0.24)(-340) + (0.11)(-300) + (0.42)(-65) + (0.18)(+10) + (0.10)(-50)\]
\[= 16.0 + 7.0 - 81.6 - 33.0 - 27.3 + 1.8 - 5.0 = \boxed{-122.1\;\text{kJ/mol}}\]
Moderately negative ‚Äî the glass is thermodynamically susceptible to water attack, but the high silica content (high $D$) provides kinetic protection. A common situation: thermodynamically unstable but kinetically stable.

Equation 30: Shannon Entropy of Mixing

‚ö° Shannon Mixing Entropy
\[ H_{\text{mix}} = -\sum_i p_i \ln p_i \]
where $p_i$ is the mole fraction of oxide $i$ and the sum runs over all oxides present ($p_i \gt 0$).

Historical Context

Claude E. Shannon (1948), working at Bell Laboratories, published ‚ÄúA Mathematical Theory of Communication,‚Äù which defined information entropy ‚Äî a measure of the ‚Äúsurprise‚Äù or ‚Äúuncertainty‚Äù in a probability distribution. While Shannon was thinking about telegraph signals, not pottery, his entropy formula has become one of the most widely applied mathematical tools across all sciences.

In glass science, the Shannon entropy of the oxide composition emerged as a useful metric through the ‚Äúhigh-entropy‚Äù materials movement (Cantor et al., 2004; Yeh et al., 2004), originally applied to metallic alloys, and subsequently extended to oxide glasses by Inoue (2000s) and others.

Deep Physical Meaning

Shannon entropy measures the compositional complexity of the glass:

  • $H_{\text{mix}} = 0$: A single-component glass (e.g., pure vitreous silica). Zero surprise ‚Äî you know exactly what every atom is.
  • $H_{\text{mix}} \sim 1.0$: A moderately complex glass with 3‚Äì4 significant components. Typical of simple soda-lime or lead glazes.
  • $H_{\text{mix}} \sim 1.5‚Äì2.0$: A complex glass with 5‚Äì8 significant components. Typical of stoneware glazes.
  • $H_{\text{mix}} \gt 2.0$: A highly complex ‚Äúhigh-entropy‚Äù glass with many components in near-equal proportions.

Physical significance:

  1. Configurational entropy: Higher mixing entropy means more ways to arrange atoms in the structure. This additional entropy stabilises the glass against crystallisation (entropy opposes the driving force for ordering).
  2. Glass-forming ability: High-entropy compositions tend to be better glass formers because the large number of distinct species frustrates the formation of any particular crystal structure. This is the ‚Äúconfusion principle‚Äù ‚Äî the liquid is ‚Äúconfused‚Äù about which crystal to form, so it forms none.
  3. Property smoothness: High-entropy glasses tend to have smoother property-composition landscapes ‚Äî small changes in one component have less dramatic effects on properties.

Equation 31: Dietzel Field Strength

‚ö° Dietzel Field Strength
\[ F = \frac{z_c}{a^2} \]
where $z_c$ is the formal charge of the cation and $a = r_c + r_{\text{O}^{2-}}$ is the cation‚Äìoxygen distance in √Ö ($r_{\text{O}^{2-}} = 1.40$ √Ö).

Historical Context

Adolf Dietzel (1942) at the Kaiser-Wilhelm-Institut introduced the field strength concept to classify oxide cations into three categories based on their electrostatic interaction with the surrounding oxygen anions. This classification became one of the foundational concepts of glass science.

Deep Physical Meaning

The field strength $F$ measures the electrostatic intensity at the surface of a cation as experienced by a neighbouring oxygen ion. It controls whether the cation acts as a network former, modifier, or intermediate:

$\gt 1.3$Network formerSi4+ ($F = 1.57$), B3+ ($F = 1.34$)Forms glass skeleton
$0.4 - 1.3$IntermediateAl3+ ($F = 0.84$), Ti4+ ($F = 1.04$), Zn2+ ($F = 0.53$)Can be either

A cation with high field strength ($F \gt 1.3$) creates such a strong local electric field that it attracts and holds 3‚Äì4 oxygen anions in a tight tetrahedral arrangement. This is the structural origin of the ASI threshold (Eq. [eq:dd_asi]).

  1. Cation classification: Determine the structural role of each cation in the glass network.
  2. Average field strength: $\bar{F} = \sum x_i F_i$ correlates with surface tension, chemical durability, and elastic modulus.
  3. Phase separation tendency: Cations with very different field strengths ($\Delta F \gt 0.5$) tend to segregate into compositionally distinct domains (phase separation), creating opalescence or microstructure.
  4. Crystallisation tendency: Intermediate-$F$ cations promote nucleation because they can form stable crystal embryos with either structure.

Equation 32: Mixed Alkali Effect (MAE)

‚ö° Mixed Alkali Effect
\[ \text{MAE} = 4 \, x_{\text{Na}} \, x_{\text{K}} \]
where $x_{\text{Na}} = [\NaO] / ([\NaO] + [\KO])$ and $x_{\text{K}} = [\KO] / ([\NaO] + [\KO])$ are the alkali fractions, normalised so $x_{\text{Na}} + x_{\text{K}} = 1$.

Historical Context

The mixed alkali effect was first reported by Weber & Becker in 1883 and has puzzled glass scientists for over 140 years. When two different alkali oxides are present in a glass, certain properties ‚Äî particularly ionic conductivity, internal friction, and diffusion coefficient ‚Äî deviate dramatically from the linear interpolation between the single-alkali end members. The effect is strongest at the 50/50 mixture, where ionic conductivity can be several orders of magnitude lower than either pure alkali glass.

Deep Physical Meaning

The factor $4 x_{\text{Na}} x_{\text{K}}$ is maximised when $x_{\text{Na}} = x_{\text{K}} = 0.5$, giving MAE $= 1.0$. It equals zero when only one alkali is present. This parabolic form captures the essence of the mixed alkali effect:

üî¨ Why Two Alkalis Fight Each Other
In a single-alkali glass, all alkali cations diffuse through similar pathways in the network (channels of appropriate size for that cation). When two dissimilar alkalis coexist, they partially ‚Äúblock‚Äù each other's diffusion pathways:
  1. Na+ (radius 0.99 √Ö) creates channels sized for Na+.
  2. K+ (radius 1.38 √Ö) creates channels sized for K+.
  3. When Na+ hops into a K+-sized site, it sits loosely and creates a local strain field that traps it. Conversely, K+ in a Na+-sized site is squeezed and immobilised.
  4. The result: both species move more slowly than either would alone, producing a dramatic reduction in ionic conductivity and increase in viscosity.

The Dynamic Structure Model (Maass, Bunde & Ingram, 1992) formalises this as a mismatch between the alkali cation and its local site energy landscape.

What You Can Understand

  1. Electrical resistivity: Mixed-alkali glazes have much higher electrical resistivity than single-alkali glazes. This can affect kiln thermocouple readings (junction potential) and electrolysis behaviour.
  2. Chemical durability: The MAE slightly improves durability because reduced alkali mobility means slower leaching rates.
  3. Internal friction peak: Mixed-alkali glasses show a characteristic mechanical loss peak in dilatometric or DMA measurements. This indicates energy dissipation during mechanical oscillation ‚Äî relevant for vibration damping applications.
  4. Viscosity anomaly: Melt viscosity is slightly higher than expected at intermediate mixtures.

Worked Example

\[x_{\text{Na}} = \frac{0.24}{0.24 + 0.11} = 0.686, \qquad x_{\text{K}} = \frac{0.11}{0.35} = 0.314\]
\[\text{MAE} = 4 \times 0.686 \times 0.314 = \boxed{0.86}\]
Strong mixed-alkali effect ‚Äî nearly at the maximum. The Na/K ratio of ‚àº2:1 is in the region of large MAE. This correlates with enhanced electrical resistance and slightly improved durability.

The three controlling variables:

  1. Optical basicity $\Lambda$: Higher basicity promotes reduction (more Fe2+) by increasing the activity of O2- in the melt, pushing the equilibrium toward the reduced product. A $\Lambda$ increase of 0.1 units shifts the Fe2+/Fe3+ ratio by roughly a factor of 2.
  2. Temperature $T$: Higher temperature promotes reduction (Le Chatelier's principle: the endothermic reduction reaction is favoured). Cooling through the ‚Äúredox freeze-in‚Äù temperature (‚àºT$_g$ + 100¬∞C) locks in the Fe2+/Fe3+ ratio.
  3. Oxygen partial pressure $p_{\text{O}_2}$: Directly from the equilibrium expression: lower $p_{\text{O}_2}$ (reduction firing) drives the reaction toward Fe2+. Quantitatively, $\log(\text{Fe}^{2+}/\text{Fe}^{3+}) \propto -\frac{1}{4}\log p_{\text{O}_2}$.

What You Can Calculate

  • Celadon prediction: A true celadon requires Fe2+ fraction $\gt 0.6$ in a low-iron glaze (1 %‚Äì3 % Fe2O3). The equation predicts whether a given base glass composition and firing atmosphere can achieve this.
  • Tenmoku prediction: Rich amber-brown tenmoku requires moderate Fe2+/Fe3+ (‚àº0.3‚Äì0.5) in a high-iron glaze (5 %‚Äì12 % Fe2O3).
  • Copper red feasibility: By analogy, the Cu+/Cu2+ redox equilibrium follows the same basicity-temperature-oxygen framework. Predicting whether a glaze can sustain colloidal Cu0 (the red chromophore) requires $\Lambda \gt 0.58$ and strong reduction.
  • Atmosphere sensitivity: Glazes with $\Lambda$ near 0.55 are on the Fe redox knife-edge ‚Äî small variations in kiln atmosphere produce large colour shifts. This is either a problem (inconsistency) or a feature (dynamic visual character).

Worked Example

With $\Lambda \approx 0.56$ and cone 6 temperature (1222 ¬∞C), in oxidation ($\log p_{\text{O}_2} \approx -0.7$):

\[\log\frac{\text{Fe}^{2+}}{\text{Fe}^{3+}} \approx 5.0\Lambda - 3.0 + 0.5\log T - 0.25\log p_{\text{O}_2}\]
\[\approx 5.0(0.56) - 3.0 + 0.5\log(1495) - 0.25(-0.7)\]
\[\approx 2.80 - 3.0 + 1.59 + 0.18 = 1.57\]

The Fe2+/Fe3+ ratio $\approx 10^{1.57} \approx 37$ ‚Äî overwhelmingly Fe2+? This simplified approximation overestimates; the actual equilibrium with proper activity coefficients gives Fe2+ fraction $\approx \boxed{0.15\text{--}0.25}$ in oxidation. In reduction firing, this rises to ‚àº0.5‚Äì0.7, crossing the celadon threshold.

Equation 34: CIE L*a*b* Color Prediction

‚ö° CIE L*a*b* Colour from Colorant Loading
\[\begin{aligned} L^* &= L^*_0 - k_L \cdot c \\ a^* &= a^*_0 + k_a \cdot c \\ b^* &= b^*_0 + k_b \cdot c \end{aligned}\]
where $c$ is the colorant concentration (wt%), $L^*_0, a^*_0, b^*_0$ are the base-glass colour coordinates, and $k_L, k_a, k_b$ are colorant-specific absorption coefficients that depend on the redox state and coordination environment.

Historical Context

The CIE L*a*b* colour space (CIELAB) was standardised by the Commission Internationale de l'√âclairage in 1976 to provide a perceptually uniform colour metric ‚Äî equal numerical distances correspond to equal perceived colour differences. The three axes are:

  • $L^*$: Lightness ($0 = $ black, $100 = $ white)
  • $a^*$: Red-green axis ($+a^* = $ red, $-a^* = $ green)
  • $b^*$: Yellow-blue axis ($+b^* = $ yellow, $-b^* = $ blue)

Deep Physical Meaning

The linear model is a first-order approximation of the Beer‚ÄìLambert law applied to a thin glaze layer. In reality, glaze colour is a complex function of:

  1. Colorant species: Which chromophore is present (Fe3+, Fe2+, Co2+, Cu2+, Mn3+, Cr3+, etc.)
  2. Coordination: Tetrahedral vs. octahedral environments produce vastly different crystal field splitting and hence different absorption bands.
  3. Concentration: Beer's law gives linear absorbance vs. concentration at low concentrations, but at high concentrations, clustering and charge-transfer effects create nonlinearity.
  4. Glaze thickness: Thicker glaze = longer optical path length = more absorption.
  5. Base glass: The UV absorption edge and refractive index of the base glass affect how much light enters, how it propagates, and how much reaches the colorant.
  6. Surface texture: Matte surfaces scatter light before it enters the glaze, reducing the effective colour saturation.

Sensitivity & Error Analysis

No computed property is more reliable than the data that feeds it. These two equations quantify the propagation of uncertainty from composition measurements through the entire property chain. They transform the atlas from a deterministic calculator into a statistical one.

Equation 35: Taylor Error Propagation ‚Äî GUM (1997)

‚ö° Taylor / GUM Uncertainty Propagation
\[ \delta f = \sqrt{\sum_i \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}\right)^2 (\delta x_i)^2} \]
where $f$ is any computed property, $x_i$ is the mole fraction of oxide $i$, $\delta x_i$ is the uncertainty in $x_i$, and $\partial f / \partial x_i$ is the partial derivative (sensitivity coefficient) of property $f$ with respect to oxide $x_i$.

Historical Context

Brook Taylor (1715) established the Taylor series expansion of functions, but the systematic application of error propagation to physical measurements was formalised by the Guide to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement (GUM), published jointly by ISO, IEC, BIPM, OIML, IUPAC, IUPAP, and IFCC in 1993 (revised 1995, 2008). This document standardised the ‚Äúlaw of propagation of uncertainty‚Äù as the international framework for reporting measurement uncertainty.

Deep Physical Meaning

The equation is a first-order (linear) approximation of how small errors in input variables propagate through a function to produce errors in the output. The key assumptions are:

  1. Linearity: The function $f$ is approximately linear over the range of uncertainty. This is valid when $\delta x_i \ll x_i$ (relative uncertainty $\lt 5\%$), which is generally satisfied for XRF or ICP-derived oxide analyses.
  2. Independence: The uncertainties in different oxides are uncorrelated. This is not strictly true for normalised compositions (since $\sum x_i = 1$, an increase in one must decrease others), but the error is small for compositions with many components.
  3. Gaussian distribution: The uncertainties are normally distributed. This is usually valid for well-calibrated analytical instruments.

The sensitivity coefficient $\partial f / \partial x_i$ tells you how strongly each oxide affects the output property. Large $|\partial f / \partial x_i|$ means the property is sensitive to that oxide; small means it is insensitive. This is extraordinarily useful for identifying:

  • Which oxide uncertainties matter most for each property
  • Which properties are robust and which are fragile
  • Where to invest in better analytical accuracy

What You Can Calculate

For every property in the atlas, the Taylor propagation yields:

  1. Standard uncertainty ($\delta f$): The ¬± band around the predicted value. For example, COE $ = 6.8 \pm 0.3 \times 10^{-6}$ /K.
  2. Relative uncertainty: $\delta f / f$, expressed as a percentage. Properties with relative uncertainty $\gt 10\%$ should be treated with caution.
  3. Sensitivity ranking: Sort the terms $(\partial f / \partial x_i)^2 (\delta x_i)^2$ to identify which oxides dominate the uncertainty. For COE, the dominant uncertainty sources are typically $\SiO$ (large contribution, large amount) and $\NaO$ (large coefficient, moderate amount).
  4. Quality gates: If $\delta f$ exceeds a threshold, the atlas can flag the prediction as ‚Äúlow confidence‚Äù and suggest better analytical data.

Worked Example

Suppose we want the uncertainty in COE ($\alpha = \sum x_i \alpha_i$) given $\delta x_i = 0.02$ for all oxides:

\[\delta \alpha = \sqrt{\sum_i \alpha_i^2 \times (0.02)^2} = 0.02\sqrt{\sum_i \alpha_i^2}\]
With $\alpha_{\text{SiO}} = 3.8$, $\alpha_{\text{Na}} = 39.5$, $\alpha_{\text{K}} = 28.0$, $\alpha_{\text{Ca}} = 13.0$, etc.:
\[\delta \alpha \approx 0.02 \times \sqrt{14.4 + 1560 + 784 + 169 + \cdots} \approx 0.02 \times 53 \approx \boxed{1.06 \times 10^{-6}\;/\text{K}}\]

The dominant uncertainty source is $\NaO$ ($\alpha_{\text{Na}} = 39.5$ has the largest partial coefficient), not $\SiO$ ‚Äî even though $\SiO$ is the largest component by far.

Equation 36: Numerical Jacobian

‚ö° Central-Difference Jacobian
\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} \approx \frac{f(x_i + h) - f(x_i - h)}{2h} \]
where $h$ is a small perturbation (typically $h = 0.01$ moles).

Deep Physical Meaning

This is the computational engine that makes Eq. [eq:dd_taylor] practical. For complex, multi-step properties like VFT viscosity (which passes through GRD parameterisation, then VFT inversion, then temperature landmark extraction), an analytical derivative $\partial f / \partial x_i$ is intractable. The numerical Jacobian sidesteps this by simply computing the property twice ‚Äî once with oxide $x_i$ increased by $h$, once decreased by $h$ ‚Äî and taking the slope.

‚ÑπÔ∏è Why Central Differences?
The forward difference $(f(x+h) - f(x))/h$ is simpler but only first-order accurate ($O(h)$). The central difference $(f(x+h) - f(x-h))/(2h)$ is second-order accurate ($O(h^2)$), meaning it converges to the true derivative much faster as $h \to 0$. For $h = 0.01$, the central difference error is $\sim 10^{-4}$, while the forward difference error is $\sim 10^{-2}$ ‚Äî a 100√ó improvement.

What You Can Calculate

  1. Full sensitivity matrix (Jacobian): For $n$ oxides and $m$ properties, the Jacobian $J$ is an $m \times n$ matrix. Each row is a property; each column is an oxide. Entry $J_{ij}$ tells you how much property $i$ changes per unit change in oxide $j$.
  2. Critical oxide identification: For each property, the Jacobian column with the largest magnitude identifies the ‚Äúlever arm‚Äù oxide ‚Äî the one that has the most influence.
  3. Recipe optimisation: The Jacobian provides the gradient for gradient-descent optimisation. To increase a target property, adjust oxides in the direction of the positive gradient.
  4. What-If analysis: ‚ÄúIf I add 0.05 moles of whiting ($\CaOx$), what happens?‚Äù Multiply each Jacobian entry by 0.05 to get the predicted change in every property simultaneously.

Worked Example

To find $\partial(\text{COE}) / \partial[\NaO]$ with $h = 0.01$:

\[\begin{aligned} \alpha([\NaO] = 0.25) &= \text{(re-run Appen model)} = 6.85 \times 10^{-6} \\ \alpha([\NaO] = 0.23) &= \text{(re-run Appen model)} = 6.75 \times 10^{-6} \\ \frac{\partial \alpha}{\partial[\NaO]} &\approx \frac{6.85 - 6.75}{0.02} = \boxed{5.0 \times 10^{-6}\;\text{per mole Na}_2\text{O}} \end{aligned}\]
Each additional 0.01 moles of $\NaO$ increases COE by $0.05 \times 10^{-6}$/K ‚Äî a significant sensitivity, confirming that soda is a strong expansion driver.

Electrical Properties

Equation 37: Dielectric Constant ‚Äî Clausius‚ÄìMossotti

‚ö° Clausius‚ÄìMossotti Dielectric Model
\[ \frac{\varepsilon_r - 1}{\varepsilon_r + 2} = \frac{4\pi}{3} \, \frac{N_A \rho}{M} \, \alpha_p \]
where $\varepsilon_r$ is the relative dielectric constant (permittivity), $N_A$ is Avogadro's number, $\rho$ is density (g/cm^3), $M$ is molar mass (g/mol), and $\alpha_p$ is the total molar polarisability (cm^3).

Historical Context

Rudolf Clausius (1879) and Ottaviano Mossotti (1850) independently derived this relationship connecting the macroscopic dielectric constant of a material to the microscopic polarisability of its constituent atoms or molecules. The Clausius‚ÄìMossotti equation is one of the most successful bridges between continuum electrodynamics and atomic-scale physics.

In the atlas, the total molar polarisability $\alpha_p$ is computed additively from the oxide contributions (using Shannon's ionic refraction data, 1993), and the density $\rho$ is computed from the additive molar volume model.

Deep Physical Meaning

The dielectric constant measures how strongly a material polarises in response to an applied electric field. In a glass:

  1. Electronic polarisation: The electron clouds of all atoms shift slightly in response to the applied field. This is the dominant contribution at optical frequencies and connects directly to the refractive index via $\varepsilon_r(\text{optical}) \approx n^2$.
  2. Ionic polarisation: Cation-anion pairs stretch and compress in response to the field. This adds a contribution at infrared frequencies.
  3. Dipolar polarisation: In glasses (unlike crystals), there are no permanent dipoles to orient, so this contribution is minimal.
  4. Space-charge polarisation: At low frequencies, mobile alkali ions migrate short distances, creating macroscopic charge separation. This dominates at $\lt 1$ kHz and depends strongly on alkali content and the mixed-alkali effect.

The Clausius‚ÄìMossotti equation captures only the first two contributions (electronic + ionic), which together give the ‚Äúhigh-frequency‚Äù dielectric constant ($\varepsilon_r$ at ‚àº1 MHz). The low-frequency dielectric constant can be 2‚Äì5√ó larger in alkali-rich glasses due to space-charge effects.

What You Can Calculate

  • Microwave absorption: The dielectric loss tangent ($\tan\delta$) determines how much microwave energy a glaze absorbs. High $\varepsilon_r$ correlates with higher microwave absorption ‚Äî relevant for microwave-safe dishware.
  • Electrical insulation: Low-$\varepsilon_r$ glasses are better insulators for electrical applications (e.g., kiln furniture, spark plug ceramics).
  • Cross-check with refractive index: At optical frequencies, $\varepsilon_r \approx n^2$. For the atlas, $n \approx 1.52$ so $\varepsilon_r(\text{optical}) \approx 2.31$. The full Clausius‚ÄìMossotti model at MHz frequencies gives $\varepsilon_r \approx 6‚Äì8$ for typical stoneware, confirming the ionic contribution.

Thermal Transport

Equation 38: Thermal Conductivity & Derived Properties

‚ö° Thermal Transport Properties
\[\begin{aligned} \lambda &= \sum_i x_i \, \lambda_i & &[\text{Thermal conductivity, \text{W/m}}] \\[4pt] C_p &= \sum_i x_i \, C_{p,i} & &[\text{Heat capacity, \text{J/g}}] \\[4pt] \kappa &= \frac{\lambda}{\rho \, C_p} & &[\text{Thermal diffusivity, \text{mm^2/s}}] \end{aligned}\]

Deep Physical Meaning

These three equations describe how heat moves through the glass:

Thermal conductivity $\lambda$: The rate of heat flow per unit temperature gradient. In a glass, heat is conducted by two mechanisms:

  1. Phonon conduction: Vibrations propagate through the covalent Si‚ÄìO‚ÄìSi network. This is the dominant mechanism at low temperatures. Dense, well-connected networks (high $\SiO$, high $\AlO$) conduct heat better.
  2. Photon conduction (radiative transport): At temperatures above ‚àº600 ¬∞C, the glass becomes partially transparent to infrared radiation, and heat transfer by photon absorption/re-emission becomes significant. This is why kiln walls glow red ‚Äî they are radiating heat directly through the ceramic.

Oxide-specific contributions:

  • $\SiO$ ($\lambda_i \approx 1.4$ W/m): Highest ‚Äî the Si‚ÄìO network is an excellent phonon conduit.
  • $\NaO$, $\KO$ ($\lambda_i \approx 0.5‚Äì0.7$): Lower ‚Äî alkali ions scatter phonons, reducing conductivity.
  • $\BO$ ($\lambda_i \approx 0.7$): Moderate ‚Äî the planar $\text{BO}_3$ groups are less effective phonon conductors than $\text{SiO}_4$ tetrahedra.

Heat capacity $C_p$: The energy required to raise the temperature of 1 gram by 1 kelvin. For glasses, $C_p$ ranges from 0.7‚Äì1.2 J/g at room temperature, increasing slightly with temperature (the Dulong‚ÄìPetit limit). Heat capacity determines:

  • How much energy the kiln must deliver per gram of ware per degree of temperature rise
  • How quickly the pot's temperature responds to kiln changes
  • Thermal energy storage capacity

Thermal diffusivity $\kappa$: The ratio of heat conduction to heat storage: $\kappa = \lambda / (\rho C_p)$. High $\kappa$ means heat propagates quickly through the material. Low $\kappa$ means the surface heats or cools long before the interior responds, creating thermal gradients (and hence thermal stress). This is critical for:

  • Thermal shock: Low $\kappa$ means steep thermal gradients during rapid heating or cooling, increasing the risk of thermal-stress fracture.
  • Firing uniformity: High $\kappa$ means more uniform temperature distribution through the glaze layer during firing.
  • Kiln schedule design: The rate of temperature change should be slow enough that the maximum internal temperature gradient stays below the stress-fracture threshold. This rate is proportional to $\kappa$.

Worked Example

\[\begin{aligned} \lambda &\approx \boxed{1.1\;\text{W/m}} \\ C_p &\approx \boxed{0.85\;\text{J/g}} \\ \rho &\approx 2.50\;\text{g/cm^3} \\ \kappa &= \frac{1.1}{2.50 \times 0.85} = \frac{1.1}{2.125} = \boxed{0.518\;\text{mm^2/s}} \end{aligned}\]

Thermal diffusivity of ‚àº0.5 mm^2/s is standard for silicate glasses. For a 0.5 mm glaze layer, the thermal equilibration time is approximately $\tau \approx L^2/(4\kappa) = 0.25/2.07 \approx 0.12$ seconds ‚Äî the glaze layer equilibrates thermally almost instantly, which is why glaze temperature is essentially the same as kiln temperature during normal firing rates.

Glass-Forming Ability & Crystallisation

The final two equations ask the most fundamental question about any cooled melt: will it form a glass at all, or will it crystallise?

Equation 39: Turnbull Reduced Glass Transition

‚ö° Turnbull Reduced Glass Transition Temperature
\[ T_{rg} = \frac{T_g}{T_m} \]
where $T_g$ and $T_m$ are in Kelvin.

Historical Context

David Turnbull (1969, Harvard University) proposed that the reduced glass transition temperature $T_{rg} = T_g / T_m$ is the single most useful predictor of glass-forming ability. His reasoning was elegant: $T_g/T_m$ measures how close the glass transition is to the melting point. If $T_g$ is high relative to $T_m$, the cooling melt freezes into a glass before it has a chance to nucleate and grow crystals.

Turnbull showed empirically that:

  • $T_{rg} \gt 2/3$ ($\approx 0.67$): The material is an excellent glass former. Almost any cooling rate produces a glass. Silicate glazes, window glass, and vitreous silica fall in this category.
  • $T_{rg} \approx 0.5‚Äì0.67$: Moderate glass former. Requires moderately fast cooling. Some metallic glasses and calcium phosphate glasses fall here.
  • $T_{rg} \lt 0.5$: Poor glass former. Requires extremely rapid quenching (splat-cooling, melt-spinning) to avoid crystallisation.

Deep Physical Meaning

The physics behind Turnbull's criterion connects to nucleation theory:

üî¨ Nucleation vs. Vitrification: A Race Against Time
Crystal nucleation requires two conditions simultaneously:
  1. Thermodynamic driving force: The free energy difference between liquid and crystal ($\Delta G_v$) must be large enough to overcome the surface energy cost of creating a crystal nucleus. This driving force is zero at $T_m$ and increases with undercooling: $\Delta G_v \propto (T_m - T)$.
  2. Atomic mobility: Atoms must be able to rearrange to form the crystal lattice. Mobility is controlled by viscosity: high $\eta$ = low mobility. Viscosity increases dramatically as $T$ decreases toward $T_g$.

The nucleation rate $I$ is proportional to:

\[I \propto \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta G^*}{kT}\right) \times \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta G_D}{kT}\right)\]
The first exponential (thermodynamic barrier) increases with undercooling. The second (kinetic barrier) decreases with undercooling (viscosity rises). The product $I$ peaks at an intermediate temperature ‚Äî the ‚Äúnose‚Äù of the TTT (Time-Temperature-Transformation) diagram.

If $T_g$ is above the nucleation-rate maximum (i.e., the melt freezes into a glass before reaching peak nucleation), crystallisation is effectively suppressed. $T_{rg} \gt 2/3$ roughly corresponds to this condition.

What You Can Calculate

  1. Glass-forming ability: Primary predictor of whether a composition will vitrify or crystallise during kiln cooling.
  2. Critical cooling rate: Lower $T_{rg}$ = faster cooling needed to avoid crystals. For $T_{rg} \gt 0.67$, standard kiln cooling (‚àº2¬∞C/min) is sufficient. For $T_{rg} \lt 0.55$, the composition will likely devitrify regardless of cooling schedule.
  3. Devitrification prediction: If $T_{rg}$ is borderline (0.55‚Äì0.65), the glaze may partially crystallise, producing crystal-matte or stone textures. Potters who want crystal glazes deliberately seek $T_{rg}$ in this range.
  4. Crystal-glaze design: To grow macroscopic crystals (zinc silicate, wollastonite), $T_{rg}$ should be $\lt 0.60$ and the cooling schedule should include a prolonged hold at the nucleation-rate maximum.

Worked Example

With $T_g \approx 660$¬∞C = 933 K and $T_m \approx 1222$¬∞C = 1495 K:

\[T_{rg} = \frac{933}{1495} = \boxed{0.624}\]
Moderate glass-forming ability ‚Äî in the transition zone. Standard kiln cooling will produce a glass, but very slow cooling (or holds in the devitrification window) could produce partial crystallisation. The composition is a glass former but not a robust one.

Equation 40: Hrub√Ω Parameter

‚ö° Hrub√Ω Glass Stability Parameter
\[ K_H = \frac{T_x - T_g}{T_m - T_x} \]
where $T_x$ is the crystallisation onset temperature (from DSC), $T_g$ is the glass transition temperature, and $T_m$ is the melting point. All in Kelvin.

Historical Context

Arno≈°t Hrub√Ω (1972), a Czech glass scientist, proposed this parameter as an improvement over Turnbull's $T_{rg}$. The key insight was that $T_{rg}$ uses only two temperatures ($T_g$ and $T_m$), while Hrub√Ω's parameter incorporates a third critical temperature: the crystallisation onset $T_x$, measurable by Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC).

Deep Physical Meaning

The Hrub√Ω parameter divides the temperature axis between $T_g$ and $T_m$ into two regions:

Numerator ($T_x - T_g$): The width of the ‚Äústability window‚Äù ‚Äî the temperature range between the glass transition and the onset of crystallisation. Upon heating a glass through $T_g$, the atoms become mobile again but the glass remains supercooled for a while before crystals begin to nucleate. A wide stability window ($T_x - T_g$ large) means the glass is stable over a large temperature range against devitrification. This is the ‚Äúgood‚Äù direction.

Denominator ($T_m - T_x$): The proximity of the crystallisation onset to the melting point. If $T_x$ is close to $T_m$, the driving force for crystallisation is weak (the crystal barely ‚Äúwants to form‚Äù at or near its melting point). If $T_x$ is far below $T_m$, the driving force is strong and crystallisation is aggressive.

What You Can Understand

  1. Complementary to Turnbull: Turnbull's $T_{rg}$ uses only thermodynamic temperatures; Hrub√Ω's $K_H$ adds kinetic information ($T_x$). A glass with high $T_{rg}$ and high $K_H$ is an excellent glass former. Discrepancies help identify unusual crystallisation behaviour.
  2. DSC fingerprint: $K_H$ is directly measurable from a standard DSC run (heating at 10¬∞C/min): locate $T_g$ (midpoint of heat-capacity step), $T_x$ (exothermic crystallisation peak onset), and $T_m$ (endothermic melting peak onset).
  3. Crystal glaze navigation: For deliberate crystallisation, the potter wants low $K_H$ but not too low (need some glass stability for the matrix). The sweet spot is $K_H \approx 0.1‚Äì0.2$: unstable enough to crystallise, stable enough to remain partly glassy.
  4. Cooling schedule design: $K_H \lt 0.3$ suggests that slow cooling through the $T_g‚ÄìT_x$ window should be avoided unless crystals are desired. $K_H \gt 0.5$ means the kiln can be cooled at any reasonable rate without concern.

Worked Example

Assuming $T_x \approx 780$¬∞C = 1053 K (crystallisation onset from DSC data):

Moderate stability ‚Äî the glass will survive standard cooling but could devitrify under very slow or interrupted cooling schedules. Consistent with the $T_{rg} = 0.624$ assessment from Turnbull's criterion.
